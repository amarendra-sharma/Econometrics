    Chapter 1 · Foundations

    The Econometric Question

    The Question

    Does additional schooling cause higher earnings, or do individuals who obtain more schooling earn more for reasons that schooling did not produce?

    Arena⚔ The Causal Courtroom →

    Econometrics is the use of data to answer quantitative questions about the economic world. This chapter introduces no estimation formulae. Its purpose is to establish a single principle on which the remainder of the book depends: data can establish that two variables are associated, but cannot, without further assumptions, establish that one causes the other. The chapter defines the basic objects of the subject — variables, the distinction between a population and a sample, association, and the causal comparison — and introduces the data-handling tools in Python on which later computation rests.

    1.1 Data, variables, and averages

    Economic data are typically organized as a table in which each row records one unit of observation — an individual, a firm, a country — and each column records one variable, a measured characteristic that takes different values across units. The following table records two variables, years of schooling and hourly earnings, for five individuals.

    individualyears of schoolinghourly earnings ($)
    A1014
    B1215
    C1218
    D1624
    E1630

    The most elementary summary of a variable is its average, or mean: the sum of its values divided by their number. The mean of hourly earnings is

    \[ \bar y \;=\; \frac{14+15+18+24+30}{5} \;=\; 20.2 \text{ dollars per hour.} \]

    The mean is the foundation of the subject; the estimators developed in later chapters are, in a precise sense, conditional means computed under various assumptions.

    1.2 Association

    To describe the relationship between two variables, compute the mean of one within groups defined by the other. Among the two individuals with twelve years of schooling, mean earnings are \((15+18)/2 = 16.5\); among the two with sixteen years, \((24+30)/2 = 27.0\). Mean earnings rise with schooling. In a representative sample of the labour force this pattern is among the most robust empirical regularities in economics: a positive association between schooling and earnings.

    Definition 1.1 — Association

    Two variables are positively associated if larger values of one tend to occur with larger values of the other, and negatively associated if larger values of one tend to occur with smaller values of the other. Association is a descriptive property of the joint distribution of the variables; it carries no implication about cause.

    1.3 The distinction between association and causation

    The observed association admits two fundamentally different explanations, and the data cannot discriminate between them.

    Explanation A (a causal effect of schooling). Schooling raises productivity, and higher productivity commands higher earnings. Under this explanation, increasing an individual's schooling would raise that individual's earnings.

    Explanation B (selection). Individuals who acquire more schooling differ systematically in other respects — ability, family resources, preferences — that independently raise earnings. Under this explanation, the more-schooled would have earned more regardless of their schooling, and schooling itself need have no effect.

    Both explanations imply the same observed table. No quantity computed from these data, and no increase in sample size, distinguishes them. This is the central difficulty of the discipline.

    Key Idea

    Data identify association. A causal conclusion requires, in addition to the data, an assumption about the process that generated them — specifically, an assumption about how the units being compared differ in their unobserved determinants of the outcome. The credibility of a causal conclusion is the credibility of that assumption.

    1.4 The causal comparison and its missing half

    Define the causal effect of schooling for a single individual as the difference between the earnings that individual would receive with additional schooling and the earnings the same individual would receive without it. These two quantities are the individual's potential outcomes. The difficulty is that, for any individual, only one potential outcome is realized — the one corresponding to the schooling actually obtained — while the other is never observed. The causal effect for an individual is therefore not directly measurable, because one of its two components is missing for every unit.

    Definition 1.2 — Ceteris paribus effect

    The ceteris paribus (other things equal) effect of a variable on an outcome is the change in the outcome produced by a change in that variable while all other determinants of the outcome are held fixed. Estimating such an effect from observational data requires approximating the unobserved potential outcome of each treated unit using the observed outcomes of comparable untreated units.

    Every method in this book is a strategy for constructing such comparisons. Their validity rests on the comparability of the treated and untreated units in all respects other than the treatment — a requirement that the subsequent chapters make precise and, by various devices, attempt to satisfy.

    1.5 Structures of economic data

    The applicable methods depend on the structure of the data, of which four recur throughout the book.

    Data structures
    1. Cross-sectional data: many units observed at a single point in time. The setting of Chapters 2–9 and 14.
    2. Time-series data: a single unit observed at many points in time, in which the ordering of observations is informative. The setting of Chapters 15–16.
    3. Panel (longitudinal) data: many units each observed at several points in time, permitting each unit to be compared with itself across time. The setting of Chapters 10–12.
    4. Pooled cross-sections: independent cross-sectional samples drawn at different times, not following the same units. Employed in Chapter 12.

    1.6 Handling data in Python

    The computations in this book use the pandas library for data manipulation and, in later chapters, statsmodels and scikit-learn for estimation. A dataset is loaded into a DataFrame, the central tabular object, from which rows, columns, and summaries are obtained.

    import pandas as pd

# a dataset is a table; each row is one unit of observation, each column a variable
df = pd.read_csv("wages.csv")     # load a comma-separated data file
print(df.shape)                   # (number of rows, number of columns)
print(df.head())                  # the first five rows
print(df.describe())              # count, mean, standard deviation, min, max per column

    Means, group means, and the correlation between variables — the quantities of Sections 1.1 and 1.2 — are computed directly.

    # the average (mean) of a column
print(df["wage"].mean())

# the average of one variable within groups defined by another
print(df.groupby("educ")["wage"].mean())

# a numerical summary of how two variables move together
print(df[["educ", "wage"]].corr())

    The method groupby("educ")["wage"].mean() implements exactly the within-group averaging used above to describe the schooling–earnings association.     ★ Sharma's Take

    A discipline that repays adoption from the outset is to state, before any data are examined, the experiment one would conduct if experimentation were possible. For the schooling question, the experiment is the random assignment of additional schooling to otherwise identical individuals, followed by comparison of their earnings. This hypothetical experiment serves as a standard against which every observational method is to be judged: each technique in this book is an attempt to approximate it when assignment is not under the analyst's control. The formulation also functions as a diagnostic. If the experiment cannot be stated precisely, the causal question itself has not been posed precisely, and no estimation procedure can supply the missing clarity.

    • Four data structures — cross-section, time series, panel, and pooled cross-sections — determine which methods apply (Section 1.5).
    • The pandas library provides the tools for loading data and computing means, group means, and correlations (Section 1.6).

    Chapter 2 · The Workhorse

    Simple Linear Regression

    The Question

    By how much does hourly earnings change, on average, with one additional year of schooling?

    Arena⚔ Least-Squares Archer →

    This chapter develops the simple linear regression model, the foundation of the entire book. It specifies the model and its assumptions, derives the ordinary least squares estimator by minimization, establishes its unbiasedness and sampling variance, defines the coefficient of determination, and presents its estimation in Python. The multiple-regression model of Chapter 3 and every subsequent method are extensions of the apparatus introduced here.

    2.1 The model and its assumptions

    Definition 2.1 — Simple linear regression model

    The model is \( y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x + u \), where \(y\) is the outcome, \(x\) the explanatory variable, \(\beta_0\) and \(\beta_1\) unknown population parameters (the intercept and slope), and \(u\) the disturbance, comprising all determinants of \(y\) other than \(x\). The slope \(\beta_1\) is the change in \(y\) associated with a one-unit change in \(x\); it is the ceteris paribus effect of \(x\) on \(y\) only under Assumption SLR.4 below.

    Assumptions SLR.1–SLR.5
    1. SLR.1 (linearity in parameters): \(y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x + u\). The restriction is on how the parameters enter; \(x\) may itself be a transformation such as \(\log\) or a square.
    2. SLR.2 (random sampling): the \(n\) observations are drawn independently from the population.
    3. SLR.3 (variation in \(x\)): \(\sum_i (x_i-\bar x)^2 > 0\); the explanatory variable is not constant.
    4. SLR.4 (zero conditional mean): \(\mathrm{E}[u\mid x]=0\). The disturbance has mean zero at every value of \(x\); equivalently, \(x\) is uncorrelated with all other determinants of \(y\). This is the identifying assumption for a causal interpretation.
    5. SLR.5 (homoskedasticity): \(\mathrm{Var}(u\mid x)=\sigma^2\), constant across \(x\). Required for the standard-error formula of Section 2.3, not for unbiasedness; relaxed in Chapter 7.

    2.2 The ordinary least squares estimator

    Given estimates \(\hat\beta_0,\hat\beta_1\), the fitted value for observation \(i\) is \(\hat y_i = \hat\beta_0 + \hat\beta_1 x_i\) and the residual is \(\hat u_i = y_i - \hat y_i\). The ordinary least squares (OLS) estimator chooses \(\hat\beta_0,\hat\beta_1\) to minimize the sum of squared residuals,

    \[ \mathrm{SSR}(\hat\beta_0,\hat\beta_1) = \sum_{i=1}^{n}\big(y_i - \hat\beta_0 - \hat\beta_1 x_i\big)^2. \]

    Squaring renders the criterion non-negative and differentiable and penalizes large residuals more than small ones. The minimum is obtained by setting the partial derivatives to zero, yielding the normal equations

    \[ \sum_i \hat u_i = 0, \qquad \sum_i x_i \hat u_i = 0, \]

    which state that the residuals have mean zero and are uncorrelated with the regressor. Solving them gives

    \[ \hat\beta_1 = \frac{\sum_i (x_i-\bar x)(y_i-\bar y)}{\sum_i (x_i-\bar x)^2}, \qquad \hat\beta_0 = \bar y - \hat\beta_1\bar x. \]

    The slope is the sample covariation of \(x\) and \(y\) divided by the sample variation of \(x\); the intercept formula ensures the fitted line passes through the point of means \((\bar x,\bar y)\).

    2.3 Properties of the estimator

    Proposition 2.1 — Unbiasedness

    Under SLR.1–SLR.4, \(\mathrm{E}[\hat\beta_1]=\beta_1\). Proof sketch. Substituting \(y_i=\beta_0+\beta_1x_i+u_i\) into the slope formula gives \(\hat\beta_1 = \beta_1 + \sum_i w_i u_i\), where \(w_i = (x_i-\bar x)/\sum_j (x_j-\bar x)^2\) depends only on the regressor. Taking the expectation conditional on the \(x_i\) and applying SLR.4, \(\mathrm{E}[\sum_i w_i u_i \mid x]=\sum_i w_i\,\mathrm{E}[u_i\mid x]=0\). Hence \(\mathrm{E}[\hat\beta_1\mid x]=\beta_1\), and unconditionally \(\mathrm{E}[\hat\beta_1]=\beta_1\).

    Proposition 2.2 — Sampling variance

    Under SLR.1–SLR.5, \( \mathrm{Var}(\hat\beta_1\mid x) = \dfrac{\sigma^2}{\sum_i (x_i-\bar x)^2}. \) The variance falls with the error variance \(\sigma^2\), and rises as the variation in \(x\) falls. The error variance is estimated by \(\hat\sigma^2 = \mathrm{SSR}/(n-2)\), the divisor \(n-2\) reflecting the two estimated parameters. Under SLR.1–SLR.5 the OLS estimator is, moreover, the minimum-variance linear unbiased estimator (the Gauss–Markov theorem).

    2.4 Goodness of fit

    Decompose the total variation in \(y\) into explained and residual components. With \(\mathrm{SST}=\sum_i (y_i-\bar y)^2\), \(\mathrm{SSE}=\sum_i (\hat y_i-\bar y)^2\), and \(\mathrm{SSR}=\sum_i \hat u_i^2\), the normal equations imply \(\mathrm{SST}=\mathrm{SSE}+\mathrm{SSR}\). The coefficient of determination is

    \[ R^2 = \frac{\mathrm{SSE}}{\mathrm{SST}} = 1 - \frac{\mathrm{SSR}}{\mathrm{SST}} \in [0,1], \]

    the proportion of the variation in \(y\) accounted for by the regression. \(R^2\) measures fit, not the validity of a causal interpretation; a high \(R^2\) is neither necessary nor sufficient for an unbiased estimate of \(\beta_1\).

    Worked Example 2.1

    For the five individuals of Chapter 1 (\(x=(10,12,12,16,16)\), \(y=(14,15,18,24,30)\)), the means are \(\bar x = 13.2\) and \(\bar y = 20.2\). The required sums are \(\sum (x-\bar x)(y-\bar y)=66.8\) and \(\sum (x-\bar x)^2 = 28.8\), giving \(\hat\beta_1 = 66.8/28.8 = 2.32\) and \(\hat\beta_0 = 20.2 - 2.32(13.2) = -10.4\). The fitted line is \(\widehat{\text{wage}} = -10.4 + 2.32\,\text{educ}\), with \(R^2 = 0.86\). The intercept lies far outside the range of the data and is not interpretable as the earnings of an individual with no schooling.

    2.5 Estimating a regression in Python

    A regression is estimated with statsmodels using a formula of the form "y ~ x", in which an intercept is included by default. The fitted object exposes the coefficients, their standard errors, and the goodness-of-fit statistics.

    import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

# the formula "y ~ x" specifies a regression of y on x with an intercept
result = smf.ols("wage ~ educ", data=df).fit()
print(result.summary())            # full output: coefficients, standard errors, R-squared

b0 = result.params["Intercept"]    # estimated intercept
b1 = result.params["educ"]         # estimated slope
print(b1)

    The estimates may be reproduced from the formulae of Section 2.2 directly in numpy, which confirms that the regression coefficient is the covariation of \(x\) and \(y\) divided by the variation of \(x\).

    import numpy as np

x = df["educ"].values
y = df["wage"].values
xbar, ybar = x.mean(), y.mean()

# slope = covariation of x and y, divided by variation of x
b1 = np.sum((x - xbar) * (y - ybar)) / np.sum((x - xbar)**2)
b0 = ybar - b1 * xbar
print(b0, b1)                      # identical to the statsmodels estimates

    ★ Sharma's Take

    The sampling variance of the slope, \(\sigma^2/\sum(x_i-\bar x)^2\), shows that precision is determined not only by sample size but by the dispersion of the explanatory variable: the estimate is sharp when \(x\) varies widely and imprecise when \(x\) is concentrated, irrespective of \(n\). This has a consequence for the design of data collection that is frequently overlooked. Where the analyst can influence which observations are gathered, precision is increased by deliberately securing variation in the explanatory variable of interest, rather than by accumulating observations that are similar in that variable. A moderate sample with wide variation in \(x\) yields a more precise slope than a large sample in which \(x\) is nearly constant. Precision is a property to be designed into the data, not merely estimated from them.

    Chapter 3 · Controlling for Confounders

    Multiple Regression and Omitted-Variable Bias

    The Question

    Is the estimated return to schooling a genuine effect of schooling, or does it reflect ability, which is correlated with both schooling and earnings?

    Arena⚔ The Omitted-Variable Detective →

    The simple regression of Chapter 2 attributes a causal interpretation to the slope only under the strong assumption that the regressor is uncorrelated with all other determinants of the outcome. Multiple regression weakens this requirement by including observed confounders as additional regressors. This chapter defines the multiple-regression model, establishes through the Frisch–Waugh–Lovell theorem the precise sense in which it “controls for” a variable, derives the omitted-variable bias formula, and treats the consequences of collinearity.

    3.1 The model

    Definition 3.1 — Multiple regression model

    The model is \( y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + \cdots + \beta_k x_k + u \). The coefficient \(\beta_j\) is the change in \(y\) associated with a one-unit change in \(x_j\) holding the other regressors fixed — the partial effect of \(x_j\).

    Assumptions MLR.1–MLR.4
    1. MLR.1 (linearity in parameters): \(y=\beta_0+\sum_{j}\beta_j x_j+u\).
    2. MLR.2 (random sampling).
    3. MLR.3 (no perfect collinearity): no regressor is an exact linear combination of the others. Exact collinearity makes the partial effects unidentifiable; imperfect collinearity is permitted.
    4. MLR.4 (zero conditional mean): \(\mathrm{E}[u\mid x_1,\dots,x_k]=0\). The disturbance has mean zero given all regressors; the identifying assumption now requires only that the disturbance be uncorrelated with the included regressors.

    OLS estimates \(\hat\beta_0,\dots,\hat\beta_k\) by minimizing the sum of squared residuals, exactly as in Chapter 2; under MLR.1–MLR.4 each \(\hat\beta_j\) is unbiased for \(\beta_j\).

    3.2 The meaning of “controlling for”: the Frisch–Waugh–Lovell theorem

    Proposition 3.1 — Frisch–Waugh–Lovell

    The multiple-regression coefficient \(\hat\beta_1\) equals the slope from the simple regression of \(y\) on \(\tilde x_1\), where \(\tilde x_1\) is the residual from regressing \(x_1\) on all the other regressors. Equivalently, \(\hat\beta_1\) is obtained by (i) removing from \(x_1\) the part explained by the other regressors, and (ii) relating \(y\) to the remaining variation in \(x_1\).

    The theorem makes the operation of “controlling for” precise. The coefficient on schooling in a regression that also includes experience and ability is computed from the variation in schooling that is orthogonal to experience and ability — that is, from comparisons among individuals with the same experience and ability who differ in schooling. It is in this exact sense that multiple regression approximates the ceteris paribus comparison of Chapter 1.

    Key Idea

    A regression coefficient changes when a control is added because it is then computed from a different, residualized regressor — it answers a different question. Neither the controlled nor the uncontrolled estimate is automatically correct; the appropriate set of controls is the set under which MLR.4 holds.

    3.3 Omitted-variable bias

    Suppose the population model is \(y=\beta_0+\beta_1 x_1+\beta_2 x_2+u\) but \(x_2\) is omitted, and the short regression of \(y\) on \(x_1\) alone is estimated. Let \(\delta_1\) be the slope from regressing \(x_2\) on \(x_1\). Then the short-regression coefficient \(\tilde\beta_1\) satisfies

    \[ \mathrm{E}[\tilde\beta_1] = \beta_1 + \beta_2\,\delta_1. \]
    Definition 3.2 — Omitted-variable bias

    The bias \(\beta_2\,\delta_1\) is the product of the effect of the omitted variable on the outcome (\(\beta_2\)) and the regression coefficient of the omitted variable on the included variable (\(\delta_1\)). Its sign is determined by the signs of these two factors, both of which can typically be reasoned about from subject-matter knowledge prior to estimation.

    For schooling and ability, \(\beta_2>0\) (ability raises earnings) and \(\delta_1>0\) (ability rises with schooling), so the bias is positive: omitting ability causes the schooling coefficient to overstate the causal return. The direction of the bias is thus predictable in advance.

    3.4 Collinearity

    When regressors are highly but not perfectly correlated, the partial effects remain identified but are imprecisely estimated. The variance of \(\hat\beta_j\) is

    \[ \mathrm{Var}(\hat\beta_j) = \frac{\sigma^2}{\mathrm{SST}_j\,(1-R_j^2)}, \]

    where \(R_j^2\) is the coefficient of determination from regressing \(x_j\) on the other regressors. As \(R_j^2\to 1\), the variance diverges. Collinearity therefore inflates standard errors but introduces no bias: the estimates remain unbiased, merely imprecise. Deleting a collinear regressor to reduce the standard error reintroduces omitted-variable bias and is generally inadvisable; the appropriate response is additional data.

    3.5 Estimating multiple regression in Python

    Additional regressors are included by extending the formula with +. Comparing the coefficient on the variable of interest across specifications reveals the influence of the added controls.

    import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

# additional regressors are added with "+"
short = smf.ols("wage ~ educ", data=df).fit()
long  = smf.ols("wage ~ educ + exper + IQ", data=df).fit()

print(short.params["educ"])        # schooling coefficient, no controls
print(long.params["educ"])         # schooling coefficient, controlling for exper and IQ

    The Frisch–Waugh–Lovell theorem can be verified directly: residualize both the outcome and the regressor of interest against the remaining controls, then regress one residual on the other.

    # Frisch-Waugh-Lovell: the multiple-regression coefficient on educ equals the
# slope from regressing residualized wage on residualized educ
e_y = smf.ols("wage ~ exper + IQ", data=df).fit().resid     # wage with controls removed
e_x = smf.ols("educ ~ exper + IQ", data=df).fit().resid     # educ with controls removed

import numpy as np
b_fwl = np.sum(e_x * e_y) / np.sum(e_x**2)
print(b_fwl)                       # equals long.params["educ"]

    Chapter 4 · Statistical Inference

    Sampling Uncertainty, Confidence Intervals, and Tests

    The Question

    An estimate is computed from one sample. How is its uncertainty quantified, and how is the hypothesis that an effect is zero to be assessed?

    Arena⚔ The p-Hacking Casino →⚔ The Bootstrap Forge →

    An estimate without a measure of its uncertainty is uninterpretable. This chapter develops statistical inference for the regression coefficients: the origin of sampling uncertainty, the sampling distribution of the estimator, the standard error, confidence intervals, hypothesis tests for single and joint restrictions, and their computation in Python. The probabilistic concepts required are introduced as they arise; no prior study of statistics is assumed.

    4.1 The sampling distribution

    Distinguish the population, the entirety of units of interest, from the sample, the subset observed. The parameter \(\beta_1\) is a fixed feature of the population; the estimator \(\hat\beta_1\) is computed from the sample and varies across samples. A different sample, drawn from the same population, would yield a different value of \(\hat\beta_1\).

    Definition 4.1 — Sampling distribution

    The sampling distribution of an estimator is the distribution of its values across the repeated samples that might be drawn from the population. Inference concerns two features of this distribution: its centre, which determines bias, and its dispersion, which determines precision.

    Under MLR.1–MLR.4 the estimator is unbiased: the sampling distribution is centred on \(\beta_1\) (Chapter 2). The remaining task is to characterize its dispersion and shape.

    4.2 Normality of the estimator

    The estimator is a linear function of the disturbances: \(\hat\beta_1 = \beta_1 + \sum_i w_i u_i\) (Chapter 2). A sum of many independent contributions is governed by the central limit theorem, which states that such a sum is approximately normally distributed regardless of the distribution of the individual terms, provided the sample is large.

    Proposition 4.1 — Approximate normality

    Under MLR.1–MLR.4 and random sampling, for large \(n\) the standardized estimator \((\hat\beta_1-\beta_1)/\mathrm{se}(\hat\beta_1)\) is approximately standard normal. If, in addition, the disturbances are exactly normal (an assumption labelled MLR.6), the result holds exactly in finite samples, with the standard normal replaced by Student's \(t\) distribution with \(n-k-1\) degrees of freedom.

    The practical consequence is that the sampling distribution is bell-shaped: estimates near \(\beta_1\) are common and estimates far from it are rare, with the rate of decline governed by the dispersion of the distribution.

    4.3 The standard error

    Definition 4.2 — Standard error

    The standard error of \(\hat\beta_1\), denoted \(\mathrm{se}(\hat\beta_1)\), is the estimated standard deviation of its sampling distribution — the typical magnitude of the deviation of the estimate from \(\beta_1\). For the simple-regression slope, \(\mathrm{se}(\hat\beta_1)=\hat\sigma/\sqrt{\sum_i(x_i-\bar x)^2}\), where \(\hat\sigma^2=\mathrm{SSR}/(n-k-1)\).

    The standard error is computed from a single sample; it estimates the dispersion that would be observed across repeated samples without requiring them. It decreases with the residual dispersion \(\hat\sigma\) and increases as the variation in the regressor falls, reproducing the variance formula of Chapter 2.

    The two-standard-error rule

    For a bell-shaped sampling distribution, approximately 95% of estimates lie within two standard errors of the centre (more precisely, 1.96). This fact underlies both confidence intervals and tests.

    4.4 Confidence intervals

    Definition 4.3 — Confidence interval

    The 95% confidence interval for \(\beta_1\) is \(\hat\beta_1 \pm c\cdot\mathrm{se}(\hat\beta_1)\), where \(c\approx 1.96\) for large samples. The interval is constructed by a procedure that, across repeated samples, contains the true \(\beta_1\) in 95% of instances.

    The confidence interval reports magnitude and precision jointly: a narrow interval distant from zero and a wide interval containing zero may share the same point estimate yet convey different conclusions. It is generally more informative than a test outcome alone.

    4.5 Hypothesis tests

    To test the null hypothesis \(H_0:\beta_1=0\) against the alternative \(H_1:\beta_1\ne 0\), form the \(t\)-statistic \(t=\hat\beta_1/\mathrm{se}(\hat\beta_1)\), the number of standard errors separating the estimate from zero. Under \(H_0\), \(t\) follows the \(t_{n-k-1}\) distribution. The hypothesis is rejected at the 5% level when \(|t|\) exceeds the critical value (approximately 2 for large samples).

    Definition 4.4 — p-value

    The p-value is the probability, computed under the null hypothesis, of obtaining a test statistic at least as extreme as the one observed. It is not the probability that the null hypothesis is true, nor the probability that the result is due to chance; it is a measure, computed under the assumption that the null holds, of the discrepancy between the data and that assumption.

    Joint tests. To test that a set of \(q\) coefficients are jointly zero, compare the residual sum of squares of the unrestricted and restricted models through the \(F\)-statistic

    \[ F = \frac{(\mathrm{SSR}_r - \mathrm{SSR}_{ur})/q}{\mathrm{SSR}_{ur}/(n-k-1)} \sim F_{q,\,n-k-1}. \]

    A large \(F\) indicates that the restricted model fits substantially worse, leading to rejection of the joint null. The \(F\)-test is the appropriate instrument when collinearity renders individual coefficients insignificant though the group is jointly significant.

    4.6 Inference in Python

    The fitted statsmodels object exposes the standard errors, \(t\)-statistics, p-values, and confidence intervals for all coefficients.

    import statsmodels.formula.api as smf
result = smf.ols("wage ~ educ + exper", data=df).fit()

print(result.bse["educ"])          # standard error of the schooling coefficient
print(result.tvalues["educ"])      # t-statistic for H0: coefficient = 0
print(result.pvalues["educ"])      # corresponding p-value
print(result.conf_int().loc["educ"])   # 95% confidence interval

    Joint hypotheses are tested with the f_test method, which accepts a string specifying the restrictions.

    # joint test that experience and its square contribute nothing: H0 both coefficients = 0
result = smf.ols("wage ~ educ + exper + I(exper**2)", data=df).fit()
ftest = result.f_test("exper = 0, I(exper ** 2) = 0")
print(ftest)                       # F-statistic and its p-value

    Chapter 5 · Functional Form

    Logarithms, Polynomials, and Interactions

    The Question

    How are relationships that are proportional, curved, or dependent on a second variable represented within a model that remains linear in its parameters?

    Arena⚔ The Model Zoo and Specification Safari →

    The linearity required by the regression model is linearity in the parameters, not in the variables. A wide class of nonlinear relationships is therefore accommodated by transforming the variables — through logarithms, powers, and products — while estimation proceeds by ordinary least squares unchanged. This chapter treats the logarithmic transformation and its interpretation as a proportional effect, the elasticity, polynomial terms and their level-dependent marginal effects, and interactions, together with their estimation in Python.

    5.1 The logarithmic transformation

    The natural logarithm \(\ln(\cdot)\) converts a multiplicative scale into an additive one: equal differences in \(\ln(y)\) correspond to equal proportional differences in \(y\). For small changes,

    \[ \Delta\ln(y) \approx \frac{\Delta y}{y}, \]

    the proportional change in \(y\). Consequently a coefficient on a regressor in a regression of \(\ln(y)\) is interpreted as a proportional, or percentage, effect.

    Definition 5.1 — Interpretation of logarithmic specifications
    • Level–level (\(y\) on \(x\)): a one-unit increase in \(x\) changes \(y\) by \(\beta_1\) units.
    • Log–level (\(\ln y\) on \(x\)): a one-unit increase in \(x\) changes \(y\) by approximately \(100\beta_1\) percent.
    • Level–log (\(y\) on \(\ln x\)): a one-percent increase in \(x\) changes \(y\) by approximately \(\beta_1/100\) units.
    • Log–log (\(\ln y\) on \(\ln x\)): a one-percent increase in \(x\) changes \(y\) by approximately \(\beta_1\) percent. Here \(\beta_1\) is the elasticity of \(y\) with respect to \(x\).

    5.2 Polynomial terms

    A quadratic specification \( y=\beta_0+\beta_1 x+\beta_2 x^2+u \) permits the relationship to curve. The marginal effect is no longer constant: differentiating, \(\partial y/\partial x = \beta_1+2\beta_2 x\), which varies with \(x\). When \(\beta_2<0\) the relationship rises and then falls, attaining a maximum at the turning point \(x^\ast=-\beta_1/(2\beta_2)\).

    Key Idea

    In any specification containing a power of a regressor, the effect of that regressor is not a single coefficient but a function of its level, \(\beta_1+2\beta_2 x\). It must be reported at specified values — typically the mean or several points across the range — rather than as one number.

    5.3 Interactions

    An interaction term represents an effect that depends on a second variable. In \( y=\beta_0+\beta_1 x_1+\beta_2 x_2+\beta_3 x_1 x_2+u \), the effect of \(x_1\) is \(\partial y/\partial x_1 = \beta_1+\beta_3 x_2\), a function of \(x_2\). A nonzero \(\beta_3\) indicates that the two variables modify each other's effects; the sign of \(\beta_3\) determines whether they are complementary or substitutive.

    5.4 Influential observations

    Because the OLS criterion weights observations by their distance from the mean of the regressor (Chapter 2), a small number of extreme observations can determine the estimated form. A nonlinear specification supported only by a few outlying points is not robust. Two safeguards are standard: inspection of a scatter plot before adopting a functional form, and re-estimation with the extreme observations excluded to confirm that the conclusion does not depend on them.

    5.5 Functional forms in Python

    Transformations are written directly into the formula. The function np.log applies the logarithm; the wrapper I(...) protects an arithmetic expression such as a square; the colon : forms an interaction.

    import numpy as np
import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

# log outcome: coefficient is approximately a proportional (percentage) effect
m_log = smf.ols("np.log(wage) ~ educ", data=df).fit()

# quadratic: I(...) protects an arithmetic expression inside a formula
m_quad = smf.ols("wage ~ exper + I(exper**2)", data=df).fit()

# interaction: the colon forms the product term
m_int = smf.ols("wage ~ educ + exper + educ:exper", data=df).fit()

    In a polynomial specification the marginal effect and the turning point are computed from the coefficients.

    # in the quadratic model the marginal effect of experience depends on its level
b1 = m_quad.params["exper"]
b2 = m_quad.params["I(exper ** 2)"]
exper0 = 10
marginal_effect = b1 + 2*b2*exper0       # derivative of the fitted curve at exper = 10
turning_point   = -b1 / (2*b2)           # experience level at which the curve peaks
print(marginal_effect, turning_point)

    Chapter 6 · Categorical Regressors

    Dummy Variables

    The Question

    How are qualitative characteristics — union membership, region, sex — incorporated into a regression, and how are the resulting coefficients interpreted?

    Many determinants of economic outcomes are qualitative rather than quantitative. They are incorporated through dummy variables, which encode category membership numerically. This chapter establishes the interpretation of a dummy coefficient as a difference in conditional means, the representation of multi-category variables, the dummy-variable trap, and interactions between dummies and continuous regressors, together with their estimation in Python.

    6.1 The binary regressor

    Definition 6.1 — Dummy variable

    A dummy (or indicator) variable takes the value 1 when a condition holds and 0 otherwise. In the regression \( y=\beta_0+\beta_1 d+u \) with \(d\) a dummy, the conditional mean of \(y\) is \(\beta_0\) for the group with \(d=0\) and \(\beta_0+\beta_1\) for the group with \(d=1\). The coefficient \(\beta_1\) is the difference in mean outcome between the two groups.

    The group coded zero is the base (or reference) group, against which the dummy coefficient is measured. When the dummy is included alongside other regressors, its coefficient becomes the between-group difference holding those regressors fixed — a conditional, rather than unconditional, mean difference.

    6.2 Multi-category variables and the dummy-variable trap

    A categorical variable with \(m\) categories is represented by \(m-1\) dummies, one category being omitted as the base. Each coefficient is the difference in mean outcome between its category and the base. Including all \(m\) dummies together with an intercept produces exact collinearity, since the dummies sum to one for every observation, duplicating the intercept; this is the dummy-variable trap, a violation of MLR.3.

    Key Idea

    One category must be omitted to serve as the base. All coefficients are then interpreted relative to that base; the choice of base does not affect the fitted values but determines which comparisons the coefficients express, and should be made for interpretability.

    6.3 Interactions with continuous regressors

    A dummy interacted with a continuous regressor permits the slope to differ across groups. In \( y=\beta_0+\beta_1 x+\beta_2 d+\beta_3 (d\cdot x)+u \), the relationship for the base group (\(d=0\)) is \(\beta_0+\beta_1 x\) and for the other group (\(d=1\)) is \((\beta_0+\beta_2)+(\beta_1+\beta_3)x\). Thus \(\beta_2\) shifts the intercept and \(\beta_3\) shifts the slope. The hypothesis that the two groups share the same regression line is the joint hypothesis \(\beta_2=\beta_3=0\), tested by an \(F\)-statistic (the Chow test).

    6.4 Dummy variables in Python

    A binary variable coded 0/1 enters the formula directly. A categorical variable is wrapped in C(...), which constructs the required dummies and omits a base level automatically; the base may be specified with Treatment.

    Chapter 7 · Non-Constant Variance

    Heteroskedasticity

    The Question

    When the dispersion of the disturbance varies with the regressors, are the coefficient estimates and their standard errors still valid?

    Arena⚔ The Heteroskedasticity Thermal Camera →

    The homoskedasticity assumption SLR.5/MLR.5 — that the disturbance has constant variance across observations — is routinely violated in economic data. This chapter establishes the consequences: the OLS estimator remains unbiased, but its conventional standard errors are invalid, so that inference is distorted. The remedy, heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors, is developed and shown to be the appropriate default, together with the formal test for non-constant variance and the Python implementation.

    7.1 Definition and consequences

    Definition 7.1 — Homoskedasticity and heteroskedasticity

    The disturbance is homoskedastic if \(\mathrm{Var}(u\mid x)=\sigma^2\), constant across the regressors, and heteroskedastic if \(\mathrm{Var}(u\mid x)\) varies with them.

    Heteroskedasticity is the typical condition of cross-sectional economic data: units with larger values of a regressor frequently exhibit greater dispersion in the outcome. Its consequences are precisely delimited.

    Proposition 7.1

    Under MLR.1–MLR.4, the OLS estimator is unbiased irrespective of the form of \(\mathrm{Var}(u\mid x)\). Heteroskedasticity does not bias the coefficients. However, the conventional variance formula of Chapter 4, which assumes homoskedasticity, is no longer valid; the conventional standard errors are therefore incorrect, and the associated \(t\)- and \(F\)-statistics, confidence intervals, and p-values are invalid.

    Unbiasedness follows because it depends only on MLR.4, the conditional mean of the disturbance, not on its variance. The conventional standard errors are typically too small under heteroskedasticity, overstating precision and significance.

    Key Idea

    Heteroskedasticity affects the standard errors, not the coefficients. The remedy is therefore not to alter the estimator but to compute its standard errors by a method valid under non-constant variance.

    7.2 Robust standard errors

    The heteroskedasticity-robust (or heteroskedasticity-consistent) standard errors, due to White, estimate the variance of the OLS estimator without assuming constant error variance, allowing each observation its own variance. They are valid whether or not heteroskedasticity is present: under homoskedasticity they coincide asymptotically with the conventional standard errors, and under heteroskedasticity they remain valid where the conventional standard errors fail. As they impose no cost when homoskedasticity holds and restore validity when it does not, they are the appropriate default for cross-sectional regression.

    7.3 Testing for heteroskedasticity

    The Breusch–Pagan test assesses whether the squared residuals are systematically related to the regressors. Its null hypothesis is homoskedasticity; rejection indicates non-constant variance. The related White test admits more general forms by including squares and cross-products of the regressors. In practice, because robust standard errors are valid regardless of the test's outcome, the tests are diagnostic rather than decisive; applied work generally proceeds directly to robust standard errors.

    7.4 Robust inference in Python

    Robust standard errors are obtained by passing cov_type="HC1" to the fit method. The coefficients are unchanged; only the standard errors, and the statistics derived from them, differ.

    import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

ols    = smf.ols("wage ~ educ + exper", data=df).fit()                 # classical SE
robust = smf.ols("wage ~ educ + exper", data=df).fit(cov_type="HC1")   # robust SE

print(ols.bse["educ"])      # standard error assuming constant error variance
print(robust.bse["educ"])   # standard error valid under heteroskedasticity
# the coefficient is identical; only the standard error changes

    The Breusch–Pagan test is available in statsmodels.stats.

    import statsmodels.stats.api as sms

# Breusch-Pagan test: H0 is constant error variance (homoskedasticity)
bp = sms.het_breuschpagan(ols.resid, ols.model.exog)
print("LM statistic:", bp[0], "  p-value:", bp[1])

    Chapter 8 · Measurement and Specification

    Measurement Error and Proxy Variables

    The Question

    When a regressor is observed with error — reported rather than true income, a test score rather than ability — what is the consequence for the estimated coefficient?

    Economic variables are measured imperfectly. This chapter establishes the consequences of measurement error, which differ sharply according to whether the error is in the outcome or in a regressor: error in the outcome reduces precision but preserves unbiasedness, whereas error in a regressor biases its coefficient toward zero, a phenomenon termed attenuation. The chapter relates attenuation to the use of proxy variables, treats the detection of functional-form misspecification, and presents the relevant Python procedures.

    8.1 Measurement error in the outcome

    Suppose the outcome is measured with error, the observed value being the true value plus a random term uncorrelated with the regressors. The measurement error is absorbed into the disturbance. Provided it satisfies MLR.4, the OLS estimator remains unbiased; the only consequence is a larger disturbance variance, hence larger standard errors. Measurement error in the outcome reduces precision but does not bias the coefficients.

    8.2 Measurement error in a regressor: attenuation

    Now suppose a regressor is measured with error: the observed regressor equals the true regressor plus an independent random term (the classical errors-in-variables model). The observed regressor contains a component — the error — that is unrelated to the outcome, so the estimated coefficient is biased toward zero.

    Proposition 8.1 — Attenuation

    Under the classical errors-in-variables model with a single regressor, the OLS coefficient on the mismeasured regressor satisfies

    \[ \operatorname{plim}\hat\beta_1 = \beta_1\cdot\frac{\sigma_{x^\ast}^2}{\sigma_{x^\ast}^2+\sigma_e^2}, \]

    where \(\sigma_{x^\ast}^2\) is the variance of the true regressor and \(\sigma_e^2\) the variance of the measurement error. The multiplicative factor — the reliability ratio — lies strictly between zero and one, so the estimate is biased toward zero; the bias increases with the noise variance.

    Key Idea

    Classical measurement error in the outcome reduces precision but preserves unbiasedness; classical measurement error in a regressor biases its coefficient toward zero. The distinction is essential: a noisy outcome warrants no special concern, whereas a noisy regressor warrants careful attention.

    8.3 Proxy variables

    When a determinant of the outcome cannot be measured, an observable correlate — a proxy — may be substituted, as in the use of a test score for ability in Chapter 3. By Proposition 8.1, a proxy measured with error has an attenuated coefficient and therefore removes only part of the confounding it is intended to control. This accounts for the pattern observed in Chapter 3, in which the coefficient on the variable of interest continued to move as successively better proxies were introduced: each imperfect proxy performs only partial adjustment. A coefficient that has not stabilized across improving proxies should be regarded as incompletely corrected.

    8.4 Functional-form misspecification

    A distinct specification error is an incorrect functional form — the omission of a required nonlinear term (Chapter 5). The RESET test assesses this by adding powers of the fitted values to the regression and testing their joint significance; a significant result indicates that the conditional mean is misspecified. It serves as a general diagnostic for omitted nonlinearity.

    8.5 Measurement error and specification tests in Python

    The attenuation result is demonstrated by adding noise of known variance to a correctly measured regressor and observing the resulting coefficient.

    Functional-form misspecification is assessed with the RESET test.

    Chapter 9 · Endogeneity

    Instrumental Variables

    The Question

    When a confounder cannot be measured, and no adequate proxy exists, can the causal effect of a regressor nonetheless be estimated?

    Arena⚔ The Instrument Plumber →

    When a regressor is correlated with the disturbance — through an unobserved confounder, reverse causation, or measurement error — OLS is biased and no set of observed controls suffices. The method of instrumental variables estimates the effect by isolating variation in the regressor that is unrelated to the disturbance. This chapter defines endogeneity, the requirements an instrument must satisfy, the two-stage least squares estimator, the problem of weak instruments, and the Python implementation.

    9.1 Endogeneity

    Definition 9.1 — Endogeneity

    A regressor is endogenous if it is correlated with the disturbance, \(\mathrm{Cov}(x,u)\ne 0\), in violation of MLR.4. The OLS estimator is then biased and inconsistent. Three sources are standard: an omitted variable correlated with both the regressor and the outcome; simultaneity, in which the outcome also influences the regressor; and measurement error in the regressor (Chapter 8).

    9.2 The instrument

    An instrument \(z\) is a variable that induces variation in the endogenous regressor that is unrelated to the disturbance. The endogenous regressor contains variation correlated with the disturbance (which biases OLS) and, through the instrument, variation that is not; the method uses only the latter.

    Definition 9.2 — Conditions for a valid instrument
    • Relevance: \(\mathrm{Cov}(z,x)\ne 0\); the instrument is correlated with the endogenous regressor. This condition is testable from the first-stage regression.
    • Exogeneity (the exclusion restriction): \(\mathrm{Cov}(z,u)=0\); the instrument is uncorrelated with the disturbance, affecting the outcome only through the regressor. This condition is not testable and must be justified by argument.

    9.3 Two-stage least squares

    The two-stage least squares (2SLS) estimator implements the use of instrument-induced variation in two steps.

    Proposition 9.1 — 2SLS

    First stage: regress the endogenous regressor on the instrument (and any exogenous controls), obtaining the fitted values — the component of the regressor explained by the instrument. Second stage: regress the outcome on these fitted values (and the controls). The resulting coefficient is the 2SLS estimate. Because the fitted values depend only on the instrument and the exogenous controls, they are uncorrelated with the disturbance, and the estimate is consistent under Definition 9.2.

    Estimation should be carried out by a dedicated 2SLS routine rather than by literally running two regressions, because the latter produces incorrect standard errors in the second stage.

    9.4 Weak instruments

    When the instrument is only weakly correlated with the endogenous regressor, the instrument-induced variation is small, and the 2SLS estimator is imprecise and may exhibit substantial bias — in extreme cases exceeding that of OLS. The strength of the instrument is assessed by the \(F\)-statistic on the excluded instrument in the first-stage regression; a common rule of thumb requires it to exceed ten.

    Key Idea

    A valid instrument must satisfy both relevance and exogeneity. Relevance is verifiable from the first stage; exogeneity is not testable and rests on argument. A weak instrument (low relevance) yields imprecise and possibly biased estimates; a violation of exogeneity yields inconsistency that no diagnostic detects.

    9.5 Instrumental-variables estimation in Python

    Two-stage least squares is provided by the linearmodels library, whose formula syntax places the endogenous regressor and its instruments in square brackets. The routine computes correct standard errors.

    from linearmodels.iv import IV2SLS

# syntax: dependent ~ exogenous + [endogenous ~ instruments]
# here educ is endogenous and z is the instrument; exper is an exogenous control
model = IV2SLS.from_formula("wage ~ 1 + exper + [educ ~ z]", data=df)
result = model.fit(cov_type="robust")
print(result.summary)
print(result.params["educ"])       # the 2SLS estimate of the schooling effect

    The strength of the instrument is assessed by the first-stage \(F\)-statistic on the excluded instrument.

    import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

# first stage: regress the endogenous regressor on the instrument and controls
first = smf.ols("educ ~ z + exper", data=df).fit()
# strength of the instrument: F-statistic on the excluded instrument (rule of thumb > 10)
print(first.f_test("z = 0"))

If any plausible such channel exists, the exclusion restriction is doubtful, and no statistical procedure will disclose the resulting inconsistency. The weakness of an instrument announces itself in the first-stage statistics; a violation of exogeneity does not, and it is therefore the point on which scrutiny should concentrate.

    Chapter 10 · Panel Data

    Fixed Effects

    The Question

    When the same units are observed over several periods, can the influence of their unobserved, time-invariant characteristics be eliminated?

    Arena⚔ The Fixed-Effects X-Ray →

    Panel data — repeated observations on the same units — permit a powerful strategy against unobserved confounding. If a confounder is a fixed characteristic of the unit, it can be removed by comparing each unit only with itself over time, whether or not the confounder is measured. This chapter develops the fixed-effects model, the within transformation that eliminates the unit effects, the properties and limitations of the resulting estimator, and its computation in Python.

    10.1 Panel data and the fixed-effects model

    Panel data index each observation by unit \(i\) and time \(t\). The fixed-effects model assigns each unit its own intercept:

    \[ y_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta_1 x_{it} + u_{it}, \]

    where \(\alpha_i\), the fixed effect, captures all time-invariant characteristics of unit \(i\) — observed or unobserved. The parameter \(\beta_1\) is identified from variation within units over time.

    10.2 The within transformation

    Averaging the model over time for unit \(i\) gives \(\bar y_i = \alpha_i + \beta_1 \bar x_i + \bar u_i\). Subtracting this from the original equation eliminates the fixed effect:

    \[ (y_{it}-\bar y_i) = \beta_1 (x_{it}-\bar x_i) + (u_{it}-\bar u_i). \]
    Definition 10.1 — Fixed-effects (within) estimator

    The within transformation expresses each variable as a deviation from its unit-specific time mean. OLS applied to the transformed data yields the fixed-effects (or within) estimator. Because \(\alpha_i\) is eliminated, the estimator is consistent even when \(\alpha_i\) is correlated with the regressors — that is, in the presence of arbitrary time-invariant confounding.

    10.3 Properties and limitations

    The fixed-effects estimator controls for every time-invariant characteristic of the unit simultaneously, including unmeasured ones. This is its principal advantage. Two limitations follow from the same mechanism.

    • The effect of any regressor that is constant within a unit cannot be estimated, since the within transformation removes it. The effects of fixed attributes — an individual's sex, a firm's country of origin — are not identified.
    • The estimator addresses only time-invariant confounding. A confounder that varies over time in concert with the regressor remains, and biases the estimate.
    Key Idea

    Fixed effects eliminate confounding from time-invariant unit characteristics, observed or not, by comparing each unit with itself. They do not eliminate confounding from time-varying determinants, and they cannot identify the effects of characteristics that are constant within units.

    10.4 Fixed-effects estimation in Python

    The within transformation can be performed explicitly by subtracting unit means, computed with groupby(...).transform("mean"), and applying OLS to the demeaned variables.

    import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

# within transformation by hand: subtract each unit's own time-average from every variable
for col in ["y", "x"]:
    df[col + "_dm"] = df[col] - df.groupby("firm")[col].transform("mean")

fe = smf.ols("y_dm ~ x_dm - 1", data=df).fit()    # "-1": no intercept after demeaning
print(fe.params["x_dm"])           # the fixed-effects (within) estimate

    In practice a dedicated panel routine is used. The linearmodels library provides PanelOLS, which performs the transformation, manages the degrees of freedom, and supports clustered standard errors (Chapter 11), once a unit–time index is set and EntityEffects is requested.

    from linearmodels.panel import PanelOLS

# set a (unit, time) index, then request entity effects
panel = df.set_index(["firm", "year"])
fe = PanelOLS.from_formula("y ~ x + EntityEffects", data=panel).fit(
        cov_type="clustered", cluster_entity=True)     # cluster by unit (Chapter 11)
print(fe.params["x"])

    Chapter 11 · Dependence and Panels

    Clustered Standard Errors and Random Effects

    The Question

    When observations within a group share unobserved influences, the assumption of independent observations fails. How is valid inference then obtained?

    The standard errors of Chapter 4 assume independent observations. In grouped and panel data this assumption is violated: observations on the same unit, or within the same cluster, share unobserved influences and are therefore correlated. Ignoring this dependence yields standard errors that are too small and inference that is overconfident. This chapter develops clustered standard errors, which restore valid inference, and the random-effects estimator as an alternative to fixed effects, with their implementation in Python.

    11.1 Within-group dependence

    Observations that share an environment — repeated observations on one unit, or distinct units within one cluster — have disturbances that are correlated, because they are influenced by common unobserved factors. The number of independent pieces of information is then smaller than the number of observations. The conventional standard-error formula, which counts each observation as independent, accordingly understates the sampling variability of the estimator.

    11.2 Clustered standard errors

    Definition 11.1 — Clustered standard errors

    Clustered standard errors estimate the variance of the OLS estimator allowing the disturbances to be arbitrarily correlated within each cluster, while treating distinct clusters as independent. The cluster is chosen to correspond to the level at which the disturbances are dependent — the unit, the industry, or the geographic area at which shocks are shared or treatment is assigned.

    The coefficients are unchanged; only the standard errors are recomputed. Clustering typically increases the standard errors, often substantially, by acknowledging the reduced quantity of independent information. The failure to cluster in grouped data is a common cause of spuriously significant findings.

    Key Idea

    Standard errors should be clustered at the level at which the disturbances are correlated — generally the level at which treatment is assigned or shocks are shared. Clustering at too fine a level continues to overstate precision; the correct cluster is determined by the structure of the dependence, not by convenience.

    11.3 Random effects

    The fixed-effects estimator of Chapter 10 uses only within-unit variation, discarding between-unit variation and the effects of time-invariant regressors. The random-effects estimator instead retains both sources of variation, under the additional assumption that the unit effect is uncorrelated with the regressors.

    Definition 11.2 — Random effects

    The random-effects model treats the unit effect \(\alpha_i\) as a random component of the disturbance, assumed uncorrelated with the regressors. Under this assumption the estimator is more efficient than fixed effects and can identify the effects of time-invariant regressors. If the assumption fails — if \(\alpha_i\) is correlated with the regressors, the situation of unobserved confounding — the random-effects estimator is inconsistent, and fixed effects is required.

    The choice between the two is informed by comparing their estimates: substantial divergence indicates correlation between the unit effect and the regressors, favouring fixed effects; close agreement permits the more efficient random-effects estimator. The formal version of this comparison is the Hausman test.

    11.4 Clustered and panel inference in Python

    Clustered standard errors are requested through the cov_type and cov_kwds arguments of fit, with the cluster variable supplied as groups.

    import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

# cluster-robust standard errors: errors may be correlated within each firm
result = smf.ols("y ~ x", data=df).fit(
            cov_type="cluster", cov_kwds={"groups": df["firm"]})
print(result.bse["x"])             # clustered standard error (usually much larger)

    The random-effects and fixed-effects estimators are provided by linearmodels; comparing their coefficients implements the Hausman comparison.

    from linearmodels.panel import RandomEffects, PanelOLS

re_model = RandomEffects.from_formula("y ~ 1 + x", data=panel).fit()
fe_model = PanelOLS.from_formula("y ~ 1 + x + EntityEffects", data=panel).fit()
# compare: large divergence favours fixed effects (the Hausman comparison)
print(re_model.params["x"], fe_model.params["x"])

    Chapter 12 · Quasi-Experiments

    Difference-in-Differences

    The Question

    A policy is introduced for one group and not another. How is its effect estimated, separating it from permanent differences between the groups and from trends common to both?

    Arena⚔ The DiD Time Machine →

    When a policy is applied to one group and withheld from another, comparison over time of the two groups can identify its effect. The difference-in-differences estimator removes both the permanent differences between groups and the trends common to them, isolating the change attributable to the policy. This chapter develops the estimator, its identifying assumption of parallel trends, the event-study assessment of that assumption, and the Python implementation. It is the most widely used quasi-experimental design in applied economics.

    12.1 The estimator

    Let a treated group be exposed to a policy between two periods and a control group not. Two naive comparisons each fail: the before–after change in the treated group confounds the policy with trends common to both groups, and the treated–control difference after the policy confounds it with permanent differences between the groups. The difference-in-differences estimator combines them:

    \[ \hat\tau = (\bar y_{\text{treated,after}}-\bar y_{\text{treated,before}}) - (\bar y_{\text{control,after}}-\bar y_{\text{control,before}}). \]

    The control group's change estimates the common trend; subtracting it removes the trend, while the use of each group's own change removes its permanent level. Equivalently, \(\hat\tau\) is the coefficient on the interaction of a treated-group indicator with an after-period indicator in the regression \( y = \beta_0 + \beta_1\,\text{treated} + \beta_2\,\text{post} + \tau\,(\text{treated}\times\text{post}) + u. \)

    12.2 The parallel-trends assumption

    Definition 12.1 — Parallel trends

    The identifying assumption is that, in the absence of the policy, the treated and control groups would have followed parallel trends — equal changes over time, though possibly at different levels. The control group's change then provides the counterfactual change for the treated group. The assumption concerns an unobserved counterfactual and cannot be verified directly.

    If the groups were already diverging before the policy, the control's change misrepresents the treated group's counterfactual, and the estimate is biased. The assumption's plausibility is assessed from the pre-policy period.

    12.3 The event study

    An event study estimates the gap between the groups in each period relative to the period immediately preceding the policy. Two features are diagnostic. In the pre-policy periods the estimated gaps should be approximately zero, indicating no prior divergence and supporting parallel trends; in the post-policy periods they should depart from zero, tracing the emergence and persistence of the effect. Approximately zero pre-policy gaps are the evidence that earns confidence in the design; pre-policy gaps that are already trending signal a violation of parallel trends.

    Key Idea

    The credibility of a difference-in-differences estimate rests on parallel trends, which cannot be proved but can be made plausible by demonstrating that the groups moved in parallel before the policy. The event study provides this demonstration and additionally reveals the time profile of the effect.

    12.4 Difference-in-differences in Python

    The estimator is the coefficient on the interaction of the treated and post indicators; standard errors are clustered at the level of treatment assignment (Chapter 11).

    import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

# the interaction treated:post is the difference-in-differences estimate
did = smf.ols("y ~ treated + post + treated:post", data=df).fit(
          cov_type="cluster", cov_kwds={"groups": df["state"]})
print(did.params["treated:post"])      # the estimated policy effect

    The event study interacts the treated indicator with each period, the pre-policy period serving as the base, and the resulting coefficients trace the gap over time.

    # event study: interact the treated indicator with each time period
# (the period before the policy is the omitted base)
event = smf.ols("y ~ treated + C(year) + treated:C(year)", data=df).fit(
            cov_type="cluster", cov_kwds={"groups": df["state"]})
# the treated:C(year) coefficients trace the gap period by period:
# flat (near zero) before the policy, opening up after

    Chapter 13 · Discontinuities

    Regression Discontinuity

    The Question

    When a treatment is assigned by a strict threshold on a continuous variable, can the units just above and just below the threshold be used to identify its effect?

    Arena⚔ The Regression-Discontinuity Cliff →

    When a treatment is allocated according to whether a continuous variable crosses a fixed threshold, the units immediately on either side of the threshold are nearly identical in all respects save the treatment. Comparing them approximates random assignment. This chapter develops the regression-discontinuity design: the threshold as a source of identification, the estimation of the discontinuity by local regression, the bandwidth trade-off, the assessment of manipulation, and the Python implementation. It is among the most credible quasi-experimental designs.

    13.1 Identification at the threshold

    Let treatment be assigned according to whether a running variable crosses a cutoff: units at or above the cutoff are treated, units below are not. Consider units arbitrarily close to the cutoff on either side. They differ negligibly in the running variable, and hence — assuming all other determinants of the outcome vary smoothly through the cutoff — in every other characteristic; yet one set is treated and the other is not. At the cutoff, treatment is therefore as good as randomly assigned.

    The effect appears as a discontinuity in the outcome at the cutoff. Plotting the outcome against the running variable, the relationship is continuous everywhere except for a jump at the cutoff; because all determinants other than treatment vary continuously, the jump is attributable to the treatment alone, and its magnitude is the effect.

    13.2 Estimation

    The discontinuity is estimated by fitting the outcome against the running variable separately on each side of the cutoff — local linear regression — and taking the difference of the two fitted values at the cutoff. Since only the discontinuity at the cutoff is of interest, the fit must be accurate near the cutoff, which raises the choice of the window width, the bandwidth.

    Key Idea — the bandwidth trade-off

    A wide bandwidth includes more observations, reducing variance, but observations far from the cutoff may follow a different relationship, introducing bias. A narrow bandwidth reduces bias by restricting attention to comparable units, but reduces the number of observations, increasing variance. The estimate should be reported across several bandwidths; stability as the bandwidth narrows supports its credibility.

    13.3 Manipulation of the running variable

    The design requires that units be unable to control their position relative to the cutoff. If they can — if applicants just below the cutoff secure reclassification above it — then those just above differ systematically from those just below in the characteristic that enabled the manipulation, and the comparability assumption fails. Manipulation is assessed by examining the density of the running variable at the cutoff: absent manipulation, the density is continuous; manipulation produces a discontinuity — an accumulation of units just above the cutoff and a deficit just below (the McCrary test).

    13.4 Regression discontinuity in Python

    The running variable is centred at the cutoff, an indicator for being above the cutoff is constructed, and a local linear regression with separate slopes on each side is fitted within a bandwidth. The coefficient on the indicator is the estimated discontinuity.

    import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

c = 90.0                                  # the cutoff of the running variable (score)
df["above"]   = (df["score"] >= c).astype(int)
df["score_c"] = df["score"] - c           # centre the running variable at the cutoff

# local linear regression within a bandwidth h, with separate slopes on each side
h = 10.0
window = df[df["score_c"].abs() <= h]
rd = smf.ols("y ~ above + score_c + above:score_c", data=window).fit(cov_type="HC1")
print(rd.params["above"])                 # the jump at the cutoff = the treatment effect

    The estimate should be examined across several bandwidths.

    # report the estimate across several bandwidths; a stable estimate is reassuring
for h in [5, 10, 20]:
    w = df[df["score_c"].abs() <= h]
    est = smf.ols("y ~ above + score_c + above:score_c", data=w).fit().params["above"]
    print(h, est)

    Chapter 14 · Binary Outcomes

    Models for a Binary Dependent Variable

    The Question

    When the outcome is a binary event — employed or not, default or not — how is the probability of the event modelled, and how are the coefficients interpreted?

    Arena⚔ The Probability Frontier →

    When the dependent variable is binary, it records the occurrence of an event, and its conditional mean is the probability of the event. Regression remains applicable, but the bounded nature of a probability requires care in modelling and interpretation. This chapter introduces the minimal probability concepts required, develops the linear probability model and the logit and probit models, treats the interpretation of their coefficients through marginal effects, and presents their estimation in Python.

    14.1 Binary outcomes and probability

    A probability is a number in \([0,1]\) measuring the likelihood of an event. When a binary outcome is coded 1 for occurrence and 0 otherwise, its mean is the proportion of occurrences, which is the probability of the event. Consequently, modelling the conditional mean of a binary outcome is modelling the conditional probability of the event — the central observation of the chapter.

    14.2 The linear probability model

    Definition 14.1 — Linear probability model

    The linear probability model applies OLS to a binary outcome: \(\Pr(y=1\mid x) = \beta_0+\beta_1 x_1+\cdots+\beta_k x_k\). Each coefficient is the change in the probability of the event associated with a one-unit change in the regressor.

    Its advantages are the direct interpretation of coefficients as probability changes and the familiarity of OLS. Its disadvantage is that the linear form may yield fitted probabilities below zero or above one for extreme regressor values. A binary outcome is moreover intrinsically heteroskedastic, so robust standard errors are required. The linear probability model is widely used when the fitted probabilities lie predominantly in the interior of \([0,1]\).

    14.3 Logit and probit

    To constrain fitted probabilities to \([0,1]\), the probability is modelled as a nonlinear function of the regressors that increases monotonically from 0 to 1 — an S-shaped curve. The logit and probit models employ, respectively, the logistic and standard-normal cumulative distribution functions for this purpose; they differ only in the curve and typically yield similar conclusions. Both guarantee fitted probabilities within \([0,1]\).

    The coefficients of these models are not themselves probability changes, because the curve's slope varies with the regressors: the effect of a one-unit change is large where the curve is steep (near probability one-half) and small where it is flat (near 0 or 1). The effect is therefore summarized by a marginal effect — the change in probability per unit change in the regressor — evaluated at a representative point or averaged over the sample.

    Key Idea

    For a binary outcome, the linear probability model offers directly interpretable coefficients but may produce fitted probabilities outside \([0,1]\); logit and probit guarantee valid probabilities but require marginal effects for interpretation. A common practice is to use the linear model for an interpretable summary and a logit or probit to confirm that conclusions do not depend on the boundary behaviour.

    14.4 Binary-outcome models in Python

    The linear probability model is OLS on the binary outcome, with robust standard errors.

    import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

# linear probability model: OLS on a 0/1 outcome; use robust SE (always heteroskedastic)
lpm = smf.ols("employed ~ educ + exper", data=df).fit(cov_type="HC1")
print(lpm.params["educ"])          # change in probability of employment per year of school

    Logit and probit are estimated by smf.logit and smf.probit; the coefficients are converted to marginal effects with get_margeff.

    # logit and probit keep predicted probabilities in [0, 1]
logit  = smf.logit("employed ~ educ + exper", data=df).fit()
probit = smf.probit("employed ~ educ + exper", data=df).fit()

# raw coefficients are on a transformed scale; convert to marginal effects
margins = logit.get_margeff(at="overall")   # average marginal effect on the probability
print(margins.summary())

    Chapter 15 · Time Series I

    Stationarity and Spurious Regression

    The Question

    Two unrelated series both trend over time, and a regression of one on the other reports a strong, significant relationship. Is the relationship real?

    Arena⚔ The Spurious-Regression Oracle →

    Time-series data violate the assumption of independent observations: successive values of an economic series are related, and many series wander without returning to a fixed level. This wandering produces a hazard absent from cross-sectional analysis — the spurious regression, in which unrelated series appear strongly related. This chapter develops the concepts of autocorrelation and stationarity, demonstrates the spurious-regression problem, and establishes the testing and differencing procedures that guard against it, with their Python implementation.

    15.1 Features of time-series data

    In a time series the ordering of observations is informative, because a value depends on its predecessors. Autocorrelation is the correlation of a series with its own past values; it implies that successive observations carry overlapping information, so that the effective number of independent observations is below the count (as with clustering in Chapter 11).

    A series that has no tendency to return to a fixed level — each value being the previous value plus a random increment — is a random walk, the leading example of a non-stationary series.

    Definition 15.1 — Stationarity

    A series is (weakly) stationary if its mean and variance are constant over time and the correlation between observations depends only on their separation in time, not on their position. A non-stationary series violates these conditions; a random walk, whose variance grows without bound, is non-stationary.

    15.2 Spurious regression

    Proposition 15.1 — Spurious regression

    The regression of one independent random walk on another typically yields a large coefficient, a large \(t\)-statistic, and a high \(R^2\), falsely indicating a relationship where none exists. This is not an exceptional outcome but the usual result of regressing one non-stationary series on another.

    The mechanism is that both series wander, and over any finite sample their wanderings exhibit apparent co-movement that the regression registers as a relationship. The standard errors of Chapter 4, derived under independence, are grossly understated for wandering series, producing extreme overconfidence. The high \(R^2\) is genuine arithmetic; the relationship it appears to certify is not.

    Key Idea

    For time-series data, a high \(R^2\) and a large \(t\)-statistic are not evidence of a genuine relationship; they are the expected symptoms of regressing one non-stationary series on another. The stationarity of the series must be assessed before any time-series regression is interpreted.

    15.3 Testing and differencing

    Stationarity is assessed by the augmented Dickey–Fuller test, whose null hypothesis is that the series contains a unit root (is non-stationary); failure to reject indicates non-stationarity. A non-stationary series is rendered stationary by differencing — replacing the series by its period-to-period change. The change in a random walk is the random increment, which is stationary. A spurious relationship in levels disappears in differences, confirming its spurious nature. The procedure is therefore to test each series, difference where necessary, and conduct the regression in the transformed (stationary) variables.

    15.4 Stationarity and differencing in Python

    The augmented Dickey–Fuller test is provided by statsmodels.tsa.stattools.adfuller; its second return value is the p-value.

    from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import adfuller

# augmented Dickey-Fuller test: H0 is a unit root (non-stationary, wandering series)
stat, pvalue = adfuller(df["gdp"])[:2]
print(pvalue)        # large p-value: fail to reject -> treat the series as non-stationary

    Differencing is performed with the diff method; a time-series regression in differences typically uses autocorrelation-robust standard errors (Chapter 16).

    import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

# differencing converts a non-stationary series to its (stationary) period-to-period change
df["d_gdp"] = df["gdp"].diff()
df["d_x"]   = df["x"].diff()

# regress in differences rather than levels; a spurious level relationship vanishes here
reg = smf.ols("d_gdp ~ d_x", data=df.dropna()).fit(cov_type="HAC", cov_kwds={"maxlags": 4})
print(reg.params["d_x"])

    Chapter 16 · Time Series II

    Cointegration and Forecasting

    The Question

    Chapter 15 prescribed differencing for non-stationary series. But if two series are bound together in the long run, does differencing discard the relationship? And how is a model's predictive performance properly assessed?

    Chapter 15 established that non-stationary series should be differenced before regression. This prescription has an important exception: when two non-stationary series are tied together in the long run, a relationship that differencing would destroy. This chapter develops cointegration, the formal expression of such a long-run relationship, together with the assessment of forecasting models by out-of-sample evaluation and the correction of standard errors for autocorrelation, with their Python implementation.

    16.1 Cointegration

    Two non-stationary series may move together so that a particular combination of them is stationary: each wanders, but the distance between them is stable, restored whenever it widens. Such series are cointegrated.

    Definition 16.1 — Cointegration

    Two non-stationary series are cointegrated if a linear combination of them is stationary. The combination represents a stable long-run relationship: although each series wanders, deviations from the relationship do not accumulate but are corrected over time.

    For cointegrated series, regression in levels is appropriate — indeed necessary, since differencing removes the long-run relationship. The Engle–Granger test assesses cointegration by regressing one series on the other and testing whether the residual (the deviation from the relationship) is stationary; a stationary residual indicates cointegration.

    Key Idea

    Differencing is correct for series that merely happen to wander concurrently, but destroys the relationship between cointegrated series, which should be analyzed in levels. The two cases are distinguished by testing whether the series are individually non-stationary yet possess a stationary linear combination.

    16.2 Out-of-sample evaluation

    A model's fit to the data on which it was estimated — its \(R^2\) — is an unreliable measure of predictive performance, because the model was fitted to those observations. The appropriate measure is performance on data not used in estimation.

    Definition 16.2 — Out-of-sample evaluation

    The data are partitioned into an earlier training period and a later test period. The model is estimated on the training period and its predictions compared with the realized outcomes in the test period. A model that fits the training period well but predicts the test period poorly has fitted noise rather than structure.

    Out-of-sample evaluation is the principal safeguard against overfitting and the foundation of the predictive methods of Chapter 17.

    16.3 Autocorrelation-robust standard errors

    In a stationary time-series regression the disturbances are typically autocorrelated, so the conventional standard errors are understated (as with clustering in Chapter 11). The Newey–West standard errors correct for autocorrelation and heteroskedasticity, leaving the coefficients unchanged and adjusting the standard errors to reflect the true precision. They are appropriate for any regression on time-ordered data.

    16.4 Cointegration and forecasting in Python

    The Engle–Granger cointegration test is provided by statsmodels.tsa.stattools.coint.

    from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import coint

# Engle-Granger test: H0 is NO cointegration (no stable long-run relationship)
stat, pvalue, _ = coint(df["y"], df["x"])
print(pvalue)        # small p-value: reject -> the two non-stationary series are cointegrated

    Out-of-sample evaluation partitions the sample by time, estimates on the training period, and measures error on the test period; autocorrelation-robust (Newey–West) standard errors are requested with cov_type="HAC".

    import statsmodels.formula.api as smf
import numpy as np

# evaluate a model out of sample: fit on the early period, predict the later period
train = df.iloc[:split]
test  = df.iloc[split:]

model = smf.ols("y ~ x1 + x2", data=train).fit(cov_type="HAC", cov_kwds={"maxlags": 4})
pred  = model.predict(test)
rmse  = np.sqrt(np.mean((test["y"] - pred)**2))     # out-of-sample error, the honest test
print(rmse)

    Chapter 17 · Prediction and Causal Inference

    Penalized Regression and Double Machine Learning

    The Question

    Algorithms that predict outcomes from many inputs have become extraordinarily accurate. Two questions follow. First, how are such methods constructed, and what do they optimize? Second — and this is the deeper question for an econometrician — can the predictive power of these methods be harnessed to estimate a causal effect, or does prediction belong to a different enterprise altogether?

    Arena⚔ The ML Foundry →

    This chapter develops two bodies of method that have reshaped applied econometrics. The first is penalized regression — the ridge and lasso estimators — which control the variance of an estimator by deliberately introducing bias, and which form the predictive core of modern statistical learning. The second is double (or debiased) machine learning, a framework that uses flexible predictive methods to estimate causal effects in the presence of many control variables. The treatment is self-contained: each estimator is defined formally through the optimization problem it solves, its statistical properties are derived or stated precisely, and its implementation in Python is presented in full. The chapter is longer and more demanding than those preceding it, as befits material that sits at the current research frontier yet rests entirely on the foundations the earlier chapters established.

    17.1 Two estimation problems

    It is essential at the outset to distinguish two objectives that are frequently conflated.

    The first is prediction. Given a vector of predictors \(x\), the goal is to construct a function \(\hat f(x)\) whose value is close, on average, to the realized outcome \(y\) for observations not used in estimation. Predictive quality is judged solely by out-of-sample accuracy; the interpretation of the individual coefficients of \(\hat f\) is immaterial.

    The second is causal inference. Here a particular covariate, the treatment \(d\), is singled out, and the goal is to estimate the effect on \(y\) of an exogenous change in \(d\), holding fixed the remaining covariates. As established in Chapters 1, 3, and 9, this requires an identifying assumption about the relationship between \(d\) and the unobserved determinants of \(y\); predictive accuracy is neither necessary nor sufficient for it.

    Key Idea

    Predictive accuracy and causal validity are distinct properties of an estimator. A model may predict \(y\) with high accuracy while yielding a severely biased estimate of the effect of any particular covariate, and conversely. The methods of statistical learning are, in the first instance, prediction methods. Their use in causal inference requires that they be embedded within an identification strategy, a point developed formally in Sections 17.6–17.9.

    17.2 The bias–variance decomposition

    Consider estimating a regression function \(f(x)=\mathrm{E}[y\mid x]\) from a sample, obtaining an estimate \(\hat f\). Fix a target point \(x_0\) and let the data at that point be generated by \(y_0 = f(x_0) + \varepsilon\), where \(\varepsilon\) has mean zero and variance \(\sigma^2\) and is independent of the training sample. The expected squared prediction error at \(x_0\), taken over both the training sample and the new disturbance \(\varepsilon\), admits the decomposition

    \[ \mathrm{E}\big[(y_0 - \hat f(x_0))^2\big] \;=\; \underbrace{\sigma^2}_{\text{irreducible}} \;+\; \underbrace{\big(f(x_0) - \mathrm{E}[\hat f(x_0)]\big)^2}_{\text{bias}^2} \;+\; \underbrace{\mathrm{Var}\big(\hat f(x_0)\big)}_{\text{variance}}. \]

    The three terms are distinct in origin. The irreducible error \(\sigma^2\) reflects noise in the outcome itself and cannot be reduced by any estimator. The squared bias measures how far the estimator is centred from the truth; it is large when the model is too rigid to represent \(f\). The variance measures the sensitivity of the estimate to the particular training sample; it is large when the model is so flexible that it adapts to sample-specific noise.

    The decomposition makes precise a tension that governs every modelling choice. Increasing model flexibility — adding regressors, raising polynomial degree, deepening a tree — reduces bias but raises variance. Restricting flexibility does the reverse. Because the two terms enter the prediction error additively and with opposite responses to flexibility, there exists an intermediate degree of flexibility that minimizes their sum. The estimators of this chapter are devices for locating that minimum in a disciplined way.

    Definition 17.1 — Overfitting

    An estimator overfits when its flexibility is great enough that variance dominates the prediction error: the in-sample fit is excellent because the estimator has adapted to the noise of the training sample, but out-of-sample accuracy is poor because that noise does not recur. Overfitting is diagnosed by a large gap between in-sample and out-of-sample error.

    17.3 Cross-validation

    Because bias and variance cannot be observed directly, the degree of flexibility must be selected by estimating out-of-sample prediction error. The standard procedure is \(K\)-fold cross-validation.

    Definition 17.2 — \(K\)-fold cross-validation

    Partition the \(n\) observations into \(K\) disjoint subsets (folds) of approximately equal size, \(I_1,\dots,I_K\). For each \(k\), estimate the model on the observations not in fold \(k\) and compute its mean squared prediction error on the observations in fold \(k\). The cross-validation estimate of prediction error is the average of these \(K\) fold-specific errors,

    \[ \mathrm{CV} \;=\; \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K} \frac{1}{|I_k|}\sum_{i\in I_k}\big(y_i - \hat f^{(-k)}(x_i)\big)^2, \]

    where \(\hat f^{(-k)}\) denotes the estimator fitted with fold \(k\) removed. To select a tuning parameter, \(\mathrm{CV}\) is computed over a grid of candidate values and the value minimizing it is chosen.

    The validity of the procedure rests on a single principle: each prediction is evaluated on data that played no part in producing it, so the cross-validation error is an approximately unbiased estimate of genuine out-of-sample error. The choice \(K=5\) or \(K=10\) is conventional, trading computational cost against the variance of the estimate.

    17.4 Penalized regression: ridge and lasso

    Ordinary least squares chooses coefficients to minimize the residual sum of squares alone. When the number of predictors \(p\) is large relative to the sample size \(n\), this objective is minimized by coefficients that fit the training noise, producing high variance; and when \(p\) exceeds \(n\) the minimizer is not even unique. Penalized regression addresses both problems by adding to the least-squares objective a penalty that increases with the magnitude of the coefficients, thereby constraining their size.

    Throughout this section the predictors are assumed standardized — each rescaled to have mean zero and unit variance — so that a single penalty applies comparably across predictors measured in different units. The intercept is not penalized.

    17.4.1 The ridge estimator

    Definition 17.3 — Ridge regression

    For a penalty parameter \(\lambda \ge 0\), the ridge estimator solves

    \[ \hat\beta^{\,\text{ridge}}(\lambda) \;=\; \arg\min_{\beta}\;\sum_{i=1}^{n}\Big(y_i - \beta_0 - \sum_{j=1}^{p}x_{ij}\beta_j\Big)^{2} \;+\; \lambda\sum_{j=1}^{p}\beta_j^{2}. \]

    The first term is the familiar residual sum of squares; the second, the \(\ell_2\) penalty, is \(\lambda\) times the sum of squared coefficients.

    The parameter \(\lambda\) governs the strength of the penalty. At \(\lambda=0\) the estimator reduces to ordinary least squares. As \(\lambda\to\infty\) every coefficient is driven toward zero. Intermediate values shrink the coefficients toward zero by an amount that the data, through the residual-sum-of-squares term, can resist in proportion to the evidence for each coefficient.

    Remark 17.1 — closed form and the \(p>n\) case

    Collecting the standardized predictors into the \(n\times p\) matrix \(X\) and the outcomes into the vector \(y\), the ridge objective is \(\lVert y - X\beta\rVert^2 + \lambda\lVert\beta\rVert^2\), whose unique minimizer is

    \[ \hat\beta^{\,\text{ridge}}(\lambda) \;=\; \big(X^{\top}X + \lambda I\big)^{-1}X^{\top}y. \]

    (Matrix notation here merely abbreviates the sums above.) The term \(\lambda I\) adds \(\lambda\) to each diagonal entry of \(X^{\top}X\), which guarantees the matrix is invertible for any \(\lambda>0\) — even when \(p>n\), where \(X^{\top}X\) alone is singular and ordinary least squares is undefined. This is the precise sense in which the penalty stabilizes estimation in high dimensions.

    17.4.2 The lasso estimator

    Definition 17.4 — Lasso regression

    For a penalty parameter \(\lambda \ge 0\), the lasso estimator solves

    \[ \hat\beta^{\,\text{lasso}}(\lambda) \;=\; \arg\min_{\beta}\;\sum_{i=1}^{n}\Big(y_i - \beta_0 - \sum_{j=1}^{p}x_{ij}\beta_j\Big)^{2} \;+\; \lambda\sum_{j=1}^{p}\lvert\beta_j\rvert. \]

    The penalty — the \(\ell_1\) penalty — is \(\lambda\) times the sum of the absolute values of the coefficients.

    The ridge and lasso objectives differ only in whether the coefficients are penalized through their squares or their absolute values. This apparently minor difference has a decisive consequence: the lasso sets some coefficients exactly to zero, and thereby performs variable selection, whereas ridge shrinks all coefficients toward zero but sets none of them exactly to zero.

    The reason is made exact in the special case of orthonormal standardized predictors, where each penalized estimator has a closed form in terms of the ordinary least squares coefficient \(\hat\beta_j^{\,\text{ols}}\):

    \[ \hat\beta_j^{\,\text{ridge}} = \frac{\hat\beta_j^{\,\text{ols}}}{1+\lambda}, \qquad \hat\beta_j^{\,\text{lasso}} = \operatorname{sign}\!\big(\hat\beta_j^{\,\text{ols}}\big)\,\max\!\Big(\big|\hat\beta_j^{\,\text{ols}}\big| - \tfrac{\lambda}{2},\,0\Big). \]

    The ridge solution multiplies every coefficient by the common factor \(1/(1+\lambda)\): a proportional shrinkage that never reaches zero for a finite \(\lambda\). The lasso solution subtracts the constant \(\lambda/2\) from the magnitude of each coefficient and truncates at zero — the operation known as soft-thresholding. Any coefficient whose least-squares magnitude is at most \(\lambda/2\) is set exactly to zero; the remainder are pulled toward zero by the fixed amount \(\lambda/2\). Variable selection is thus a direct consequence of the constant, non-vanishing derivative of the absolute-value penalty, in contrast to the derivative \(2\beta_j\) of the squared penalty, which vanishes as \(\beta_j\to 0\) and so cannot force a coefficient to the boundary.

    Remark 17.2 — the geometric view

    Penalized estimation may equivalently be written as minimizing the residual sum of squares subject to a constraint on the penalty: \(\sum_j\beta_j^2 \le t\) for ridge, \(\sum_j|\beta_j| \le t\) for lasso. The ridge constraint region is a sphere; the lasso constraint region is a diamond (a cross-polytope) whose vertices lie on the coordinate axes. The least-squares contours generically first touch the diamond at a vertex — a point at which some coordinates are exactly zero — whereas they touch the smooth sphere at a generic point with all coordinates nonzero. This is the geometric counterpart of the soft-thresholding formula.

    17.4.3 Selecting the penalty

    The penalty parameter \(\lambda\) controls the position of each estimator on the bias–variance spectrum: small \(\lambda\) yields low bias and high variance (approaching ordinary least squares), large \(\lambda\) yields high bias and low variance. It is therefore selected to minimize estimated out-of-sample error by cross-validation (Definition 17.2), evaluated over a grid of candidate values. Because every penalized coefficient is shrunk toward zero, both estimators are biased toward zero by construction; this bias is the price paid for the reduction in variance, and it is acceptable when the objective is prediction. Section 17.6 shows why it is not acceptable when the objective is the estimation of a causal effect.

    17.6 Why penalized regression does not estimate causal effects

    Consider the apparently natural strategy of estimating the effect of a treatment \(d\) by applying the lasso to a regression of \(y\) on \(d\) together with a large collection of controls \(X\), and reading off the coefficient on \(d\). This strategy is invalid, for two distinct reasons.

    First, the lasso shrinks every penalized coefficient toward zero, including the coefficient on \(d\). The resulting estimate of the treatment effect is attenuated by an amount that depends on \(\lambda\) and is generally unknown. A penalized coefficient is not a consistent estimator of a structural parameter.

    Second, and more seriously, the lasso selects controls according to their value in predicting the outcome. A variable may be a weak predictor of \(y\) yet a strong predictor of the treatment \(d\); such a variable is, by the omitted-variable analysis of Chapter 3, an important confounder, since its omission biases the treatment coefficient in proportion to its association with \(d\). The lasso, judging relevance by outcome prediction alone, may set its coefficient to zero, reintroducing precisely the omitted-variable bias the controls were meant to remove. Selection of controls by outcome prediction is therefore systematically misaligned with the requirements of causal inference.

    Key Idea

    A coefficient produced by a regularized estimator is not a valid estimate of a causal effect: it is attenuated by the penalty, and the variable-selection step discards confounders that predict the treatment but not the outcome. The flexibility of statistical learning is nonetheless valuable for causal inference, provided it is directed at the prediction subproblems and kept away from the target parameter itself. The construction that achieves this is developed next.

    17.7 The partially linear model and orthogonalization

    The framework that reconciles flexible prediction with causal estimation is the partially linear model:

    \[ y \;=\; \theta\, d \;+\; g(X) \;+\; u, \qquad \mathrm{E}[u\mid d,X]=0, \] \[ d \;=\; m(X) \;+\; v, \qquad \mathrm{E}[v\mid X]=0. \]

    Here \(\theta\) is the scalar causal parameter of interest — the effect of the treatment, assumed constant across units — while \(g(X)=\mathrm{E}[y\mid X, d=0]\) and \(m(X)=\mathrm{E}[d\mid X]\) are unknown functions of the controls, permitted to be nonlinear and high-dimensional. These two functions are termed nuisance functions: they are not of direct interest, but must be estimated en route to \(\theta\).

    The route is the partialling-out identity of Chapter 3 (the Frisch–Waugh–Lovell theorem), now applied with general nuisance functions. Define the two residuals

    \[ \tilde y \;=\; y - \mathrm{E}[y\mid X], \qquad \tilde d \;=\; d - m(X) \;=\; v. \]

    Substituting the model for \(y\) and using \(\mathrm{E}[y\mid X] = \theta\, m(X) + g(X)\) gives \(\tilde y = \theta\,\tilde d + u\). Because \(\tilde d = v\) is, by construction, orthogonal to the controls and to \(u\), the parameter \(\theta\) is identified as the coefficient of a simple regression of \(\tilde y\) on \(\tilde d\):

    \[ \theta \;=\; \frac{\mathrm{E}[\tilde y\,\tilde d]}{\mathrm{E}[\tilde d^{\,2}]}. \]

    This is the population analogue of regressing the outcome residual on the treatment residual. The construction residualizes both variables with respect to the controls; the role of the second residualization — of the treatment — is established in the following section.

    17.8 Double machine learning

    The identification result of Section 17.7 requires the nuisance functions \(\mathrm{E}[y\mid X]\) and \(m(X)\), which are unknown. Double machine learning estimates them with flexible predictive methods — penalized regression, random forests, gradient boosting, or others — and forms the residuals from the fitted values.

    Definition 17.5 — The double machine learning estimator

    Let \(\hat\ell(X)\) and \(\hat m(X)\) be estimates of \(\mathrm{E}[y\mid X]\) and \(\mathrm{E}[d\mid X]\). Form residuals \(\hat{\tilde y}_i = y_i - \hat\ell(X_i)\) and \(\hat{\tilde d}_i = d_i - \hat m(X_i)\). The estimator of \(\theta\) is

    \[ \hat\theta \;=\; \frac{\sum_i \hat{\tilde d}_i\,\hat{\tilde y}_i}{\sum_i \hat{\tilde d}_i^{\,2}}, \]

    the slope of the regression of the outcome residual on the treatment residual.

    The decisive property of this estimator is its insensitivity to errors in the estimated nuisance functions. Suppose \(\hat\ell\) and \(\hat m\) deviate from the true \(\ell\) and \(m\) by small amounts. One may show that the resulting error in \(\hat\theta\) depends, to first order, only on the product of the two nuisance errors, not on either separately. Because each error is small, their product is of second order and hence negligible. An estimating equation with this property — that the first-order effect of nuisance error vanishes — is said to satisfy a Neyman orthogonality condition.

    The orthogonality is precisely what the double residualization secures, and it is why the naive single-residualization strategy fails. Had only the outcome been residualized, the error in \(\hat\theta\) would depend on the nuisance error to first order, so that the slow convergence of a flexible, regularized nuisance estimator would transmit directly into a first-order bias in \(\theta\) — the regularization bias of Section 17.6. Residualizing the treatment as well removes that first-order channel, leaving only the harmless second-order product. Under the orthogonality condition, and with the cross-fitting of Section 17.9, \(\hat\theta\) is consistent and asymptotically normal at the parametric rate \(\sqrt{n}\), so that conventional standard errors and confidence intervals are valid even though the nuisance functions are estimated by methods that converge more slowly.

    17.9 Cross-fitting

    One further safeguard is required. If a nuisance function is estimated on the same observations to which it is then applied, a flexible estimator will have partially fitted the noise of those observations, so the residuals are correlated with that noise. This correlation induces a bias in \(\hat\theta\) that does not vanish even asymptotically. The remedy is cross-fitting, an application of sample splitting.

    Definition 17.6 — Cross-fitting

    Partition the sample into \(K\) folds. For each fold \(k\), estimate the nuisance functions on the observations outside fold \(k\), and use those fitted functions to compute the residuals \(\hat{\tilde y}_i,\hat{\tilde d}_i\) for the observations inside fold \(k\). Every observation thus receives residuals computed from nuisance estimates that did not use it. The estimator \(\hat\theta\) is then formed from the assembled residuals as in Definition 17.5.

    Cross-fitting is the same out-of-sample principle as cross-validation (Definition 17.2), applied here not to select a tuning parameter but to ensure that each residual is statistically independent of the nuisance estimate that produced it. Together, Neyman orthogonality and cross-fitting permit the use of arbitrary high-quality predictive methods for the nuisance functions while retaining valid inference on \(\theta\).

    17.10 Estimating a causal effect by double machine learning in Python

    The estimator is assembled from components already encountered: a flexible learner for the two nuisance predictions, a manual cross-fitting loop, and a final residual-on-residual regression with heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors. The learner below is an ensemble of randomized trees (ExtraTreesRegressor); any flexible regressor may be substituted.

    import numpy as np
from sklearn.ensemble import ExtraTreesRegressor
import statsmodels.api as sm

def fit_learner(seed):
    return ExtraTreesRegressor(n_estimators=80, min_samples_leaf=3, random_state=seed)

K = 5
folds = np.array_split(np.random.default_rng(0).permutation(len(y)), K)
y_res = np.zeros(len(y))
d_res = np.zeros(len(y))

for k in range(K):
    test  = folds[k]
    train = np.concatenate([folds[j] for j in range(K) if j != k])
    # predict each test fold from a learner trained on the OTHER folds (cross-fitting)
    y_res[test] = y[test] - fit_learner(0).fit(X[train], y[train]).predict(X[test])
    d_res[test] = d[test] - fit_learner(1).fit(X[train], d[train]).predict(X[test])

# the causal effect is the slope of the residual-on-residual regression
final = sm.OLS(y_res, d_res).fit(cov_type="HC1")
theta = final.params[0]
se    = final.bse[0]

    The loop iterates over folds, fits each nuisance function on the complementary folds, and predicts the held-out fold, so that every residual is computed out of sample. The final regression of y_res on d_res returns the estimate theta and its standard error se.     17.11 Scope and limitations

    The validity of the double machine learning estimator rests on the assumption embedded in the partially linear model of Section 17.7: that conditional on the controls \(X\), the treatment residual \(v\) is uncorrelated with the outcome disturbance \(u\). This is the unconfoundedness (or selection-on-observables) assumption, and it requires that \(X\) contain all common causes of \(d\) and \(y\). No estimation method can establish this assumption from data; it is an identifying restriction justified by subject-matter knowledge.

    The contribution of double machine learning is therefore precisely delimited. It relaxes the functional-form requirement of conventional control-variable regression, permitting the confounders to enter through unknown, possibly nonlinear, high-dimensional functions while preserving valid inference on the treatment effect. It does not relax the identifying requirement that the confounders be observed. When an important confounder is unobserved, the estimator is biased, and identification must instead be sought through the instrumental-variables strategy of Chapter 9 or the quasi-experimental designs of Chapters 12 and 13 — within which, it should be noted, machine learning may still be employed for the associated prediction subproblems.

    Key Idea

    Double machine learning addresses the problem of flexible control for observed confounders; it does not address the problem of identification in the presence of unobserved confounders. The distinction between these two problems is the organizing theme of the entire book, and it is not dissolved by the introduction of more powerful predictive methods.

    17.12 Heterogeneous effects

    The partially linear model restricts the treatment effect \(\theta\) to be constant across units. When the effect is believed to vary with observed characteristics — that is, when one seeks the conditional effect \(\theta(x)\) — the same orthogonalization-and-cross-fitting template extends to estimate it: the final residual-on-residual regression is replaced by a flexible model relating the outcome residual to the treatment residual as a function of covariates. The causal forest is the leading instance of this extension. The methods lie beyond the scope of this chapter, but the underlying principle — orthogonalize to protect the causal parameter, cross-fit to keep predictions honest — is the one developed above, and it is the durable lesson of this material.

    ★ Sharma's Take

    The intended use of an estimate should determine the method chosen to produce it, and the two purposes treated in this chapter call for opposite priorities. When the object is to rank or flag units — to identify which applicants are likely to default or which patients require monitoring — the relevant criterion is out-of-sample predictive accuracy, and the interpretability of individual coefficients is irrelevant; the flexible, regularized methods of Sections 17.2–17.5 are appropriate, and they should be evaluated by cross-validation alone. When the object is to intervene — to determine the consequence of altering a policy, a price, or a rule — predictive accuracy is no longer the operative criterion, and a coefficient extracted from an accurate predictive model is, as Section 17.6 demonstrates, generally a biased estimate of the causal quantity required. The recurring and costly error in applied work is the use of a predictive model's coefficients to guide intervention. Double machine learning is significant precisely because it supplies a principled means of retaining the flexibility of predictive methods while restoring the discipline of causal estimation; it should be understood not as a prediction method but as an estimator of a causal parameter that happens to use prediction methods as components.

    1. conceptual State the bias–variance decomposition of expected prediction error and explain, in terms of its three components, why a model that achieves zero training error is generally undesirable.
      Solution
      The decomposition is \(\mathrm{E}[(y_0-\hat f(x_0))^2]=\sigma^2+\text{bias}^2+\text{variance}\). A model with zero training error has adapted to the noise of the training sample; it therefore has very high variance, since a different sample would yield a very different fitted function. Although its bias may be low, the large variance term makes its expected out-of-sample error high. Zero training error is a symptom of overfitting (Definition 17.1).
    2. analytical For a single standardized orthonormal predictor with ordinary least squares coefficient \(\hat\beta^{\,\text{ols}}\), derive the ridge solution \(\hat\beta^{\,\text{ridge}}=\hat\beta^{\,\text{ols}}/(1+\lambda)\) by minimizing \((\hat\beta^{\,\text{ols}}-\beta)^2+\lambda\beta^2\) over \(\beta\).
      Solution
      Differentiate the objective with respect to \(\beta\): \(-2(\hat\beta^{\,\text{ols}}-\beta)+2\lambda\beta=0\). Hence \(\hat\beta^{\,\text{ols}}=\beta(1+\lambda)\), giving \(\beta=\hat\beta^{\,\text{ols}}/(1+\lambda)\). The second derivative \(2(1+\lambda)>0\) confirms a minimum. The shrinkage factor \(1/(1+\lambda)\) is strictly between 0 and 1 for \(\lambda>0\) and never equals zero.
    3. analytical For the same orthonormal case, the lasso minimizes \((\hat\beta^{\,\text{ols}}-\beta)^2+\lambda|\beta|\). Show that the solution is the soft-thresholding rule \(\hat\beta^{\,\text{lasso}}=\operatorname{sign}(\hat\beta^{\,\text{ols}})\max(|\hat\beta^{\,\text{ols}}|-\lambda/2,\,0)\), and explain why this can equal exactly zero whereas the ridge solution cannot.
      Solution
      For \(\beta>0\) the objective is differentiable with derivative \(-2(\hat\beta^{\,\text{ols}}-\beta)+\lambda\); setting it to zero gives \(\beta=\hat\beta^{\,\text{ols}}-\lambda/2\), valid when this is positive. Symmetrically for \(\beta<0\), \(\beta=\hat\beta^{\,\text{ols}}+\lambda/2\) when negative. If \(|\hat\beta^{\,\text{ols}}|\le\lambda/2\) neither one-sided solution is feasible and the minimum is at the kink \(\beta=0\). Combining the cases yields the stated rule. The mechanism is the non-vanishing derivative \(\pm\lambda\) of the penalty at the kink, which the curvature of the data term cannot overcome when the least-squares coefficient is small; the squared penalty has derivative \(2\lambda\beta\to 0\) at the origin and so exerts no such force.
    4. conceptual Explain why standardizing the predictors is necessary before applying ridge or lasso, but not before ordinary least squares.
      Solution
      Ordinary least squares is invariant to the scaling of predictors: rescaling a predictor merely rescales its coefficient, leaving fitted values and inference unchanged. The penalties \(\sum\beta_j^2\) and \(\sum|\beta_j|\), however, depend on the magnitudes of the coefficients, which depend on the units of the predictors. Without standardization, a predictor measured in small units (hence with a large coefficient) would be penalized more heavily than an identical predictor measured in large units. Standardization places all predictors on a common scale so that the penalty is applied comparably.
    5. conceptual A researcher applies the lasso to a regression of an outcome on a treatment and one hundred controls, and reports the lasso coefficient on the treatment as the causal effect. Identify the two distinct errors in this procedure.
      Solution
      First, the lasso penalty shrinks the treatment coefficient toward zero, so the reported value is attenuated by an unknown amount and is not a consistent estimate of the effect. Second, the lasso retains controls according to their usefulness in predicting the outcome; a confounder strongly associated with the treatment but weakly associated with the outcome may be dropped, reintroducing omitted-variable bias. Both errors are discussed in Section 17.6; the remedy is the orthogonalized, cross-fitted estimator of Sections 17.7–17.9.
    6. conceptual Define Neyman orthogonality and explain why it is the property that distinguishes double machine learning from the naive procedure of residualizing only the outcome.
      Solution
      An estimating equation for \(\theta\) is Neyman orthogonal if the first-order effect of small errors in the estimated nuisance functions on the estimate of \(\theta\) is zero. Under orthogonality, nuisance error enters only through the product of the two nuisance errors, which is second order and negligible, so flexible nuisance estimators that converge slowly do not transmit first-order bias to \(\theta\). Residualizing only the outcome does not satisfy orthogonality: the treatment-side nuisance error enters to first order, so regularization bias in the outcome model passes directly into \(\theta\). Residualizing the treatment as well restores orthogonality (Section 17.8).
    7. conceptual Explain the purpose of cross-fitting and the consequence of omitting it.
      Solution
      Cross-fitting computes each observation's residuals from nuisance functions estimated on other observations, so that the residuals are independent of the nuisance estimates applied to them. Without it, a flexible learner partially fits the noise of the observations it is later applied to, making the residuals correlated with that noise and inducing a bias in \(\hat\theta\) that does not vanish asymptotically (Section 17.9).

    9. python Modify the double machine learning cell of Section 17.10 to replace the randomized-tree learner with LassoCV for both nuisance functions. Report the resulting estimate and confidence interval, and verify that the interval contains the true effect.
      Solution
      Replace fit_learner(seed) with a pipeline that standardizes and then applies LassoCV; the cross-fitting loop and final regression are unchanged.     10. python analytical In the double machine learning cell, estimate the effect without cross-fitting by fitting each nuisance function on the full sample and predicting the same sample, and compare with the cross-fitted estimate. Explain any difference using the argument of Section 17.9.
      Solution
      Fit fit_learner once on all of X for each nuisance function and form residuals on the same data; compute \(\hat\theta\) from those residuals. The non-cross-fitted estimate is biased toward the in-sample fit because the learner has partially absorbed the noise of each observation it predicts, shrinking the residuals; the magnitude of the discrepancy grows with the flexibility of the learner. Cross-fitting eliminates this source of bias by ensuring each prediction is out of sample.
