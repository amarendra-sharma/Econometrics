    The Econometric Question

    The Question

    Does additional schooling cause higher earnings, or do individuals who obtain more schooling earn more for reasons that schooling did not produce?

    Arena⚔ The Causal Courtroom →

    Econometrics is the use of data to answer quantitative questions about the economic world. This chapter introduces no estimation formulae. Its purpose is to establish a single principle on which the remainder of the book depends: data can establish that two variables are associated, but cannot, without further assumptions, establish that one causes the other. The chapter defines the basic objects of the subject — variables, the distinction between a population and a sample, association, and the causal comparison — and introduces the data-handling tools in Python on which later computation rests.

    1.1 Data, variables, and averages

    Economic data are typically organized as a table in which each row records one unit of observation — an individual, a firm, a country — and each column records one variable, a measured characteristic that takes different values across units. The following table records two variables, years of schooling and hourly earnings, for five individuals.

    individualyears of schoolinghourly earnings ($)
    A1014
    B1215
    C1218
    D1624
    E1630

    The most elementary summary of a variable is its average, or mean: the sum of its values divided by their number. The mean of hourly earnings is

    \[ \bar y \;=\; \frac{14+15+18+24+30}{5} \;=\; 20.2 \text{ dollars per hour.} \]

    The mean is the foundation of the subject; the estimators developed in later chapters are, in a precise sense, conditional means computed under various assumptions.

    1.2 Association

    To describe the relationship between two variables, compute the mean of one within groups defined by the other. Among the two individuals with twelve years of schooling, mean earnings are \((15+18)/2 = 16.5\); among the two with sixteen years, \((24+30)/2 = 27.0\). Mean earnings rise with schooling. In a representative sample of the labour force this pattern is among the most robust empirical regularities in economics: a positive association between schooling and earnings.

    Definition 1.1 — Association

    Two variables are positively associated if larger values of one tend to occur with larger values of the other, and negatively associated if larger values of one tend to occur with smaller values of the other. Association is a descriptive property of the joint distribution of the variables; it carries no implication about cause.

    1.3 The distinction between association and causation

    The observed association admits two fundamentally different explanations, and the data cannot discriminate between them.

    Explanation A (a causal effect of schooling). Schooling raises productivity, and higher productivity commands higher earnings. Under this explanation, increasing an individual's schooling would raise that individual's earnings.

    Explanation B (selection). Individuals who acquire more schooling differ systematically in other respects — ability, family resources, preferences — that independently raise earnings. Under this explanation, the more-schooled would have earned more regardless of their schooling, and schooling itself need have no effect.

    Both explanations imply the same observed table. No quantity computed from these data, and no increase in sample size, distinguishes them. This is the central difficulty of the discipline.

    Key Idea

    Data identify association. A causal conclusion requires, in addition to the data, an assumption about the process that generated them — specifically, an assumption about how the units being compared differ in their unobserved determinants of the outcome. The credibility of a causal conclusion is the credibility of that assumption.

    1.4 The causal comparison and its missing half

    Define the causal effect of schooling for a single individual as the difference between the earnings that individual would receive with additional schooling and the earnings the same individual would receive without it. These two quantities are the individual's potential outcomes. The difficulty is that, for any individual, only one potential outcome is realized — the one corresponding to the schooling actually obtained — while the other is never observed. The causal effect for an individual is therefore not directly measurable, because one of its two components is missing for every unit.

    Definition 1.2 — Ceteris paribus effect

    The ceteris paribus (other things equal) effect of a variable on an outcome is the change in the outcome produced by a change in that variable while all other determinants of the outcome are held fixed. Estimating such an effect from observational data requires approximating the unobserved potential outcome of each treated unit using the observed outcomes of comparable untreated units.

    Every method in this book is a strategy for constructing such comparisons. Their validity rests on the comparability of the treated and untreated units in all respects other than the treatment — a requirement that the subsequent chapters make precise and, by various devices, attempt to satisfy.

    1.5 A first map of the strategies

    It is worth seeing the whole map before studying any single route. The skill the rest of the book builds — and the one the Causal Courtroom arena asks you to practise — is recognising which strategy a given question calls for. You are not yet expected to estimate any of them; you only need to know, in plain terms, what each one does and the situation it fits. Five recur throughout.

    The five comparison strategies
    1. Randomized controlled trial (RCT). Decide who is treated by something like a coin flip. Randomization makes the treated and untreated groups comparable in every respect, observed and unobserved, so a plain difference in their average outcomes is already the causal effect. When a real experiment is possible it is the benchmark; the remaining four are devices for imitating it when randomization is not possible.
    2. Controlling for confounders. When the two groups differ in measured characteristics, compare only units that share those characteristics — equivalently, put the characteristics into a regression as control variables. This removes the part of the gap explained by the controls, but is only as credible as the claim that the important confounders are observed. (Chapters 2–6.)
    3. Instrumental variables (IV). When a key confounder is unobserved, find a third variable — an instrument — that nudges the treatment but touches the outcome only through the treatment. The part of the treatment driven by that nudge is as good as random, and reveals the effect. (Chapter 9.)
    4. Difference-in-differences (DiD). When a treatment switches on for one group at a known date, compare the before-to-after change in that group with the change over the same window in a group that was never treated. Subtracting the untreated group's change nets out whatever was trending anyway. (Chapter 12.)
    5. Regression discontinuity (RD). When treatment is assigned by a sharp cutoff in some running variable — a scholarship granted just above a test-score threshold — compare units just above the cutoff with those just below. Being a hair apart on the score, they are otherwise nearly identical, so the jump in outcomes at the cutoff is the effect. (Chapter 13.)

    These are not interchangeable. Each buys its credibility with a different assumption, and each suits some questions and not others. The Courtroom puts a causal claim on trial and asks you to call the strategy whose assumption is defensible for that claim — the judgement this entire book is about. You will return to each strategy in depth in its own chapter; for now, the goal is simply to tell them apart.

    1.6 Structures of economic data

    The applicable methods depend on the structure of the data, of which four recur throughout the book.

    Data structures
    1. Cross-sectional data: many units observed at a single point in time. The setting of Chapters 2–9 and 14.
    2. Time-series data: a single unit observed at many points in time, in which the ordering of observations is informative. The setting of Chapters 15–16.
    3. Panel (longitudinal) data: many units each observed at several points in time, permitting each unit to be compared with itself across time. The setting of Chapters 10–12.
    4. Pooled cross-sections: independent cross-sectional samples drawn at different times, not following the same units. Employed in Chapter 12.

    A discipline that repays adoption from the outset is to state, before any data are examined, the experiment one would conduct if experimentation were possible. For the schooling question, the experiment is the random assignment of additional schooling to otherwise identical individuals, followed by comparison of their earnings. This hypothetical experiment serves as a standard against which every observational method is to be judged: each technique in this book is an attempt to approximate it when assignment is not under the analyst's control. The formulation also functions as a diagnostic. If the experiment cannot be stated precisely, the causal question itself has not been posed precisely, and no estimation procedure can supply the missing clarity.

    Chapter 2 · The Workhorse

    Simple Linear Regression

    The Question

    By how much does hourly earnings change, on average, with one additional year of schooling?

    Arena⚔ Least-Squares Archer →

    This chapter builds the one tool the rest of the book extends: the simple linear regression. Everything here serves a single, very practical task — drawing the "best" straight line through a cloud of points, then saying clearly what its slope means, how much to trust it, and when it may mislead. The plain-English account comes first; each derivation is tucked into a collapsible box for readers who want the formal version.

    A motivating question

    Suppose you are pricing houses. For hundreds of recent sales you know each home's size in square feet and its sale price. Bigger homes clearly sell for more — but by how much? Is it $100 a square foot, or $200? And how confident can you be in that number, given just this sample? "How much does one more unit of \(x\) move \(y\), and how sure are we?" is precisely what simple regression answers. We develop the method on this housing question, then turn the very same machinery on the book's running question — the earnings return to a year of schooling.

    2.1 The model and its assumptions

    Definition 2.1 — Simple linear regression model

    The model is \( y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x + u \), where \(y\) is the outcome, \(x\) the explanatory variable, \(\beta_0\) and \(\beta_1\) unknown population parameters (the intercept and slope), and \(u\) the disturbance, comprising all unobserved determinants of \(y\) other than \(x\). The slope \(\beta_1\) is the change in \(y\) associated with a one-unit change in \(x\); it is the ceteris paribus effect of \(x\) on \(y\) only under Assumption SLR.4 below. The disturbance \(u\) is worth pausing on: it is everything else that moves the outcome and that we have not put in the model — location, age, and condition for a house; ability and family background for a worker. We never observe \(u\), and the entire question of whether a slope is causal comes down to how \(u\) relates to \(x\).

    The model rests on five assumptions, and they reward being understood one at a time — much of the book is the story of what happens when each one fails and how to cope (non-constant variance in Chapter 7, mismeasured regressors in Chapter 8, unobserved confounders in Chapter 9, and so on). Stated plainly once, the later chapters become variations on a theme.

    Assumptions SLR.1–SLR.5
    1. SLR.1 (linearity in parameters): \(y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x + u\). Why we assume it: it fixes the shape we are fitting — a line in the parameters — so that a single number \(\beta_1\) can summarise the effect. The restriction is only on how \(\beta_0,\beta_1\) enter; \(x\) may itself be a \(\log\) or a square, so genuinely curved relationships remain within reach (Chapter 5).
    2. SLR.2 (random sampling): the \(n\) observations are drawn independently from the population. Why: it lets the sample stand in fairly for the population, so that what we learn generalises. It fails for clustered or time-dependent data, handled later (Chapters 10–12, 15).
    3. SLR.3 (variation in \(x\)): \(\sum_i (x_i-\bar x)^2 > 0\); \(x\) is not constant. Why: a slope measures how \(y\) changes as \(x\) changes — if \(x\) never varies there is nothing to measure, and the more \(x\) varies the sharper the estimate (Section 2.3).
    4. SLR.4 (zero conditional mean): \(\mathrm{E}[u\mid x]=0\); equivalently, \(x\) is uncorrelated with the unobserved determinants of \(y\) collected in \(u\). Why it is the crucial one: this is what turns a slope into a cause. It says the units we compare at different \(x\) do not also differ systematically in what we left out. It is the most often violated and the hardest to defend — bigger houses sit in nicer neighbourhoods, more-schooled workers tend to have higher ability — and nearly every method after this chapter exists to rescue it.
    5. SLR.5 (homoskedasticity): \(\mathrm{Var}(u\mid x)=\sigma^2\), constant across \(x\). Why: it is not needed to get the slope right — only for the textbook standard-error formula to be valid. When it fails (Chapter 7) the estimate stays unbiased but our stated precision is wrong, so we switch to robust standard errors.

    Note the division of labour: SLR.1–SLR.4 make the estimate trustworthy (right on average), while SLR.5 affects only how we gauge its precision. That split is why some violations bias the answer itself and others merely distort the error bars.

    2.2 How OLS chooses the line

    Through any cloud of points one could draw countless straight lines; we need a rule that picks one. The rule is this: for each candidate line, measure how far it misses every point vertically — those misses are the residuals \(\hat u_i = y_i - \hat y_i\), where \(\hat y_i=\hat\beta_0+\hat\beta_1 x_i\) is the line's prediction — and choose the line that makes the misses collectively smallest. "Smallest" is made precise by squaring each miss and adding them up: ordinary least squares (OLS) chooses \(\hat\beta_0,\hat\beta_1\) to minimise the sum of squared residuals,

    \[ \mathrm{SSR}(\hat\beta_0,\hat\beta_1) = \sum_{i=1}^{n}\big(y_i - \hat\beta_0 - \hat\beta_1 x_i\big)^2. \]

    Why squared, rather than the plain or absolute miss? Three practical reasons. Squaring makes every miss count as positive, so over- and under-predictions cannot cancel; it penalises a few large misses far more than many tiny ones, so the line refuses to badly fit any point it could fit; and — decisively — it yields a clean calculus solution, a single closed-form formula rather than an iterative search. That last property is why this rule, among all sensible ones, became the workhorse.

    Carrying out the minimisation gives the two estimates we use throughout:

    \[ \hat\beta_1 = \frac{\sum_i (x_i-\bar x)(y_i-\bar y)}{\sum_i (x_i-\bar x)^2}, \qquad \hat\beta_0 = \bar y - \hat\beta_1\bar x. \]

    Read them in words. The slope \(\hat\beta_1\) is how \(x\) and \(y\) move together, divided by how much \(x\) moves on its own — co-movement scaled by \(x\)'s own spread. The intercept then shifts the line so it passes through the average point \((\bar x,\bar y)\). For the housing data, \(\hat\beta_1\) is simply the dollars of price that come, on average, with each extra square foot.

    For the mathematically curious: deriving the estimates

    Setting the partial derivatives of \(\mathrm{SSR}\) to zero gives the normal equations

    \[ \sum_i \hat u_i = 0, \qquad \sum_i x_i \hat u_i = 0, \]

    which say the residuals average to zero and are uncorrelated with the regressor — the two facts OLS uses up. The first gives \(\hat\beta_0=\bar y-\hat\beta_1\bar x\); substituting into the second and solving for \(\hat\beta_1\) yields the slope formula above. These two conditions, not any distributional assumption, are the entire content of "least squares."

    2.3 Why we can trust the estimate

    OLS hands us a number, \(\hat\beta_1\). A different random sample of houses would hand us a slightly different one. So the honest question is not "is \(\hat\beta_1\) correct?" — it never is, exactly — but "does our recipe for producing it have good properties across the samples we might have drawn?" Four properties matter, and each answers a worry a sceptic would raise.

    Is it right on average? (Unbiasedness.) A sceptic might fear the method systematically overstates or understates the effect. Unbiasedness answers that worry: averaged over all the samples we might have drawn, the estimate lands on the truth. It promises nothing about any single estimate — only that the procedure is not tilted.

    Proposition 2.1 — Unbiasedness

    Under SLR.1–SLR.4, \(\mathrm{E}[\hat\beta_1]=\beta_1\): on average across samples, OLS recovers the true slope. The load-bearing assumption is SLR.4; if \(x\) is correlated with what we left out, this breaks and the estimate is biased — the subject of Chapter 3.

    For the mathematically curious: the proof

    Substituting \(y_i=\beta_0+\beta_1x_i+u_i\) into the slope formula gives \(\hat\beta_1 = \beta_1 + \sum_i w_i u_i\), where \(w_i = (x_i-\bar x)/\sum_j (x_j-\bar x)^2\) depends only on the regressor. Conditioning on the \(x_i\) and applying SLR.4, \(\mathrm{E}[\sum_i w_i u_i \mid x]=\sum_i w_i\,\mathrm{E}[u_i\mid x]=0\). Hence \(\mathrm{E}[\hat\beta_1\mid x]=\beta_1\), and unconditionally \(\mathrm{E}[\hat\beta_1]=\beta_1\). The estimate is the truth plus a weighted average of the disturbances; SLR.4 makes that second piece vanish on average.

    How sharp is it? (Sampling variance.) Unbiased is not enough — an estimate that is right on average but swings wildly from sample to sample is still useless in the one sample we hold. The variance measures that swing.

    Proposition 2.2 — Sampling variance

    Under SLR.1–SLR.5, \( \mathrm{Var}(\hat\beta_1\mid x) = \dfrac{\sigma^2}{\sum_i (x_i-\bar x)^2}. \) In words: precision improves when the noise \(\sigma^2\) is small and when \(x\) varies widely. This is why observing very different house sizes buys more precision than collecting many similar houses — with real consequences for how one gathers data (see Sharma's Take). The noise is estimated by \(\hat\sigma^2=\mathrm{SSR}/(n-2)\), the divisor \(n-2\) reflecting the two parameters already used.

    For the mathematically curious: where the formula comes from

    From \(\hat\beta_1-\beta_1=\sum_i w_i u_i\) with non-random weights \(w_i\), independence (SLR.2) gives \(\mathrm{Var}(\hat\beta_1\mid x)=\sum_i w_i^2\,\mathrm{Var}(u_i\mid x)\). Under homoskedasticity (SLR.5) each \(\mathrm{Var}(u_i\mid x)=\sigma^2\), so the variance is \(\sigma^2\sum_i w_i^2 = \sigma^2/\sum_i (x_i-\bar x)^2\), using \(\sum_i w_i^2 = 1/\sum_i(x_i-\bar x)^2\). When SLR.5 fails, \(\sigma^2\) cannot be pulled out and this formula is wrong — the motivation for robust standard errors in Chapter 7.

    Is it the best such estimate? (Efficiency.) Among all estimators that are both linear in \(y\) and unbiased, OLS has the smallest variance — it wastes no information. This is the Gauss–Markov theorem, and it is why OLS, rather than some other unbiased rule, is the default: under SLR.1–SLR.5 you cannot do better without giving something up.

    Does more data help? (Consistency.) As the sample grows, \(\hat\beta_1\) converges to the true \(\beta_1\): the denominator \(\sum_i(x_i-\bar x)^2\) grows, the variance shrinks toward zero, and with enough data the estimate is almost surely close to the truth — provided SLR.4 holds. Consistency is the large-sample counterpart of unbiasedness, and it is the property we lean on when exact unbiasedness is out of reach in later chapters.

    The four properties, in one breath

    Unbiased = right on average; small variance = sharp in your one sample; efficient = the sharpest among fair (linear, unbiased) rules; consistent = converges to the truth with enough data. The first three rest on SLR.1–SLR.4 for "right" and add SLR.5 for honest error bars. Lose SLR.4 and "right on average" is the first casualty — which is why the entire second half of the book is devoted to defending it.

    2.4 Goodness of fit

    Decompose the total variation in \(y\) into explained and residual components. With \(\mathrm{SST}=\sum_i (y_i-\bar y)^2\), \(\mathrm{SSE}=\sum_i (\hat y_i-\bar y)^2\), and \(\mathrm{SSR}=\sum_i \hat u_i^2\), the normal equations imply \(\mathrm{SST}=\mathrm{SSE}+\mathrm{SSR}\). The coefficient of determination is

    \[ R^2 = \frac{\mathrm{SSE}}{\mathrm{SST}} = 1 - \frac{\mathrm{SSR}}{\mathrm{SST}} \in [0,1], \]

    the proportion of the variation in \(y\) accounted for by the regression. \(R^2\) measures fit, not the validity of a causal interpretation; a high \(R^2\) is neither necessary nor sufficient for an unbiased estimate of \(\beta_1\).

    Worked Example 2.1

    For the five individuals of Chapter 1 (\(x=(10,12,12,16,16)\), \(y=(14,15,18,24,30)\)), the means are \(\bar x = 13.2\) and \(\bar y = 20.2\). The required sums are \(\sum (x-\bar x)(y-\bar y)=66.8\) and \(\sum (x-\bar x)^2 = 28.8\), giving \(\hat\beta_1 = 66.8/28.8 = 2.32\) and \(\hat\beta_0 = 20.2 - 2.32(13.2) = -10.4\). The fitted line is \(\widehat{\text{wage}} = -10.4 + 2.32\,\text{educ}\), with \(R^2 = 0.86\). The intercept lies far outside the range of the data and is not interpretable as the earnings of an individual with no schooling.

    2.5 Estimating a regression in Python

    A regression is estimated with statsmodels using a formula of the form "y ~ x", in which an intercept is included by default. The fitted object exposes the coefficients, their standard errors, and the goodness-of-fit statistics.

    import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

# the formula "y ~ x" specifies a regression of y on x with an intercept
result = smf.ols("wage ~ educ", data=df).fit()
print(result.summary())            # full output: coefficients, standard errors, R-squared

b0 = result.params["Intercept"]    # estimated intercept
b1 = result.params["educ"]         # estimated slope
print(b1)

    The estimates may be reproduced from the formulae of Section 2.2 directly in numpy, which confirms that the regression coefficient is the covariation of \(x\) and \(y\) divided by the variation of \(x\).

    import numpy as np

x = df["educ"].values
y = df["wage"].values
xbar, ybar = x.mean(), y.mean()

# slope = covariation of x and y, divided by variation of x
b1 = np.sum((x - xbar) * (y - ybar)) / np.sum((x - xbar)**2)
b0 = ybar - b1 * xbar
print(b0, b1)                      # identical to the statsmodels estimates

Beside the slope, std err says how much that 0.15 would wobble from sample to sample; the t statistic is the slope divided by its standard error; and P>|t| — here essentially 0 — is the probability of a slope this large if size truly had no effect, so a tiny value means the relationship is far too strong to be chance. The [0.025 0.975] columns give the 95% confidence interval — the range of slopes the data are consistent with. R-squared is the share of the variation in price that size alone explains; the remainder lives in \(u\) — location, age, condition, lot size. That is exactly the force behind SLR.4: if larger homes also sit in pricier neighbourhoods, size is correlated with that omitted quality, and our $150 is part size and part neighbourhood. Chapter 3 adds such controls. Here, with simulated data in which size is the only true driver, the estimate is clean and recovers the built-in $150.

    The sampling variance of the slope, \(\sigma^2/\sum(x_i-\bar x)^2\), shows that precision is determined not only by sample size but by the dispersion of the explanatory variable: the estimate is sharp when \(x\) varies widely and imprecise when \(x\) is concentrated, irrespective of \(n\). This has a consequence for the design of data collection that is frequently overlooked. A moderate sample with wide variation in \(x\) yields a more precise slope than a large sample in which \(x\) is nearly constant. Precision is a property to be designed into the data, not merely estimated from them.

    Chapter 3 · Controlling for Confounders

    Multiple Regression and Omitted-Variable Bias

    The Question

    Is the estimated return to schooling a genuine effect of schooling, or does it reflect ability, which is correlated with both schooling and earnings?

    Arena⚔ The Omitted-Variable Detective →

    Chapter 2's single slope can be read as a causal effect only if the one regressor is unrelated to everything else that moves the outcome — a demanding requirement that rarely holds. Multiple regression is the workhorse remedy: by adding the confounders we can observe as extra regressors, it compares units that are alike in those variables and differ only in the one we care about. This chapter explains, in plain language first, what “controlling for” a variable actually does, why leaving a confounder out biases the estimate in a direction we can usually predict in advance, and what collinearity does and does not do. The formal statements sit in collapsible boxes for those who want them.

    A motivating example

    Does an extra year of schooling really raise earnings — or do people who get more schooling also tend to have more of something else (ability, drive, family resources) that would have raised their earnings anyway? If that something is left out of the regression, the schooling coefficient quietly absorbs it and overstates the true return. Multiple regression lets us hold observable pieces of that confounding (experience, a test-score proxy for ability) fixed and watch the schooling coefficient move as we do. The direction it moves is itself a clue about the confounding. We estimate exactly this in Python below.

    3.1 The model

    Definition 3.1 — Multiple regression model

    The model is \( y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + \cdots + \beta_k x_k + u \). The coefficient \(\beta_j\) is the change in \(y\) associated with a one-unit change in \(x_j\) holding the other regressors fixed — the partial effect of \(x_j\).

    Assumptions MLR.1–MLR.5
    1. MLR.1 (linear in parameters): \(y=\beta_0+\sum_{j}\beta_j x_j+u\). Linear in the coefficients (the \(x\)'s may be logs, squares, or interactions). Why: it is what lets us read each \(\beta_j\) as a constant partial effect.
    2. MLR.2 (random sampling). Why: so sample relationships estimate population ones. Breaks under selection or clustered/dependent data.
    3. MLR.3 (no perfect collinearity): no regressor is an exact linear combination of the others. Why: if two regressors move in lockstep, no data can separate their effects, so the partial effects are not even defined. High-but-imperfect correlation is allowed — see §3.4.
    4. MLR.4 (zero conditional mean): \(\mathrm{E}[u\mid x_1,\dots,x_k]=0\) — the unobserved factors are, on average, unrelated to every included regressor. Why: this is the identifying assumption, the one that makes each \(\hat\beta_j\) unbiased. Breaks when a confounder is omitted or a regressor is mismeasured. It now requires only that \(u\) be unrelated to the included regressors — which is exactly why adding good controls helps.
    5. MLR.5 (homoskedasticity): \(\mathrm{Var}(u\mid x_1,\dots,x_k)=\sigma^2\), the same for all regressor values. Why: not needed for unbiasedness, but needed for the textbook variance formulas and for OLS to be efficient. Breaks when the spread of \(u\) widens with an \(x\) (Chapter 7).

    OLS estimates \(\hat\beta_0,\dots,\hat\beta_k\) by minimizing the sum of squared residuals, exactly as in Chapter 2. Under MLR.1–MLR.4 each \(\hat\beta_j\) is unbiased for \(\beta_j\); adding MLR.5, OLS is BLUE — the minimum-variance linear unbiased estimator, the Gauss–Markov theorem for multiple regression — and the usual standard errors are valid. As the sample grows the estimators are also consistent, converging to the true coefficients.

    3.2 What “controlling for” really means

    When we add experience and ability to a wage regression and report the schooling coefficient as “controlling for experience and ability,” here is what the math is doing. It strips out of schooling the part that can be predicted from experience and ability, leaving only the schooling variation that has nothing to do with them — and relates wages to that. In effect it compares people with the same experience and ability who happen to differ in schooling. That is exactly the ceteris paribus comparison of Chapter 1, reconstructed from observational data. It also explains why a coefficient changes when you add a control: it is now computed from a different, leftover slice of the regressor, so it answers a slightly different question.

    For the mathematically curious: the Frisch–Waugh–Lovell theorem

    The multiple-regression coefficient \(\hat\beta_1\) equals the slope from the simple regression of \(y\) on \(\tilde x_1\), where \(\tilde x_1\) is the residual from regressing \(x_1\) on all the other regressors. Equivalently, \(\hat\beta_1\) is obtained by (i) removing from \(x_1\) the part explained by the other regressors, and (ii) relating \(y\) to the remaining variation in \(x_1\). The demonstration below verifies this numerically: residualize both wage and schooling against the controls, regress one residual on the other, and recover the multiple-regression coefficient exactly.

    Key Idea

    A regression coefficient changes when a control is added because it is then computed from a different, residualized regressor — it answers a different question. Neither the controlled nor the uncontrolled estimate is automatically correct; the appropriate set of controls is the set under which MLR.4 holds.

    3.3 Omitting a confounder: a bias you can sign in advance

    Leave out a variable that (a) belongs in the model and (b) is correlated with a regressor you kept, and that regressor's coefficient gets contaminated. It picks up not only its own effect but a slice of the missing variable's effect, smuggled in through their correlation. The useful part is that you can usually predict the direction of the damage before running anything, from two judgments: does the omitted variable raise or lower the outcome, and is it positively or negatively related to the included regressor? Same-sign answers push the coefficient up; opposite-sign answers push it down.

    For schooling and ability: ability raises earnings (positive) and ability rises with schooling (positive), so the two positives multiply to an upward bias — the uncontrolled schooling coefficient overstates the true return. That is exactly why, in the demonstration, the coefficient falls as the ability proxy enters: we are stripping out an upward bias.

    For the mathematically curious: the omitted-variable bias formula

    Let the population model be \(y=\beta_0+\beta_1 x_1+\beta_2 x_2+u\), but suppose \(x_2\) is omitted and we regress \(y\) on \(x_1\) alone. Let \(\delta_1\) be the slope from regressing the omitted \(x_2\) on the included \(x_1\). Then

    \[ \mathrm{E}[\tilde\beta_1] = \beta_1 + \underbrace{\beta_2\,\delta_1}_{\text{bias}}. \]

    The bias is the product of the omitted variable's effect on the outcome (\(\beta_2\)) and its association with the included regressor (\(\delta_1\)); its sign is the product of their signs — exactly the two-judgment rule above. It vanishes if the omitted variable either does not affect \(y\) (\(\beta_2=0\)) or is uncorrelated with \(x_1\) (\(\delta_1=0\)).

    3.4 Collinearity: imprecision, not bias

    When two regressors are highly (but not perfectly) correlated, multiple regression still identifies their separate effects — it just struggles to pin them down precisely. The intuition follows from §3.2: to estimate the partial effect of \(x_j\), OLS uses only the part of \(x_j\) not explained by the other regressors. If most of \(x_j\) is explained by the others, little independent variation is left, and a coefficient estimated from little variation is noisy. So collinearity inflates standard errors but introduces no bias — the estimates stay centered on the truth, just wobbly. The tempting fix of dropping one collinear regressor usually reintroduces omitted-variable bias, trading a precision problem for a bias problem; the honest response is more, or more variable, data.

    For the mathematically curious: the variance of a coefficient and the VIF

    \[ \mathrm{Var}(\hat\beta_j) = \frac{\sigma^2}{\mathrm{SST}_j\,(1-R_j^2)}, \] where \(R_j^2\) is the \(R^2\) from regressing \(x_j\) on all the other regressors. The factor \(1/(1-R_j^2)\) is the variance inflation factor (VIF): it equals 1 when \(R_j^2=0\) (no inflation) and diverges as \(R_j^2\to 1\). A noisier outcome (larger \(\sigma^2\)) and less spread in \(x_j\) (smaller \(\mathrm{SST}_j\)) also enlarge the variance — the same precision levers as simple regression, now with collinearity added.

    3.5 Estimating multiple regression in Python

    Additional regressors are included by extending the formula with +. Comparing the coefficient on the variable of interest across specifications reveals the influence of the added controls.

    The Frisch–Waugh–Lovell theorem can be verified directly: residualize both the outcome and the regressor of interest against the remaining controls, then regress one residual on the other.

    The same machinery works far from schooling. Suppose we evaluate whether a voluntary job-training program raises earnings.     ★ Sharma's Take

    The direction in which a coefficient moves as a control is added is itself informative about the sign of the confounding, and this information is frequently left unused. The omitted-variable bias formula states that the movement equals minus the bias removed; thus a coefficient that falls when a control enters indicates that the omitted control was biasing it upward, while a coefficient that rises indicates downward bias. Reporting that an estimate is “robust to controls” discards this content. The disciplined practice is to record the direction of each control's effect on the coefficient of interest and to compare it with the sign predicted by \(\beta_2\delta_1\); agreement corroborates the analyst's understanding of the confounding structure, and disagreement signals a determinant that has not been correctly conceived.

    Chapter 4 · Statistical Inference

    Sampling Uncertainty, Confidence Intervals, and Tests

    The Question

    An estimate is computed from one sample. How is its uncertainty quantified, and how is the hypothesis that an effect is zero to be assessed?

    Arena⚔ The p-Hacking Casino →⚔ The Bootstrap Forge →

    An estimate with no measure of its uncertainty is uninterpretable: you cannot tell a real signal from a lucky draw. This chapter builds the machinery that attaches uncertainty to a regression coefficient — where sampling uncertainty comes from, the standard error that measures it, the confidence interval that reports a range of plausible values, and the t- and F-tests that ask whether an effect, or a group of effects, could plausibly be zero.

    A motivating example

    A school district pilots a new online tutoring tool for a semester and estimates a positive effect on test scores. Two different questions follow, and inference answers only the first. Could the estimate be noise? — a single sample can show a positive effect even when the true effect is zero, so we need the standard error, a confidence interval, and a t-test to judge whether zero is still plausible. Is the effect big enough to matter? — that is a question of economic, not statistical, significance, and no p-value answers it. Keeping these two questions apart is the central discipline of this chapter.

    4.1 The sampling distribution

    Distinguish the population, the entirety of units of interest, from the sample, the subset observed. The parameter \(\beta_1\) is a fixed feature of the population; the estimator \(\hat\beta_1\) is computed from the sample and varies across samples. A different sample, drawn from the same population, would yield a different value of \(\hat\beta_1\).

    Definition 4.1 — Sampling distribution

    The sampling distribution of an estimator is the distribution of its values across the repeated samples that might be drawn from the population. Inference concerns two features of this distribution: its centre, which determines bias, and its dispersion, which determines precision.

    Under MLR.1–MLR.4 the estimator is unbiased: the sampling distribution is centred on \(\beta_1\) (Chapter 2). The remaining task is to characterize its dispersion and shape.

    4.2 Why the estimator is bell-shaped

    The estimate is built by adding up the random noise across all the observations. When you add up many small independent random pieces, the total comes out bell-shaped almost no matter what the individual pieces look like — this is the central limit theorem, and it is why the sampling distribution of a regression coefficient is approximately normal in reasonable samples. The practical upshot: estimates near the truth are common, estimates far from it are rare, and how fast “far” becomes rare is set by the standard error.

    For the mathematically curious: normality, MLR.6, and the t distribution

    The estimator is a linear function of the disturbances, \(\hat\beta_1 = \beta_1 + \sum_i w_i u_i\). Under MLR.1–MLR.4 with random sampling, for large \(n\) the standardized estimator \((\hat\beta_1-\beta_1)/\mathrm{se}(\hat\beta_1)\) is approximately standard normal. If the disturbances are exactly normal (the optional assumption MLR.6), the result holds exactly in finite samples, with the standard normal replaced by Student's \(t\) distribution with \(n-k-1\) degrees of freedom. In large samples the \(t\) and the normal are practically identical, which is why the “approximately 2” critical value works.

    4.3 The standard error

    Definition 4.2 — Standard error

    The standard error of \(\hat\beta_1\), denoted \(\mathrm{se}(\hat\beta_1)\), is the estimated standard deviation of its sampling distribution — the typical magnitude of the deviation of the estimate from \(\beta_1\). For the simple-regression slope, \(\mathrm{se}(\hat\beta_1)=\hat\sigma/\sqrt{\sum_i(x_i-\bar x)^2}\), where \(\hat\sigma^2=\mathrm{SSR}/(n-k-1)\).

    The standard error is computed from a single sample; it estimates the dispersion that would be observed across repeated samples without requiring them. It decreases with the residual dispersion \(\hat\sigma\) and increases as the variation in the regressor falls, reproducing the variance formula of Chapter 2.

    The two-standard-error rule

    For a bell-shaped sampling distribution, approximately 95% of estimates lie within two standard errors of the centre (more precisely, 1.96). This fact underlies both confidence intervals and tests.

    4.4 Confidence intervals

    Definition 4.3 — Confidence interval

    The 95% confidence interval for \(\beta_1\) is \(\hat\beta_1 \pm c\cdot\mathrm{se}(\hat\beta_1)\), where \(c\approx 1.96\) for large samples. The interval is constructed by a procedure that, across repeated samples, contains the true \(\beta_1\) in 95% of instances.

    The confidence interval reports magnitude and precision jointly: a narrow interval distant from zero and a wide interval containing zero may share the same point estimate yet convey different conclusions. It is generally more informative than a test outcome alone.

    4.5 Hypothesis tests

    This is the book's first hypothesis test, so here is the whole idea in plain terms before any formulas. A hypothesis test is a disciplined way to ask whether the data are compatible with a specific claim about the population. We begin from a null hypothesis — a sceptical default we are willing to believe until the data argue otherwise, almost always “the effect is zero” (\(H_0:\beta_1=0\)). Against it stands the alternative we suspect (\(H_1:\beta_1\ne 0\), some non-zero effect). The reasoning is proof-by-contradiction softened for a noisy world: assume the null is true, then ask how surprising our actual estimate would be under that assumption. If an estimate this far from zero would almost never arise by chance when the true effect really is zero, we treat the null as implausible and reject it; if such an estimate is unremarkable, we fail to reject.

    Two cautions are built into the language. First, we never “accept” or “prove” the null — failing to reject zero means only that the data do not rule it out, not that the effect is truly zero (a wide confidence interval can be consistent with both zero and a large effect). Second, a test can err two ways: it can reject a null that is actually true (a false positive, or Type I error, whose rate we fix in advance as the significance level \(\alpha\), usually 5%), or it can fail to reject a null that is actually false (a false negative, or Type II error, more likely in small samples). Choosing \(\alpha=0.05\) says we accept a 5% false-alarm rate in exchange for the power to detect real effects.

    With that framework in place, the test of \(H_0:\beta_1=0\) is mechanical. Form the \(t\)-statistic \(t=\hat\beta_1/\mathrm{se}(\hat\beta_1)\), the number of standard errors separating the estimate from zero. Under \(H_0\), \(t\) follows the \(t_{n-k-1}\) distribution, so we know which values are surprising. The hypothesis is rejected at the 5% level when \(|t|\) exceeds the critical value (approximately 2 for large samples) — equivalently, whenever the 95% confidence interval excludes zero.

    Definition 4.4 — p-value

    The p-value is the probability, computed under the null hypothesis, of obtaining a test statistic at least as extreme as the one observed. It is not the probability that the null hypothesis is true, nor the probability that the result is due to chance; it is a measure, computed under the assumption that the null holds, of the discrepancy between the data and that assumption.

    Testing several coefficients at once. Sometimes the question is not “does this one variable matter?” but “do these variables, as a group, matter?” — for example, whether a set of neighborhood dummies jointly belongs in a price regression. The F-test answers this by asking how much worse the model fits when the whole group is forced to zero: a big jump in unexplained variation means the group matters. A large F (small p) rejects the joint null that all of them are zero. The F-test is especially valuable under collinearity, where two variables can each look individually insignificant yet be jointly significant — their shared variation keeps either from standing out alone, but together they clearly belong.

    For the mathematically curious: the F statistic

    To test that \(q\) coefficients are jointly zero, compare the residual sum of squares of the unrestricted and restricted (group-dropped) models:

    \[ F = \frac{(\mathrm{SSR}_r - \mathrm{SSR}_{ur})/q}{\mathrm{SSR}_{ur}/(n-k-1)} \sim F_{q,\,n-k-1}. \]

    The numerator is the per-restriction rise in unexplained variation when the group is dropped; the denominator is the leftover variance. A large ratio means the restrictions hurt the fit a lot relative to noise, so the joint null is rejected.

    One test vs. many: the multiple-testing trap

    A 5% test wrongly rejects a true null about 5% of the time. Run one test and that is a calculated risk; run twenty and you should expect roughly one “significant” result even if nothing is going on. Searching across many regressions, subsamples, or specifications and reporting only the significant one — p-hacking — turns a 5% error rate into a near-certainty of a false positive. The honest defenses are deciding the test in advance, reporting every test you ran, and tightening thresholds when you test many hypotheses. You will feel this directly in The p-Hacking Casino.

    4.6 Inference in Python

    The fitted statsmodels object exposes the standard errors, \(t\)-statistics, p-values, and confidence intervals for all coefficients.

    import statsmodels.formula.api as smf
result = smf.ols("wage ~ educ + exper", data=df).fit()

print(result.bse["educ"])          # standard error of the schooling coefficient
print(result.tvalues["educ"])      # t-statistic for H0: coefficient = 0
print(result.pvalues["educ"])      # corresponding p-value
print(result.conf_int().loc["educ"])   # 95% confidence interval

    Joint hypotheses are tested with the f_test method, which accepts a string specifying the restrictions.

    # joint test that experience and its square contribute nothing: H0 both coefficients = 0
result = smf.ols("wage ~ educ + exper + I(exper**2)", data=df).fit()
ftest = result.f_test("exper = 0, I(exper ** 2) = 0")
print(ftest)                       # F-statistic and its p-value

    Chapter 5 · Functional Form

    Logarithms, Polynomials, and Interactions

    The Question

    How are relationships that are proportional, curved, or dependent on a second variable represented within a model that remains linear in its parameters?

    Arena⚔ The Model Zoo and Specification Safari →

    “Linear” regression is more flexible than it sounds: the linearity it requires is in the parameters, not the variables. So we can capture relationships that are proportional, curved, or dependent on a second variable simply by transforming the inputs — taking logs, adding squared terms, or multiplying two regressors — and OLS runs exactly as before. The work, and the interest, is almost entirely in interpretation: each transformation changes what a coefficient means.

    A motivating example

    Two everyday claims about wages need shapes a straight line cannot draw. First, “each extra year of experience helps, but less and less” — earnings rise with experience, then flatten and eventually peak. A straight line forces a constant effect; a quadratic lets the slope shrink and turn over. Second, “an extra year of schooling raises wages by about the same percentage for everyone” — not the same number of dollars (a level effect), but the same proportional bump. A log outcome expresses exactly that. This chapter gives you the small toolkit — logs, polynomials, interactions — for saying such things precisely, and estimates the experience profile below.

    5.1 The logarithmic transformation

    Economists reach for the natural log constantly, for one reason: differences in \(\ln(y)\) are proportional (percentage) changes in \(y\). Take logs of wages and a coefficient stops meaning “dollars per unit” and starts meaning “percent per unit” — usually the more natural economic statement, and one that also tames skewed, all-positive variables like wages, prices, and firm size. The four combinations of logging the outcome, the regressor, both, or neither give four interpretations worth committing to memory.

    For the mathematically curious: why \(\Delta\ln y\) is a proportional change

    For small changes, \(\Delta\ln(y) \approx \dfrac{\Delta y}{y}\), the proportional change in \(y\); multiplying by 100 gives the percentage change. The approximation is excellent for changes under about 10% and drifts for larger ones (see the exact formula below). This is why a coefficient in a regression of \(\ln y\) reads as a proportional, or percentage, effect.

    Definition 5.1 — Interpretation of logarithmic specifications
    • Level–level (\(y\) on \(x\)): a one-unit increase in \(x\) changes \(y\) by \(\beta_1\) units.
    • Log–level (\(\ln y\) on \(x\)): a one-unit increase in \(x\) changes \(y\) by approximately \(100\beta_1\) percent.
    • Level–log (\(y\) on \(\ln x\)): a one-percent increase in \(x\) changes \(y\) by approximately \(\beta_1/100\) units.
    • Log–log (\(\ln y\) on \(\ln x\)): a one-percent increase in \(x\) changes \(y\) by approximately \(\beta_1\) percent. Here \(\beta_1\) is the elasticity of \(y\) with respect to \(x\).
    For the mathematically curious: the exact percentage effect

    In a log–level model the “\(100\beta\) percent” rule is a small-change approximation. The exact percentage effect of a one-unit rise in \(x\) is \(100\,(e^{\beta}-1)\). For \(\beta=0.05\) this is 5.13% (about 5%); for \(\beta=0.30\) it is 35.0%, not 30% — so prefer the exact form when a coefficient is large or the regressor is a 0/1 dummy.

    5.2 Polynomial terms

    Adding a squared term lets the line bend. In \( y=\beta_0+\beta_1 x+\beta_2 x^2+u \), the effect of \(x\) is no longer a single number — it grows or shrinks as \(x\) itself grows. With \(\beta_2<0\) the curve rises then falls, peaking at a turning point; the experience–earnings profile (up fast early, flattening, eventually declining) is the textbook case.

    For the mathematically curious: marginal effect and turning point

    Differentiating, the marginal effect is \(\partial y/\partial x = \beta_1+2\beta_2 x\), which varies with \(x\). Setting it to zero gives the turning point \(x^\ast=-\beta_1/(2\beta_2)\) — a maximum when \(\beta_2<0\), a minimum when \(\beta_2>0\).

    Key Idea

    In any specification containing a power of a regressor, the effect of that regressor is not a single coefficient but a function of its level, \(\beta_1+2\beta_2 x\). It must be reported at specified values — typically the mean or several points across the range — rather than as one number.

    5.3 Interactions

    An interaction term represents an effect that depends on a second variable. In \( y=\beta_0+\beta_1 x_1+\beta_2 x_2+\beta_3 x_1 x_2+u \), the effect of \(x_1\) is \(\partial y/\partial x_1 = \beta_1+\beta_3 x_2\), a function of \(x_2\). A nonzero \(\beta_3\) indicates that the two variables modify each other's effects; the sign of \(\beta_3\) determines whether they are complementary or substitutive. A positive \(\beta_3\) means each variable raises the other's marginal effect: the two are complements — for example, if the wage return to a year of schooling is larger for workers who also have more experience. A negative \(\beta_3\) means each variable lowers the other's marginal effect: the two are substitutes — the return to schooling is smaller for the more experienced, as though experience and schooling were alternative routes to the same earning power. A useful check: read \(\partial y/\partial x_1=\beta_1+\beta_3 x_2\) and ask whether a larger \(x_2\) makes the effect of \(x_1\) bigger (complements) or smaller (substitutes).

    5.4 Influential observations

    Because the OLS criterion weights observations by their distance from the mean of the regressor (Chapter 2), a small number of extreme observations can determine the estimated form. A nonlinear specification supported only by a few outlying points is not robust. Two safeguards are standard: inspection of a scatter plot before adopting a functional form, and re-estimation with the extreme observations excluded to confirm that the conclusion does not depend on them.

    5.5 Functional forms in Python

    Transformations are written directly into the formula. The function np.log applies the logarithm; the wrapper I(...) protects an arithmetic expression such as a square; the colon : forms an interaction.

    import numpy as np
import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

# log outcome: coefficient is approximately a proportional (percentage) effect
m_log = smf.ols("np.log(wage) ~ educ", data=df).fit()

# quadratic: I(...) protects an arithmetic expression inside a formula
m_quad = smf.ols("wage ~ exper + I(exper**2)", data=df).fit()

# interaction: the colon forms the product term
m_int = smf.ols("wage ~ educ + exper + educ:exper", data=df).fit()

    In a polynomial specification the marginal effect and the turning point are computed from the coefficients.

    # in the quadratic model the marginal effect of experience depends on its level
b1 = m_quad.params["exper"]
b2 = m_quad.params["I(exper ** 2)"]
exper0 = 10
marginal_effect = b1 + 2*b2*exper0       # derivative of the fitted curve at exper = 10
turning_point   = -b1 / (2*b2)           # experience level at which the curve peaks
print(marginal_effect, turning_point)

These are distinct economic claims, and the data themselves bear on their plausibility. The form should be chosen to express the hypothesis the analyst intends to test, rather than adopted by default; it is a component of the model's economic content, not a presentational detail.

    Chapter 6 · Categorical Regressors

    Dummy Variables

    The Question

    How are qualitative characteristics — union membership, region, sex — incorporated into a regression, and how are the resulting coefficients interpreted?

    Many of the things that move economic outcomes are not numbers but categories — union member or not, which region, which industry, male or female. A dummy variable turns a category into a 0/1 switch the regression can use, and the payoff is that its coefficient has a clean meaning: a difference in average outcome between groups. This chapter covers that interpretation, how to handle a category with many levels (and the trap that catches the unwary), and how to let a dummy change not just the intercept but the slope.

    A motivating example

    Suppose women in a sample earn less than men on average. Three questions follow, and dummy variables answer all three. How big is the raw gap? — a regression of log-wage on a single female dummy reads the percentage gap straight off. Does the gap survive controls? — add schooling and experience and the female coefficient becomes the gap between otherwise-similar workers. Does schooling pay off differently for women? — interact the female dummy with schooling and the slope itself is allowed to differ. We estimate all three below.

    6.1 The binary regressor

    Code a category as 1 for “yes” and 0 for “no,” drop it into the regression, and its coefficient is simply the average outcome gap between the yes-group and the no-group. With a log outcome that gap is a percentage difference — use the exact \(100(e^\beta-1)\) from Chapter 5, since dummy coefficients are often large enough that the approximation drifts. Nothing about OLS changes; only the reading does.

    Definition 6.1 — Dummy variable

    A dummy (or indicator) variable takes the value 1 when a condition holds and 0 otherwise. In the regression \( y=\beta_0+\beta_1 d+u \) with \(d\) a dummy, the conditional mean of \(y\) is \(\beta_0\) for the group with \(d=0\) and \(\beta_0+\beta_1\) for the group with \(d=1\). The coefficient \(\beta_1\) is the difference in mean outcome between the two groups.

    The group coded zero is the base (or reference) group, against which the dummy coefficient is measured. When the dummy is included alongside other regressors, its coefficient becomes the between-group difference holding those regressors fixed — a conditional, rather than unconditional, mean difference.

    6.2 Multi-category variables and the dummy-variable trap

    For a category with \(m\) levels (say four regions), you create \(m-1\) dummies and leave one out as the base. Each coefficient then reads as that level's average gap from the base. You must omit one: include all \(m\) dummies and an intercept and the model breaks — the dummy-variable trap.

    For the mathematically curious: why the trap is exact collinearity

    The \(m\) dummies sum to exactly 1 for every observation (each unit is in exactly one category), which reproduces the intercept's column of 1s. That is an exact linear dependence among the regressors — a violation of MLR.3 — so the coefficients are not identified, and software either silently drops a column or returns an error. Omitting one category restores identification.

    Key Idea

    One category must be omitted to serve as the base. All coefficients are then interpreted relative to that base; the choice of base does not affect the fitted values but determines which comparisons the coefficients express, and should be made for interpretability.

    6.3 Letting the slope differ: dummy interactions

    A dummy on its own shifts the whole line up or down — a different intercept for each group. Interact the dummy with a continuous regressor and you let the slope differ too: the return to schooling, say, can be steeper for one group than another. So a plain dummy answers “different starting level?” and a dummy interaction answers “different payoff per unit?” Use both and each group gets its own line entirely.

    For the mathematically curious: two lines from one regression, and the Chow test

    In \( y=\beta_0+\beta_1 x+\beta_2 d+\beta_3 (d\cdot x)+u \), the base group (\(d=0\)) follows \(\beta_0+\beta_1 x\) and the other group (\(d=1\)) follows \((\beta_0+\beta_2)+(\beta_1+\beta_3)x\). So \(\beta_2\) shifts the intercept and \(\beta_3\) shifts the slope. The two groups share one identical line exactly when \(\beta_2=\beta_3=0\); testing that joint hypothesis with an \(F\)-test is the Chow test.

    6.4 Dummy variables in Python

    A binary variable coded 0/1 enters the formula directly. A categorical variable is wrapped in C(...), which constructs the required dummies and omits a base level automatically; the base may be specified with Treatment.

    Chapter 7 · Non-Constant Variance

    Heteroskedasticity

    The Question

    When the dispersion of the disturbance varies with the regressors, are the coefficient estimates and their standard errors still valid?

    Arena⚔ The Heteroskedasticity Thermal Camera →

    The homoskedasticity assumption MLR.5 — that the error has the same variance for every observation — is one of the first to fail in real economic data. The good news is that the failure is mild: it leaves the OLS coefficients unbiased and touches only the standard errors. The bad news is that the conventional standard errors then become wrong, so every t-test, F-test, confidence interval, and p-value built on them is unreliable. This chapter pins down exactly what breaks, and shows that a one-line fix — robust standard errors — restores valid inference and should be your default.

    A motivating example

    Regress household savings on income. Low-income households have little room to maneuver, so their savings cluster tightly around the line; high-income households can save heavily or splurge, so their savings scatter widely. Plot the residuals against income and they fan out — small on the left, large on the right. The line through the middle is still in the right place (the coefficient is fine), but the usual standard-error formula, which assumes uniform scatter, mismeasures how precisely that line is pinned down. We reproduce this fan below and fix the inference.

    7.1 Definition and consequences

    Heteroskedasticity is a long word for a simple picture: the spread of the errors changes across the data instead of staying constant. It is the normal state of cross-sectional data — bigger units usually vary more — so it is worth knowing precisely what it does, and does not, ruin.

    Definition 7.1 — Homoskedasticity and heteroskedasticity

    The disturbance is homoskedastic if \(\mathrm{Var}(u\mid x)=\sigma^2\), constant across the regressors, and heteroskedastic if \(\mathrm{Var}(u\mid x)\) varies with them.

    Heteroskedasticity is the typical condition of cross-sectional economic data: units with larger values of a regressor frequently exhibit greater dispersion in the outcome. Its consequences are precisely delimited.

    Proposition 7.1

    Under MLR.1–MLR.4, the OLS estimator is unbiased irrespective of the form of \(\mathrm{Var}(u\mid x)\). Heteroskedasticity does not bias the coefficients. However, the conventional variance formula of Chapter 4, which assumes homoskedasticity, is no longer valid; the conventional standard errors are therefore incorrect, and the associated \(t\)- and \(F\)-statistics, confidence intervals, and p-values are invalid.

    Unbiasedness follows because it depends only on MLR.4, the conditional mean of the disturbance, not on its variance. The conventional standard errors are typically too small under heteroskedasticity, overstating precision and significance.

    Key Idea

    Heteroskedasticity affects the standard errors, not the coefficients. The remedy is therefore not to alter the estimator but to compute its standard errors by a method valid under non-constant variance.

    7.2 Robust standard errors

    The heteroskedasticity-robust (or heteroskedasticity-consistent) standard errors, due to White, estimate the variance of the OLS estimator without assuming constant error variance, allowing each observation its own variance. They are valid whether or not heteroskedasticity is present: under homoskedasticity they coincide asymptotically with the conventional standard errors, and under heteroskedasticity they remain valid where the conventional standard errors fail. As they impose no cost when homoskedasticity holds and restore validity when it does not, they are the appropriate default for cross-sectional regression.

    For the mathematically curious: what robust standard errors actually compute

    The classical variance of \(\hat\beta\) is \(\sigma^2 (X'X)^{-1}\), which bakes in a single \(\sigma^2\). White's estimator replaces the middle with each observation's own squared residual, \((X'X)^{-1}\big(\sum_i \hat u_i^2\, x_i x_i'\big)(X'X)^{-1}\) — the “sandwich” form. It lets every observation carry its own variance, so no constant-variance assumption is needed. Variants HC1–HC3 add small-sample corrections; HC1 is the common default.

    7.3 Testing for heteroskedasticity

    The Breusch–Pagan test assesses whether the squared residuals are systematically related to the regressors. Its null hypothesis is homoskedasticity; rejection indicates non-constant variance. The related White test admits more general forms by including squares and cross-products of the regressors. In practice, because robust standard errors are valid regardless of the test's outcome, the tests are diagnostic rather than decisive; applied work generally proceeds directly to robust standard errors.

    7.4 Robust inference in Python

    Robust standard errors are obtained by passing cov_type="HC1" to the fit method. The coefficients are unchanged; only the standard errors, and the statistics derived from them, differ.

    import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

ols    = smf.ols("wage ~ educ + exper", data=df).fit()                 # classical SE
robust = smf.ols("wage ~ educ + exper", data=df).fit(cov_type="HC1")   # robust SE

print(ols.bse["educ"])      # standard error assuming constant error variance
print(robust.bse["educ"])   # standard error valid under heteroskedasticity
# the coefficient is identical; only the standard error changes

    The Breusch–Pagan test is available in statsmodels.stats.

    import statsmodels.stats.api as sms

# Breusch-Pagan test: H0 is constant error variance (homoskedasticity)
bp = sms.het_breuschpagan(ols.resid, ols.model.exog)
print("LM statistic:", bp[0], "  p-value:", bp[1])

    Chapter 8 · Measurement and Specification

    Measurement Error and Proxy Variables

    The Question

    When a regressor is observed with error — reported rather than true income, a test score rather than ability — what is the consequence for the estimated coefficient?

    Economic variables are measured imperfectly — income is self-reported, ability is glimpsed through a test score, output is estimated. Where that noise lands matters enormously. Noise in the outcome is harmless to the coefficients (it merely costs precision); noise in a regressor is corrosive, dragging its coefficient toward zero in a predictable way called attenuation. This chapter draws that sharp line, connects attenuation to the proxy variables we lean on when something cannot be measured directly, and adds a test for the separate problem of a wrong functional form.

    A motivating example

    Ask whether income affects health, using survey income — which people misremember, round, and shade. The true income is what matters, but you regress on a noisy version of it. Part of that noisy regressor is pure error, unrelated to health, and it dilutes the signal: the estimated income effect comes out smaller than the truth, never larger. The same logic explains why the test-score proxy for ability in Chapter 3 only partly cleaned up the schooling coefficient. We simulate this melting-toward-zero below.

    8.1 Measurement error in the outcome

    Suppose the outcome is measured with error, the observed value being the true value plus a random term uncorrelated with the regressors. The measurement error is absorbed into the disturbance. Provided it satisfies MLR.4, the OLS estimator remains unbiased; the only consequence is a larger disturbance variance, hence larger standard errors. Measurement error in the outcome reduces precision but does not bias the coefficients.

    8.2 Measurement error in a regressor: attenuation

    Now put the noise in a regressor: you observe the true value plus an independent random error (the classical errors-in-variables setup). Part of what you feed the regression is junk — unrelated to the outcome — and OLS, unable to tell signal from junk, reports a coefficient pulled toward zero. The noisier the measurement, the more it shrinks. This is attenuation bias, and unlike most biases its direction is always known: toward zero.

    For the mathematically curious: the reliability ratio

    Under classical errors-in-variables with a single regressor, \[ \operatorname{plim}\hat\beta_1 = \beta_1\cdot\frac{\sigma_{x^\ast}^2}{\sigma_{x^\ast}^2+\sigma_e^2}, \] where \(\sigma_{x^\ast}^2\) is the variance of the true regressor and \(\sigma_e^2\) the variance of the measurement error. The multiplier — the reliability ratio — lies strictly between 0 and 1, so the estimate is biased toward zero; it falls (more bias) as the noise variance \(\sigma_e^2\) grows.     Key Idea

    Classical measurement error in the outcome reduces precision but preserves unbiasedness; classical measurement error in a regressor biases its coefficient toward zero. The distinction is essential: a noisy outcome warrants no special concern, whereas a noisy regressor warrants careful attention.

    8.3 Proxy variables

    When a determinant of the outcome cannot be measured, an observable correlate — a proxy — may be substituted, as in the use of a test score for ability in Chapter 3. By Proposition 8.1, a proxy measured with error has an attenuated coefficient and therefore removes only part of the confounding it is intended to control. This accounts for the pattern observed in Chapter 3, in which the coefficient on the variable of interest continued to move as successively better proxies were introduced: each imperfect proxy performs only partial adjustment. A coefficient that has not stabilized across improving proxies should be regarded as incompletely corrected.

    8.4 Functional-form misspecification

    A distinct specification error is an incorrect functional form — the omission of a required nonlinear term (Chapter 5). The RESET test assesses this by adding powers of the fitted values to the regression and testing their joint significance; a significant result indicates that the conditional mean is misspecified. It serves as a general diagnostic for omitted nonlinearity.

    8.5 Measurement error and specification tests in Python

    The attenuation result is demonstrated by adding noise of known variance to a correctly measured regressor and observing the resulting coefficient.

    Chapter 9 · Endogeneity

    Instrumental Variables

    The Question

    When a confounder cannot be measured, and no adequate proxy exists, can the causal effect of a regressor nonetheless be estimated?

    Arena⚔ The Instrument Plumber →

    Sometimes the confounder you fear cannot be measured and no proxy will do — controls and robust standard errors are both powerless, because the regressor itself is tangled up with the error term. Instrumental variables is the escape hatch. The trick is to find a third variable that nudges the regressor for reasons that have nothing to do with the outcome, and to use only that clean, instrument-driven slice of variation. This chapter explains when a regressor is “endogenous,” what makes an instrument valid, how two-stage least squares puts it to work, and why a weak instrument can be worse than the disease.

    A motivating example

    Return to the schooling puzzle: more-able people get more schooling and earn more for reasons unrelated to school, so OLS overstates the return — and we cannot observe ability to control for it. Now suppose that, decades ago, some students happened to grow up closer to a college — pure geography, unrelated to their ability. Proximity pushes them to get more schooling but has no direct line to their later wages. That is an instrument: it moves schooling for an “innocent” reason, and the wage difference it traces out estimates the causal return free of the ability bias. We build exactly this below.

    9.1 Endogeneity

    “Endogenous” is the umbrella word for a regressor that is correlated with the error term — the very thing MLR.4 forbids. When it happens, OLS is not merely imprecise, it is inconsistent: more data will not save it. Three culprits produce it, and they recur throughout applied work: an omitted confounder, reverse causation (the outcome feeds back on the regressor), and measurement error in the regressor (Chapter 8).

    Definition 9.1 — Endogeneity

    A regressor is endogenous if it is correlated with the disturbance, \(\mathrm{Cov}(x,u)\ne 0\), in violation of MLR.4. The OLS estimator is then biased and inconsistent. Three sources are standard: an omitted variable correlated with both the regressor and the outcome; simultaneity, in which the outcome also influences the regressor; and measurement error in the regressor (Chapter 8).

    9.2 The instrument

    An instrument \(z\) is a variable that induces variation in the endogenous regressor that is unrelated to the disturbance. The endogenous regressor contains variation correlated with the disturbance (which biases OLS) and, through the instrument, variation that is not; the method uses only the latter. An instrument must therefore hold two jobs at once: it must genuinely move the regressor (relevance), and it must reach the outcome through no channel other than that regressor (exogeneity) — and only the first of these can be checked in the data.

    Definition 9.2 — Conditions for a valid instrument
    • Relevance: \(\mathrm{Cov}(z,x)\ne 0\); the instrument is correlated with the endogenous regressor. This condition is testable from the first-stage regression.
    • Exogeneity (the exclusion restriction): \(\mathrm{Cov}(z,u)=0\); the instrument is uncorrelated with the disturbance, affecting the outcome only through the regressor. This condition is not testable and must be justified by argument.

    9.3 Two-stage least squares

    Two-stage least squares (2SLS) is the practical recipe. Stage one: regress the troublesome regressor on the instrument (and any controls) and keep the fitted values — this is the “clean” part of the regressor, the slice the instrument explains, scrubbed of its correlation with the error. Stage two: regress the outcome on that cleaned-up regressor. The resulting slope is the IV estimate. In a sentence: throw away the contaminated variation in the regressor and keep only the instrument-driven part.

    For the mathematically curious: the IV formula and why 2SLS is consistent

    With a single instrument and no controls, the IV estimator has the closed form \[ \hat\beta_{IV} = \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(z,y)}{\operatorname{Cov}(z,x)}, \] the instrument's effect on the outcome divided by its effect on the regressor (a ratio of the reduced form to the first stage). The stage-one fitted values depend only on the instrument and the exogenous controls, so they are uncorrelated with the disturbance; regressing \(y\) on them therefore yields a consistent estimate under relevance and exogeneity (Definition 9.2). Note IV is consistent, not generally unbiased in finite samples.

    Always estimate 2SLS with a dedicated routine rather than literally running the two regressions yourself: the hand-built second stage reports the wrong standard errors, because it ignores that the first-stage fitted values were themselves estimated. The coefficient comes out identical; the standard errors do not.

    9.4 Weak instruments

    When the instrument is only weakly correlated with the endogenous regressor, the instrument-induced variation is small, and the 2SLS estimator is imprecise and may exhibit substantial bias — in extreme cases exceeding that of OLS. The strength of the instrument is assessed by the \(F\)-statistic on the excluded instrument in the first-stage regression; a common rule of thumb requires it to exceed ten.

    Key Idea

    A valid instrument must satisfy both relevance and exogeneity. Relevance is verifiable from the first stage; exogeneity is not testable and rests on argument. A weak instrument (low relevance) yields imprecise and possibly biased estimates; a violation of exogeneity yields inconsistency that no diagnostic detects.

    9.5 Instrumental-variables estimation in Python

    Two-stage least squares is provided by the linearmodels library, whose formula syntax places the endogenous regressor and its instruments in square brackets. The routine computes correct standard errors.

    from linearmodels.iv import IV2SLS

# syntax: dependent ~ exogenous + [endogenous ~ instruments]
# here educ is endogenous and z is the instrument; exper is an exogenous control
model = IV2SLS.from_formula("wage ~ 1 + exper + [educ ~ z]", data=df)
result = model.fit(cov_type="robust")
print(result.summary)
print(result.params["educ"])       # the 2SLS estimate of the schooling effect

    The strength of the instrument is assessed by the first-stage \(F\)-statistic on the excluded instrument.

    import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

# strength of the instrument: F-statistic on the excluded instrument (rule of thumb > 10)
print(first.f_test("z = 0"))

    Chapter 10 · Panel Data

    Fixed Effects

    The Question

    When the same units are observed over several periods, can the influence of their unobserved, time-invariant characteristics be eliminated?

    Arena⚔ The Fixed-Effects X-Ray →

    When you observe the same units — people, firms, countries — in more than one period, a powerful move opens up: compare each unit only with itself over time. Any confounder that is a fixed trait of the unit (talent, location, culture, management quality) then cancels out, whether or not you can measure it. That is the appeal of fixed effects, and this chapter builds it from the within transformation, states precisely what it can and cannot do, and runs it in Python.

    A motivating example

    Imagine workers whose output quality you track over many weeks. Within each worker, the more hours worked, the lower the quality — fatigue. But skilled workers both work longer hours and produce higher quality, so when you pool everyone into one scatter, the cloud slopes the wrong way and longer hours look good. Skill is a fixed trait you never measured. Compare each worker only with himself across weeks and skill cancels — the true within-worker fatigue effect reappears. The demonstration below builds exactly this sign-flip and fixes it.

    10.1 Panel data and the fixed-effects model

    Panel data index each observation by unit \(i\) and time \(t\). The fixed-effects model assigns each unit its own intercept:

    \[ y_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta_1 x_{it} + u_{it}, \]

    where \(\alpha_i\), the fixed effect, captures all time-invariant characteristics of unit \(i\) — observed or unobserved. The parameter \(\beta_1\) is identified from variation within units over time.

    10.2 The within transformation

    The mechanism is simple subtraction. For each unit, compute its own average over time, then express every variable as a deviation from that average. Because the fixed effect \(\alpha_i\) is the same in every period for unit \(i\), it equals its own time-average and the subtraction wipes it out — leaving a relationship estimated purely from how each unit deviates from its own norm over time.

    For the mathematically curious: the demeaning algebra (and first differences)

    Averaging the model over time for unit \(i\) gives \(\bar y_i = \alpha_i + \beta_1 \bar x_i + \bar u_i\). Subtracting this from the original equation eliminates the fixed effect: \[ (y_{it}-\bar y_i) = \beta_1 (x_{it}-\bar x_i) + (u_{it}-\bar u_i). \] OLS on these demeaned variables is the within (fixed-effects) estimator. First-differencing — subtracting the previous period, \(y_{it}-y_{i,t-1}\) — also removes \(\alpha_i\); it coincides with the within estimator when there are exactly two periods and is an alternative otherwise.

    Definition 10.1 — Fixed-effects (within) estimator

    The within transformation expresses each variable as a deviation from its unit-specific time mean. OLS applied to the transformed data yields the fixed-effects (or within) estimator. Because \(\alpha_i\) is eliminated, the estimator is consistent even when \(\alpha_i\) is correlated with the regressors — that is, in the presence of arbitrary time-invariant confounding.

    10.3 Properties and limitations

    The fixed-effects estimator controls for every time-invariant characteristic of the unit simultaneously, including unmeasured ones. This is its principal advantage. Two limitations follow from the same mechanism.

    • The effect of any regressor that is constant within a unit cannot be estimated, since the within transformation removes it. The effects of fixed attributes — an individual's sex, a firm's country of origin — are not identified.
    • The estimator addresses only time-invariant confounding. A confounder that varies over time in concert with the regressor remains, and biases the estimate.
    Key Idea

    Fixed effects eliminate confounding from time-invariant unit characteristics, observed or not, by comparing each unit with itself. They do not eliminate confounding from time-varying determinants, and they cannot identify the effects of characteristics that are constant within units.

    10.4 Fixed-effects estimation in Python

    The within transformation can be performed explicitly by subtracting unit means, computed with groupby(...).transform("mean"), and applying OLS to the demeaned variables.

    import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

# within transformation by hand: subtract each unit's own time-average from every variable
for col in ["y", "x"]:
    df[col + "_dm"] = df[col] - df.groupby("firm")[col].transform("mean")

fe = smf.ols("y_dm ~ x_dm - 1", data=df).fit()    # "-1": no intercept after demeaning
print(fe.params["x_dm"])           # the fixed-effects (within) estimate

    In practice a dedicated panel routine is used. The linearmodels library provides PanelOLS, which performs the transformation, manages the degrees of freedom, and supports clustered standard errors (Chapter 11), once a unit–time index is set and EntityEffects is requested.

    from linearmodels.panel import PanelOLS

# set a (unit, time) index, then request entity effects
panel = df.set_index(["firm", "year"])
fe = PanelOLS.from_formula("y ~ x + EntityEffects", data=panel).fit(
        cov_type="clustered", cluster_entity=True)     # cluster by unit (Chapter 11)
print(fe.params["x"])

    Chapter 11 · Dependence and Panels

    Clustered Errors, Random Effects, and Dynamic Panels

    The Question

    When observations within a group share unobserved influences, the assumption of independent observations fails. How is valid inference then obtained?

    Every standard error in Chapter 4 assumed your observations were independent — each one a fresh, unrelated draw. In grouped and panel data that is simply false: students in one classroom, workers at one firm, people in one state all share unobserved influences, so their outcomes move together. When you ignore that and count each observation as independent, you credit yourself with more information than you actually have, and your standard errors come out too small — making noise look like signal. This chapter fixes the inference with clustered standard errors, and revisits the fixed-versus-random-effects choice for panels.

    A motivating example

    Suppose you test whether a teaching app raises scores, with 30 students in each of 50 classrooms. The 30 students in a room share a teacher, a room, a schedule — their scores are correlated, so they are not 30 independent facts; they are closer to a handful. Treat all 1,500 as independent and the formula will report a tiny standard error and a triumphant p-value — even when the app does nothing. Cluster the standard errors by classroom and the honest, much larger uncertainty reappears. The demonstration below builds exactly this trap, with a true effect of zero.

    11.1 Within-group dependence

    Observations that share an environment — repeated observations on one unit, or distinct units within one cluster — have disturbances that are correlated, because they are influenced by common unobserved factors. The number of independent pieces of information is then smaller than the number of observations. The conventional standard-error formula, which counts each observation as independent, accordingly understates the sampling variability of the estimator.

    11.2 Clustered standard errors

    Definition 11.1 — Clustered standard errors

    Clustered standard errors estimate the variance of the OLS estimator allowing the disturbances to be arbitrarily correlated within each cluster, while treating distinct clusters as independent. The cluster is chosen to correspond to the level at which the disturbances are dependent — the unit, the industry, or the geographic area at which shocks are shared or treatment is assigned.

    The coefficients are unchanged; only the standard errors are recomputed. Clustering typically increases the standard errors, often substantially, by acknowledging the reduced quantity of independent information. The failure to cluster in grouped data is a common cause of spuriously significant findings.

    For the mathematically curious: how badly are naive SEs wrong?

    With clusters of equal size \(m\) and intra-cluster correlation \(\rho\) (the share of the disturbance variance that is common within a cluster), the conventional variance is too small by the design effect \[ 1 + (m-1)\rho. \] Naive standard errors are therefore too small by a factor of about \(\sqrt{1+(m-1)\rho}\). The damage grows with cluster size: even a mild \(\rho=0.1\) in clusters of 30 gives \(\sqrt{1+29(0.1)}\approx 2\), so the true standard error is understated about twofold. This is why ignoring clustering so reliably manufactures significance.

    Key Idea

    Standard errors should be clustered at the level at which the disturbances are correlated — generally the level at which treatment is assigned or shocks are shared. Clustering at too fine a level continues to overstate precision; the correct cluster is determined by the structure of the dependence, not by convenience.

    11.3 Random effects

    The fixed-effects estimator of Chapter 10 uses only within-unit variation, discarding between-unit variation and the effects of time-invariant regressors. The random-effects estimator instead retains both sources of variation, under the additional assumption that the unit effect is uncorrelated with the regressors.

    Definition 11.2 — Random effects

    The random-effects model treats the unit effect \(\alpha_i\) as a random component of the disturbance, assumed uncorrelated with the regressors. Under this assumption the estimator is more efficient than fixed effects and can identify the effects of time-invariant regressors. If the assumption fails — if \(\alpha_i\) is correlated with the regressors, the situation of unobserved confounding — the random-effects estimator is inconsistent, and fixed effects is required.

    The choice between the two is informed by comparing their estimates, and the Hausman test formalizes that comparison. Its null hypothesis is the random-effects assumption itself — that the unit effect is uncorrelated with the regressors. Under the null both estimators are consistent but random effects is efficient, so the two should sit close together; under the alternative only fixed effects is consistent, so they diverge. The test asks whether the gap between the two coefficient vectors is larger than sampling noise alone would produce.

    For the mathematically curious: the Hausman statistic

    The statistic weights the gap between the estimators by the variance the comparison itself carries: \[ H = (\hat\beta_{FE}-\hat\beta_{RE})'\,\big[\operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{FE})-\operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{RE})\big]^{-1}\,(\hat\beta_{FE}-\hat\beta_{RE}), \] distributed \(\chi^2\) with degrees of freedom equal to the number of compared coefficients under the null. The variance difference appears because, under the null, fixed effects is the less efficient of two consistent estimators, so \(\operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{FE})\ge\operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{RE})\) and the difference is itself a valid (positive) variance for the contrast.

    The reading is simple: reject the null and the unit effect is correlated with the regressors — the unobserved-confounding case — so fixed effects is required; fail to reject and random effects is admissible and, being more efficient, preferred. Two cautions: failing to reject is not proof the assumption holds (the test may simply lack power), and in finite samples the estimated variance difference can come out non-positive, leaving the statistic undefined — a signal to lean on the substantive argument rather than the number.

    11.5 The dynamic panel problem

    A motivating example

    Many outcomes are persistent: a firm's productivity this year depends heavily on last year's; a country's growth, a person's earnings, a region's unemployment all carry forward. To capture that, we put the lagged outcome on the right-hand side — a dynamic panel, \(y_{it}=\rho\,y_{i,t-1}+\alpha_i+u_{it}\). The trouble: the lagged outcome is built from past shocks, and the unit effect \(\alpha_i\) sits inside every period's \(y\), so the lag is mechanically correlated with the error. Neither of our panel tools survives this.

    Pooled OLS is biased upward: the lagged outcome carries the unit effect \(\alpha_i\), which also sits in the current error, inflating the apparent persistence. Fixed effects is biased downward — the famous Nickell bias: demeaning the lag induces a mechanical negative correlation with the demeaned error, of order \(1/T\), so it bites hardest in the short panels that are common in practice. The two estimates therefore bracket the truth rather than pin it down.

    For the mathematically curious: why fixed effects is biased here

    The within transformation subtracts each unit's time-average. But the demeaned lag \(y_{i,t-1}-\bar y_i\) contains \(u_{i,t-1}\) (through \(y_{i,t-1}\)), and the demeaned error \(u_{it}-\bar u_i\) also contains \(u_{i,t-1}\) (through \(\bar u_i\)). They are negatively correlated by construction, so the within estimator is biased toward zero by an amount of order \(1/T\). As \(T\to\infty\) the bias vanishes; for the short panels typical in applied work it does not.

    11.6 Arellano–Bond and system GMM

    The escape is to instrument the troublesome lag with information already inside the panel. First-difference the equation to remove \(\alpha_i\): \(\Delta y_{it}=\rho\,\Delta y_{i,t-1}+\Delta u_{it}\). The differenced lag \(\Delta y_{i,t-1}\) is still endogenous (it shares \(u_{i,t-1}\) with \(\Delta u_{it}\)), but deeper lags of the level — \(y_{i,t-2},\,y_{i,t-3},\,\ldots\) — are uncorrelated with \(\Delta u_{it}\) yet correlated with \(\Delta y_{i,t-1}\). They are valid instruments.

    The simplest version, using a single deep lag \(y_{i,t-2}\) as the instrument, is the Anderson–Hsiao estimator — ordinary 2SLS on the differenced equation, and the direct ancestor of what follows. Arellano–Bond (difference GMM) uses all available deeper lags at once, combining them efficiently by GMM. System GMM (Blundell–Bond) adds a second set of moment conditions — the levels equation instrumented by lagged differences — which is far more informative when the series is persistent (\(\rho\) near one), precisely the case where difference GMM alone grows weak.

    Key Idea

    In a dynamic panel, OLS is biased up and fixed effects biased down (Nickell bias). Arellano–Bond and system GMM difference away the unit effect and instrument the lagged outcome with its own deeper lags, recovering a consistent estimate of persistence.

    Two diagnostics are essential and should always be reported. The Hansen/Sargan test of overidentifying restrictions checks the joint validity of the instruments — a rejection signals invalid instruments. The Arellano–Bond AR(2) test checks for second-order serial correlation in the differenced residuals; its absence is what licenses using lag two and beyond as instruments. A standing danger is instrument proliferation: the count of GMM instruments grows with the square of \(T\), and too many instruments overfit the endogenous regressor, drag the estimate back toward the biased within value, and weaken the Hansen test — so the lag depth is usually capped or the instrument set “collapsed.”

    ★ Sharma's Take

    The seductive part of GMM is that the instruments come free, from inside the data; the dangerous part is that there are so many of them. When \(T\) is even moderate, difference GMM offers dozens or hundreds of moment conditions, and a specification with more instruments than units will quietly overfit, pull the estimate back toward the biased within value, and — worst of all — produce a Hansen test so weakened that it waves everything through with a suspiciously perfect p-value near one. The discipline is to keep the instrument count well below the number of units, to report that count, and to treat the AR(2) and Hansen statistics as gatekeepers rather than formalities. System GMM rescues the persistent case where differencing alone is weak, but it buys that power with an extra assumption about initial conditions; when that assumption is uncomfortable, a smaller, honest difference-GMM specification is the better citizen.

    Difference-in-Differences

    The Question

    A policy is introduced for one group and not another. How is its effect estimated, separating it from permanent differences between the groups and from trends common to both?

    Arena⚔ The DiD Time Machine →

    Difference-in-differences (DiD) is the predominant quasi-experimental design in applied microeconomics. It estimates the causal effect of an intervention administered to one group of units but not to another by contrasting the change in the treated group's outcome with the contemporaneous change in an untreated comparison group. The design removes two distinct sources of bias at once: time-invariant differences in levels between the groups, and time-varying shocks common to both. This chapter develops the estimand within the potential-outcomes framework, states the assumptions under which it is identified, derives the estimator and its regression representation, treats inference and the dynamic (event-study) specification, catalogues the principal threats to validity, and concludes with the failure of the conventional two-way fixed-effects estimator under staggered adoption and the heterogeneity-robust estimators that have superseded it.

    Running example

    Throughout, consider the evaluation of a state-level cigarette excise tax. In a given year a subset of states (“treated”) raise the tax while the remainder (“control”) do not. Per-capita cigarette consumption is declining secularly in all states, and the treated and control states differ in baseline consumption. A before-and-after comparison confined to the treated states would attribute the secular decline to the tax; a cross-sectional comparison of treated and control states in the post-period would attribute pre-existing level differences to the tax. Difference-in-differences is constructed precisely to remove both contaminations, and the design is estimated on this example in Section 12.6.

    12.1 Setup, estimand, and estimator

    Consider two groups, indexed by treatment status \(D_i\in\{0,1\}\), each observed in two periods \(t\in\{0,1\}\), where \(t=0\) precedes and \(t=1\) follows the intervention. In the potential-outcomes notation, let \(Y_{it}(1)\) denote the outcome unit \(i\) would realize in period \(t\) under treatment and \(Y_{it}(0)\) the outcome it would realize absent treatment. Only one potential outcome is observed per unit-period; because treatment is active only for treated units in the post-period, the realized outcome is \(Y_{it}=Y_{it}(0)+D_i\,t\,[\,Y_{it}(1)-Y_{it}(0)\,]\).

    The parameter of interest is the average treatment effect on the treated (ATT) in the post-period,

    \[ \tau_{ATT}=\mathbb{E}\!\left[\,Y_{i1}(1)-Y_{i1}(0)\,\middle|\,D_i=1\,\right]. \]

    The first term, \(\mathbb{E}[Y_{i1}(1)\mid D_i=1]\), is the observed post-period mean of the treated group. The counterfactual term \(\mathbb{E}[Y_{i1}(0)\mid D_i=1]\) — the outcome the treated group would have realized absent treatment — is never observed and must be supplied by the identifying assumption of Section 12.2.

    Under that assumption the ATT is identified by the difference in differences of the four group-by-period means,

    \[ \hat\tau=\big(\bar Y_{1,\text{post}}-\bar Y_{1,\text{pre}}\big)-\big(\bar Y_{0,\text{post}}-\bar Y_{0,\text{pre}}\big), \]

    where the leading subscript denotes treatment status. The inner differences eliminate each group's time-invariant level; the outer difference eliminates the trend common to both groups, which under parallel trends coincides with the treated group's counterfactual trend. What remains is the treatment effect.

    The regression and two-way fixed-effects representations

    The double difference \(\hat\tau\) is algebraically the ordinary-least-squares coefficient on the interaction term in the saturated regression

    \[ Y_{it}=\beta_0+\beta_1 D_i+\beta_2\,\text{Post}_t+\tau\,(D_i\times\text{Post}_t)+\varepsilon_{it}, \]

    in which \(\beta_1\) absorbs the permanent level difference between the groups, \(\beta_2\) the common time effect, and \(\tau\) is the interaction coefficient, identical to the double difference (Exercise 1). With many units and periods the same logic is expressed through the two-way fixed-effects (TWFE) specification

    \[ Y_{it}=\alpha_i+\lambda_t+\tau\,\text{Treat}_{it}+\varepsilon_{it}, \]

    where \(\alpha_i\) and \(\lambda_t\) are unit and period effects and \(\text{Treat}_{it}\) indicates active treatment. With a single adoption date and two groups this reproduces \(\hat\tau\) exactly. The regression form is preferred because it admits covariates, generalizes to many periods, and supplies a standard-error apparatus; the qualifications it acquires under staggered adoption are the subject of Section 12.7.

    12.2 Identification: parallel trends and auxiliary assumptions

    Assumption 12.1 — Parallel (common) trends

    In the absence of treatment, the expected change in the untreated potential outcome is identical across the two groups:

    \[ \mathbb{E}\!\left[Y_{i1}(0)-Y_{i0}(0)\mid D_i=1\right]=\mathbb{E}\!\left[Y_{i1}(0)-Y_{i0}(0)\mid D_i=0\right]. \]

    Assumption 12.1 licenses the control group's observed change as the counterfactual change for the treated group, and hence point-identifies the ATT. It concerns an unobservable counterfactual trend and so cannot be verified directly; its plausibility is assessed from pre-treatment data (Section 12.4). Two properties merit emphasis. First, the assumption restricts trends, not levels: the groups may differ arbitrarily at baseline, and the design is invariant to such differences. Second, parallel trends is not scale-invariant — trends that are parallel in levels need not remain parallel after a logarithmic or other nonlinear transformation — so the choice of functional form is itself a substantive identifying decision (Section 12.6).

    Three auxiliary conditions are required for the standard interpretation.

    The auxiliary identifying assumptions

    No anticipation. Units do not alter behaviour in the pre-period in anticipation of treatment; formally \(Y_{i0}=Y_{i0}(0)\) for treated units, so the baseline outcome is untreated. Anticipatory responses contaminate the baseline and bias the estimate.

    Stable unit treatment values (SUTVA / no interference). A unit's potential outcomes depend only on its own treatment status, not on the treatment of others. Spillovers from treated to control units violate this condition; when controls are partially affected in the same direction, the estimate is typically biased toward zero.

    Stable composition. The groups consist of the same populations (or random samples thereof) in both periods. If treatment induces selective entry or exit, the observed change conflates the treatment effect with a change in composition.

    When parallel trends holds only after conditioning on covariates \(X\), the assumption becomes conditional parallel trends, and identification proceeds within covariate strata, by regression adjustment, by inverse-probability weighting on the propensity score, or by doubly-robust combinations of the two that remain consistent if either the outcome model or the propensity model is correctly specified.

    12.3 Inference

    Observations on a given unit across periods are serially correlated, and units sharing a treatment assignment are subject to common shocks; treating unit-period observations as independent therefore understates the sampling variability, frequently to a severe degree. Standard errors should be clustered at the level at which treatment is assigned — typically the unit, such as the state. Bertrand, Duflo, and Mullainathan (2004) showed that neglect of within-unit serial correlation produces badly overstated significance in difference-in-differences applications, with conventional standard errors rejecting true null hypotheses far more often than their nominal size. When the number of clusters is small the asymptotic justification for the cluster-robust variance estimator is weak, and corrections are warranted: the wild cluster bootstrap, critical values from a \(t\)-distribution with degrees of freedom governed by the number of clusters rather than observations, or, with very few treated clusters, randomization (permutation) inference.

    12.4 Dynamic effects and the event study

    The two-period design compresses the entire post-treatment response into a single number. With multiple periods, the event-study (dynamic) specification estimates a separate treatment-group–period interaction for each period relative to a normalized base, conventionally the period immediately preceding treatment:

    \[ Y_{it}=\alpha_i+\lambda_t+\sum_{k\neq-1}\delta_k\,\mathbf{1}\{t-t^*_i=k\}\,D_i+\varepsilon_{it}, \]

    where \(t^*_i\) is the adoption date, \(k\) indexes event time (periods since treatment), and \(k=-1\) is omitted as the reference. The coefficients \(\delta_k\) for \(k\ge 0\) trace the trajectory of the treatment effect; the coefficients \(\delta_k\) for \(k<-1\) — the pre-treatment leads — should be statistically and economically indistinguishable from zero if the groups evolved in parallel before treatment.

    The event study discharges two functions. First, the pre-treatment leads constitute the central empirical evidence for Assumption 12.1: their flatness is the visible support for the design, and a systematic pre-trend signals a violation. Second, the post-treatment coefficients reveal whether the effect appears immediately or with delay and whether it persists, decays, or grows — information of independent substantive importance that the scalar ATT conceals. Two cautions attend the leads: a test of them has limited statistical power, so failure to reject zero pre-trends does not establish parallel trends; and conditioning the analysis on having passed a pre-trends test itself distorts subsequent inference, as analyzed by Roth (2022). Flat leads are corroborating evidence, not proof.

    Key Idea

    The credibility of a difference-in-differences estimate rests on the parallel-trends assumption, which cannot be proved but can be rendered plausible by demonstrating that the groups moved in parallel before treatment. The event study supplies that demonstration and, through its post-treatment coefficients, the time profile of the effect.

    12.5 Implementation

    The headline estimate is the coefficient on the treatment–period interaction, with standard errors clustered at the assignment level (Section 12.3); the event study is obtained by interacting the treatment indicator with a full set of period indicators, omitting the base period.

    import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

# difference-in-differences: the interaction is the ATT; cluster by assignment unit
did = smf.ols("y ~ treated + post + treated:post", data=df).fit(
          cov_type="cluster", cov_kwds={"groups": df["state"]})
print(did.params["treated:post"])

# event study: interact treated with each period (base period omitted)
event = smf.ols("y ~ treated + C(year) + treated:C(year)", data=df).fit(
            cov_type="cluster", cov_kwds={"groups": df["state"]})

    12.6 Threats to validity

    Beyond the failure of parallel trends itself, several distinct mechanisms can invalidate a difference-in-differences estimate; each corresponds to the violation of an assumption in Section 12.2.

    Anticipation. If units respond before the nominal treatment date — because the policy was announced in advance or otherwise expected — the pre-period outcome is no longer the untreated potential outcome and the baseline is contaminated. Anticipation manifests as nonzero leads immediately preceding adoption and can sometimes be accommodated by redefining the treatment date or excluding the anticipation window.

    Ashenfelter's dip. A common form of pre-treatment divergence arises when selection into treatment is driven by a transitory shock — the classic instance being enrolment in a training programme after a temporary fall in earnings. The treated group's pre-period outcome is then atypically depressed; mechanical mean reversion inflates its subsequent change and the effect is overstated. The dip appears in the event study as a downward pre-trend that reverses at treatment.

    Compositional change. When the membership of the groups changes between periods — through selective migration, attrition, or treatment-induced entry — the observed change confounds the treatment effect with a change in who is measured. Repeated cross-sections are especially vulnerable; panel data on fixed units mitigate but do not eliminate the problem where attrition is itself treatment-related.

    Spillovers. If the treatment of one unit affects the outcomes of control units — through general-equilibrium effects, geographic proximity, or strategic responses — SUTVA fails and the control group ceases to measure the treated group's counterfactual. The bias is typically toward zero when controls are affected in the same direction as the treated. Buffer zones, exclusion of proximate controls, or explicit spatial models are used to address it.

    Functional form. Because parallel trends is not scale-invariant, the estimate depends on whether the outcome enters in levels, logarithms, or another transformation, and the assumption can hold in at most one of them. Where no single scale is defensible, the changes-in-changes estimator of Athey and Imbens (2006) identifies the entire counterfactual distribution under a weaker, scale-free monotonicity assumption and is the appropriate generalization.

    12.7 Staggered adoption and heterogeneity-robust estimators

    The preceding analysis assumes a single adoption date common to all treated units. Many policies instead diffuse over time, with units adopting in different periods — staggered adoption. The natural response is to estimate the two-way fixed-effects regression \(Y_{it}=\alpha_i+\lambda_t+\tau\,\text{Treat}_{it}+\varepsilon_{it}\) and report the single coefficient \(\tau\). Recent work has established that this practice is unreliable when treatment effects are heterogeneous across cohorts or dynamic over time.

    Goodman-Bacon (2021) demonstrates that the TWFE estimand is a weighted average of all two-group, two-period difference-in-differences comparisons embedded in the panel. These comprise not only the desired comparisons of treated cohorts against not-yet-treated units, but also forbidden comparisons in which a later-treated cohort serves as the control for an earlier-treated cohort. In such a comparison the “control” is itself already treated, so its outcome reflects the evolving treatment effect rather than the untreated counterfactual. When effects vary over time, these comparisons enter with negative weights, and a weighted average containing negative weights need not lie within the range of the underlying effects; the TWFE coefficient can therefore be attenuated, inflated, or even of the wrong sign while every unit's true effect is positive.

    Key Idea

    With a single adoption date and two groups, two-way fixed effects is valid. Under staggered adoption with heterogeneous or dynamic effects, the single TWFE coefficient is a contaminated weighted average and may be severely biased; a heterogeneity-robust estimator is required.

    The remedy is to restrict attention to clean comparisons — treated cohorts against not-yet-treated or never-treated units — to estimate a separate effect for each adoption cohort and event time, and to aggregate these with non-negative weights. The estimators of Callaway and Sant'Anna (2021), Sun and Abraham (2021), and de Chaisemartin and D'Haultfœuille (2020), together with the imputation approach of Borusyak, Jaravel, and Spiess (2024), implement this programme and have become standard for staggered designs; the Goodman-Bacon decomposition is the diagnostic that reveals how much of a given TWFE estimate derives from forbidden comparisons. The two-group, single-date design of this chapter is immune to the problem, which arises only once adoption dates differ.

    ★ Sharma's Take

    The event-study figure is presented as a test of parallel trends, which it is, but the post-policy coefficients additionally convey the time profile of the effect, which is itself a principal finding. An effect that appears immediately and persists differs in its implications from one that emerges gradually or one that decays, although all three may share the same average. Before the estimate is reduced to a single number, the post-policy trajectory should be read as a result in its own right: whether the effect is immediate or delayed, and whether it is durable or transient, bears directly on the value of the policy and is frequently the information a decision-maker most requires. The average answers whether the policy had an effect; the trajectory answers how that effect evolved.

    Chapter 13 · Discontinuities

    Regression Discontinuity

    The Question

    When a treatment is assigned by a strict threshold on a continuous variable, can the units just above and just below the threshold be used to identify its effect?

    Arena⚔ The Regression-Discontinuity Cliff →

    The regression-discontinuity (RD) design is among the most credible quasi-experimental designs in the empirical social sciences. It applies whenever treatment is assigned by whether a continuous running variable (equivalently, forcing or assignment variable) crosses a fixed cutoff. Under a continuity assumption, units arbitrarily close to the cutoff on opposite sides are comparable in all determinants of the outcome save treatment, so the discontinuity in the outcome at the cutoff identifies the treatment effect for units at the threshold. This chapter formalizes that identification in both the sharp and fuzzy designs, develops local polynomial estimation and the central role of the bandwidth, treats the falsification tests on which the design's credibility rests, and addresses the local character of the resulting estimand.

    Running example

    In the United States the legal right to purchase alcohol commences at age 21. Individuals just below and just above this threshold are, in expectation, indistinguishable in maturity, health, and circumstance, yet they differ in legal access to alcohol. A discontinuous increase in the mortality rate precisely at age 21, within an otherwise smooth age–mortality profile, is therefore attributable to the change in legal access rather than to age itself. Here the running variable is age, the cutoff is 21, and the running variable cannot be manipulated — no individual can alter the date of birth — which renders the design especially clean. The method is developed on this example and estimated in Section 13.5.

    13.1 The sharp design: setup and estimand

    Let \(X_i\) denote the running variable and \(c\) the cutoff. In the sharp design, treatment is a deterministic, discontinuous function of the running variable, \(D_i=\mathbf{1}\{X_i\ge c\}\), so every unit at or above the cutoff is treated and every unit below is untreated. In the potential-outcomes notation, unit \(i\) has outcomes \(Y_i(1)\) and \(Y_i(0)\) under treatment and control, with observed outcome \(Y_i=Y_i(0)+D_i[Y_i(1)-Y_i(0)]\).

    Assumption 13.1 — Continuity

    The conditional expectations of the potential outcomes are continuous in the running variable at the cutoff: the maps \(x\mapsto\mathbb{E}[Y_i(0)\mid X_i=x]\) and \(x\mapsto\mathbb{E}[Y_i(1)\mid X_i=x]\) are continuous at \(x=c\).

    Continuity formalizes the comparability of units on either side of the cutoff: absent the discontinuous treatment rule, the outcome would evolve smoothly through \(c\), so any discontinuity in the observed outcome at \(c\) is attributable to treatment alone. Under Assumption 13.1 the treatment effect at the cutoff is identified as the difference of one-sided limits of the conditional mean of the observed outcome,

    \[ \tau_{SRD}=\lim_{x\downarrow c}\mathbb{E}[Y_i\mid X_i=x]-\lim_{x\uparrow c}\mathbb{E}[Y_i\mid X_i=x]=\mathbb{E}\!\left[\,Y_i(1)-Y_i(0)\mid X_i=c\,\right]. \]

    The estimand is thus the average treatment effect for units located at the cutoff — a local parameter whose interpretation and limits are taken up in Section 13.4 and the closing remark. Because no unit is observed both just above and just below the cutoff, identification rests on extrapolating each conditional mean to the boundary, which is why estimation reduces to careful local approximation (Section 13.3).

    13.2 The fuzzy design

    In many applications, crossing the cutoff does not determine treatment but only alters its probability: eligibility is conferred at the threshold yet take-up is incomplete, or the assignment rule is imperfectly enforced. The fuzzy design accommodates this case, in which the treatment probability jumps discontinuously at the cutoff without attaining the extremes of zero and one,

    \[ \lim_{x\downarrow c}\Pr(D_i=1\mid X_i=x)\neq\lim_{x\uparrow c}\Pr(D_i=1\mid X_i=x). \]

    The treatment effect is then identified as the ratio of the discontinuity in the outcome to the discontinuity in the treatment probability,

    \[ \tau_{FRD}=\frac{\displaystyle\lim_{x\downarrow c}\mathbb{E}[Y_i\mid X_i=x]-\lim_{x\uparrow c}\mathbb{E}[Y_i\mid X_i=x]}{\displaystyle\lim_{x\downarrow c}\mathbb{E}[D_i\mid X_i=x]-\lim_{x\uparrow c}\mathbb{E}[D_i\mid X_i=x]}, \]

    a structure identical to the Wald instrumental-variables estimator of Chapter 9, with the cutoff indicator serving as an instrument for treatment. Under the continuity assumptions together with a monotonicity condition — no unit is deterred from treatment by crossing into eligibility — \(\tau_{FRD}\) is the local average treatment effect for the compliers at the cutoff, namely those units whose treatment status is changed by crossing the threshold. The sharp design is the special case in which the treatment probability jumps from zero to one.

    13.3 Estimation: local polynomial regression and the bandwidth

    The conditional-mean limits are estimated by local polynomial regression: within a window of half-width \(h\) (the bandwidth) on each side of the cutoff, a low-order polynomial — in practice linear or quadratic — is fitted to the outcome against the centred running variable, and the two fitted intercepts at the cutoff are differenced to estimate \(\tau\). Observations are commonly weighted by a kernel that declines with distance from the cutoff (the triangular kernel is mean-squared-error optimal for a boundary point), conferring greatest influence on the most comparable units. In the fuzzy design the same local regression is applied to the treatment indicator, and the ratio of the two estimated discontinuities is formed.

    Key Idea — the bandwidth trade-off

    The bandwidth governs a bias–variance trade-off. A wide bandwidth admits more observations and lowers variance, but observations far from the cutoff may follow a curved relationship that a low-order polynomial approximates poorly, introducing bias. A narrow bandwidth restricts attention to the most comparable units, lowering bias, but uses fewer observations and raises variance. The estimate should be reported across a range of bandwidths, with stability as the bandwidth narrows taken as support for the design.

    For the mathematically curious: optimal bandwidth and bias correction

    Within a bandwidth \(h\), the variance of the discontinuity estimate falls roughly like \(1/(nh)\), while the bias from approximating a curved conditional mean by a polynomial of degree \(p\) grows like \(h^{p+1}\). Minimising the asymptotic mean squared error yields an optimal bandwidth that shrinks with the sample at the rate \(h^{*}\propto n^{-1/(2p+3)}\) — \(n^{-1/5}\) for local linear estimation. Data-driven selectors (Imbens and Kalyanaraman, 2012; Calonico, Cattaneo, and Titiunik, 2014) automate this choice. Because the MSE-optimal bandwidth balances squared bias against variance, the resulting point estimate retains a bias of the same order as its standard error, so conventional confidence intervals are mis-centred; the robust bias-correction procedure of Calonico, Cattaneo, and Titiunik (2014) removes the leading bias and adjusts the variance accordingly, and is the current standard for RD inference.

    High-order global polynomials — a single high-degree fit over the entire support of the running variable — should be avoided: they assign erratic weights to observations far from the cutoff, are sensitive to the chosen degree, and can manufacture spurious discontinuities, as documented by Gelman and Imbens (2019). Local linear or local quadratic estimation within a data-driven bandwidth is the preferred practice.

    13.4 Validity and falsification tests

    The credibility of an RD design rests on the continuity assumption, which, though not directly testable, carries observable implications that support a battery of falsification tests.

    Manipulation of the running variable. Identification requires that units be unable to precisely control their position relative to the cutoff. Where they can — an applicant reclassified just above an eligibility threshold, a score nudged just past a passing mark — units just above come to differ systematically from those just below in the characteristic that enabled the sorting, and continuity fails. The leading diagnostic examines the density of the running variable at the cutoff: under no manipulation the density is continuous, whereas manipulation produces a discontinuity, with an accumulation of units on the favoured side and a deficit on the other. The McCrary (2008) density test, and the local-polynomial density test of Cattaneo, Jansson, and Ma (2020), formalize this check.

    Continuity of covariates. Predetermined covariates — characteristics fixed before treatment — should display no discontinuity at the cutoff, since treatment cannot affect them. A jump in such a covariate signals a violation of continuity and casts doubt on the design. Standard practice applies the RD estimator to each covariate as a placebo outcome and confirms that the estimated discontinuities are null.

    Placebo cutoffs and bandwidth sensitivity. Estimating a discontinuity at artificial cutoffs away from the true threshold should yield null effects; a significant jump at a placebo cutoff indicates that the conditional mean is not smooth and undermines the design. The estimate should further be shown to be stable across a range of bandwidths and polynomial orders.

    13.5 Implementation

    The running variable is centred at the cutoff and an above-cutoff indicator is constructed; a local linear regression with separate slopes on each side is fitted within a bandwidth, and the coefficient on the indicator is the estimated discontinuity. The estimate is then examined across several bandwidths and the density of the running variable inspected at the cutoff.

    import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

c = 90.0                                  # cutoff of the running variable (score)
df["above"]   = (df["score"] >= c).astype(int)
df["score_c"] = df["score"] - c           # centre the running variable at the cutoff

# local linear regression within bandwidth h, separate slopes on each side
h = 10.0
window = df[df["score_c"].abs() <= h]
rd = smf.ols("y ~ above + score_c + above:score_c", data=window).fit(cov_type="HC1")
print(rd.params["above"])                 # the discontinuity = the treatment effect

    ★ Sharma's Take

    The regression-discontinuity design yields an unusually credible estimate of an unusually specific quantity, and the specificity is essential to its interpretation. The estimate is the effect of treatment for units at the cutoff — the marginal cases — and is not informative about the effect for units far from it. This is not a defect: a precise estimate for the marginal units is exactly what is required by a decision concerning where to set the cutoff. But the estimate does not generalize as a randomized experiment on the whole population would, and the qualification “for units near the threshold” should accompany every statement of the result. The credibility of the design derives entirely from confining attention to the vicinity of the cutoff, and the reach of its conclusion is confined to the same vicinity.

    Chapter 14 · Binary Outcomes

    Models for a Binary Dependent Variable

    The Question

    When the outcome is a binary event — employed or not, default or not — how is the probability of the event modelled, and how are the coefficients interpreted?

    Arena⚔ The Probability Frontier →

    When the dependent variable is binary, recording the occurrence of an event, its conditional mean is the conditional probability of the event. Linear regression remains applicable but sits uneasily with a bounded probability, motivating the nonlinear index models that are the subject of this chapter. We develop the linear probability model, then the logit and probit models through a common latent-variable formulation, estimate all three by their appropriate methods, and treat at length the interpretation of their coefficients — the central difficulty of the subject — including marginal effects, the odds-ratio reading specific to logit, the latent-index reading specific to probit, and the systematic scaling relationship between the two. Goodness-of-fit measures and a full Python implementation conclude the chapter.

    Running example

    Consider a lender deciding whether to approve a loan. For each applicant the outcome of interest is binary — the borrower either defaults or does not — and the lender wishes to model the probability of default as a function of observable characteristics, principally the applicant's debt-to-income ratio and credit score. The quantity to be modelled is therefore a conditional probability, bounded within \([0,1]\), and the questions the lender must answer are precisely those this chapter addresses: how a one-unit change in the debt-to-income ratio alters the probability of default, whether a higher credit score lowers it, and by how much. The method is developed on this example and estimated for a simulated loan portfolio in Section 14.7.

    14.1 Binary outcomes and the conditional probability

    Let \(y\in\{0,1\}\) record the occurrence of an event. Its expectation conditional on regressors \(x\) is \(\mathbb{E}[y\mid x]=\Pr(y=1\mid x)\), since \(\mathbb{E}[y\mid x]=1\cdot\Pr(y=1\mid x)+0\cdot\Pr(y=0\mid x)\). Modelling the conditional mean of a binary outcome is therefore identical to modelling the conditional probability of the event, and every model in this chapter is a specification of \(\Pr(y=1\mid x)\). A binary outcome is a Bernoulli random variable, so its conditional variance is \(\Pr(y=1\mid x)\,[1-\Pr(y=1\mid x)]\), which depends on \(x\) — a fact with consequences for estimation developed below.

    14.2 The linear probability model

    Definition 14.1 — Linear probability model

    The linear probability model (LPM) specifies the conditional probability as linear in the parameters and estimates it by ordinary least squares applied to the binary outcome:

    \[ \Pr(y=1\mid x)=\beta_0+\beta_1 x_1+\cdots+\beta_k x_k. \]

    Each coefficient \(\beta_j\) is the change in the probability of the event for a one-unit change in \(x_j\), holding the others fixed — a constant partial effect, interpretable without further transformation. This directness, together with the familiarity of OLS and its convenience in specifications with fixed effects or instrumental variables, accounts for the model's continued use.

    Two limitations follow from the linear form. First, the fitted probability is unbounded and may fall below zero or above one for sufficiently extreme regressor values, which is incoherent as a probability and distorts the implied partial effects in those regions. Second, because the outcome is Bernoulli, its conditional variance \(\Pr(y=1\mid x)[1-\Pr(y=1\mid x)]\) depends on \(x\); the errors are intrinsically heteroskedastic, and heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors are mandatory. The model is most defensible when the fitted probabilities lie predominantly in the interior of \([0,1]\), a condition examined in Sharma's Take.

    14.3 The latent-variable framework: logit and probit

    To constrain the fitted probability to \([0,1]\), the index models pass a linear index through a cumulative distribution function. A unifying derivation posits a continuous but unobserved latent variable \(y^{*}\) — a propensity, net utility, or creditworthiness — linear in the regressors,

    \[ y^{*}=x'\beta+\varepsilon,\qquad y=\mathbf{1}\{y^{*}>0\}, \]

    so that the event occurs when the latent propensity crosses a threshold normalized to zero. The probability of the event is then

    \[ \Pr(y=1\mid x)=\Pr(\varepsilon>-x'\beta)=G(x'\beta), \]

    where \(G\) is the cumulative distribution function of \(-\varepsilon\), assumed symmetric about zero. The two leading models differ only in the distribution assumed for \(\varepsilon\).

    Definition 14.2 — Logit and probit

    The logit model takes \(G\) to be the logistic CDF, \(\Lambda(z)=\dfrac{e^{z}}{1+e^{z}}=\dfrac{1}{1+e^{-z}}\). The probit model takes \(G\) to be the standard-normal CDF, \(\Phi(z)=\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^{z}\phi(u)\,du\). Both are S-shaped, increasing monotonically from 0 to 1, and both therefore deliver fitted probabilities strictly within \([0,1]\).

    Because the latent variable is unobserved, its scale is not identified: only the sign of \(y^{*}\) is observed, so multiplying \(y^{*}\), \(\beta\), and \(\varepsilon\) by a positive constant leaves \(y\) unchanged. The scale is fixed by normalizing the variance of \(\varepsilon\) — to \(\pi^{2}/3\) in the logit, to \(1\) in the probit. The coefficients \(\beta\) are thus defined only up to this normalization, which is the origin of the systematic difference in their magnitudes across the two models (Section 14.5.5).

    14.4 Estimation by maximum likelihood

    The index models are nonlinear in the parameters and are estimated by maximum likelihood rather than least squares. With independent observations, observation \(i\) contributes probability \(G(x_i'\beta)\) when \(y_i=1\) and \(1-G(x_i'\beta)\) when \(y_i=0\), so the log-likelihood is

    \[ \ell(\beta)=\sum_{i=1}^{n}\Big[\,y_i\log G(x_i'\beta)+(1-y_i)\log\big(1-G(x_i'\beta)\big)\Big]. \]

    The estimator \(\hat\beta\) maximizes \(\ell\). It has no closed form and is obtained by iterative numerical optimization, but under standard conditions it is consistent, asymptotically normal, and asymptotically efficient. Inference uses the maximum-likelihood apparatus: standard errors from the inverse information matrix, and Wald, likelihood-ratio, or score tests for hypotheses.

    14.5 Interpreting the coefficients

    The interpretation of logit and probit coefficients is the principal source of difficulty, because in neither model is a coefficient a change in probability. We treat first the readings common to both models, then those specific to each, and finally the relationship between the two.

    14.5.1 Sign, significance, and the latent index

    In both models \(\beta_j\) is the effect of \(x_j\) on the latent index \(x'\beta\), expressed in the normalized units of that model's error distribution. Its sign gives the direction of the effect on the probability — a positive coefficient raises the probability of the event, a negative coefficient lowers it — and its \(z\)-statistic and \(p\)-value test significance exactly as in linear regression. What the raw coefficient does not convey, in either model, is the magnitude of the effect on the probability itself.

    14.5.2 Marginal effects (both models)

    The effect of a regressor on the probability is the marginal (partial) effect, obtained by differentiating \(G(x'\beta)\):

    \[ \frac{\partial \Pr(y=1\mid x)}{\partial x_j}=g(x'\beta)\,\beta_j, \]

    where \(g=G'\) is the density — \(\lambda(z)=\Lambda(z)[1-\Lambda(z)]\) for the logit, \(\phi(z)\) for the probit. The marginal effect is the coefficient scaled by the height of the density at the index, so it is largest where the curve is steepest (near probability one-half) and smallest in the tails (near 0 or 1); it therefore varies across observations. Two scalar summaries are reported: the average marginal effect (AME), the partial effect averaged over the sample, \(n^{-1}\sum_i g(x_i'\beta)\,\beta_j\); and the marginal effect at the mean (MEM), \(g(\bar x'\beta)\,\beta_j\). The AME is generally preferred, as it does not rely on a possibly unrepresentative “average” unit. These probability-scale summaries are directly comparable across logit, probit, and the linear probability model, and in practice they agree closely.

    14.5.3 The logit reading: log-odds and odds ratios

    The logit affords an interpretation unavailable to the probit. Inverting the logistic form renders the log-odds linear in the regressors,

    \[ \log\frac{\Pr(y=1\mid x)}{1-\Pr(y=1\mid x)}=x'\beta, \]

    so \(\beta_j\) is the change in the log-odds of the event per unit change in \(x_j\). Exponentiating, \(e^{\beta_j}\) is the odds ratio: a one-unit increase in \(x_j\) multiplies the odds of the event by \(e^{\beta_j}\), holding the other regressors fixed. An odds ratio above one signifies a positive effect and below one a negative effect, and — unlike the probability-scale marginal effect — the reading is constant across observations. Thus \(\beta_j=0.30\) gives an odds ratio \(e^{0.30}\approx1.35\), a 35% increase in the odds. The odds ratio is a multiplicative effect on the odds, not on the probability, and the two must not be conflated.

    14.5.4 The probit reading: the latent z-score scale

    The probit coefficient has no odds interpretation. Because the probit normalizes the latent error to unit variance, \(\beta_j\) is the change in the latent index — a standard-normal \(z\)-score — per unit change in \(x_j\). A probit coefficient of \(0.20\) means that a one-unit increase in \(x_j\) shifts the latent propensity by 0.20 standard deviations, moving the argument of \(\Phi\) by that amount and changing the probability by \(\phi(x'\beta)\times0.20\) at a given \(x\). This is coherent but less immediate than the odds ratio, which is one reason the logit is often preferred when a verbal effect size is wanted; the probit's appeal lies in its consistency with a normally distributed latent error and its tractability within models that already assume normal structure.

    14.5.5 Why logit and probit coefficients differ — and why it does not matter

    Logit coefficients are systematically larger than probit coefficients, by a factor of roughly \(1.6\) (the ratio of error standard deviations is \(\pi/\sqrt{3}\approx1.81\), tempered in practice to about 1.6–1.7). This is a pure consequence of the differing scale normalizations — the logistic error has standard deviation \(\pi/\sqrt{3}\), the normal error has standard deviation one — and carries no substantive content. Translated to the probability scale, the two models deliver nearly identical marginal effects and nearly identical fitted probabilities, and the choice between them seldom affects any conclusion. The raw coefficients of the two models must therefore never be compared directly; only their marginal effects (or, for the logit, its odds ratios) should be interpreted.

    Key Idea

    In a logit or probit, the raw coefficient gives only the sign and significance of an effect. To quantify the effect on the probability, report a marginal effect (the average marginal effect is standard), which is comparable across logit, probit, and the linear probability model. The logit additionally permits an odds-ratio reading, \(e^{\beta_j}\); the probit coefficient is a latent \(z\)-score effect with no odds interpretation. Logit coefficients exceed probit coefficients by a scale factor of about 1.6, with no difference in substance.

    14.6 Goodness of fit

    There is no \(R^{2}\) for an index model in the least-squares sense. Fit is assessed through the maximized log-likelihood and statistics derived from it. McFadden's pseudo-\(R^{2}\), \(1-\ell(\hat\beta)/\ell_0\), compares the fitted log-likelihood to that of an intercept-only model \(\ell_0\); it lies in \([0,1)\) but is not a proportion of variance explained and takes much smaller values than a familiar \(R^{2}\). A likelihood-ratio test assesses the joint significance of the regressors. The percentage correctly predicted classifies each observation by whether its fitted probability exceeds a threshold (commonly one-half) and compares this to the realized outcome; it is intuitive but can mislead when the event is rare, since predicting “no event” for every observation may attain a high hit rate while conveying nothing.

    14.7 Implementation

    The linear probability model is OLS on the binary outcome with robust standard errors; the logit and probit are maximum-likelihood fits whose coefficients are converted to average marginal effects, with the logit's coefficients additionally exponentiated to odds ratios.

    Chapter 15 · Time Series I

    Stationarity and Spurious Regression

    The Question

    Two unrelated series both trend over time, and a regression of one on the other reports a strong, significant relationship. Is the relationship real?

    Arena⚔ The Spurious-Regression Oracle →

    Time-series data violate the assumption of independent observations: successive values of an economic series are related, and many series wander without returning to a fixed level. This wandering produces a hazard absent from cross-sectional analysis — the spurious regression, in which unrelated series appear strongly related. This chapter develops the concepts of autocorrelation and stationarity, demonstrates the spurious-regression problem, and establishes the testing and differencing procedures that guard against it, with their Python implementation.

    Running example

    Consider an analyst who suspects that one macroeconomic series drives another — that a country's price level tracks its money stock, say, or that two national-income aggregates move together. Plotted over several decades both series climb steadily, and a regression of one on the other returns a large coefficient, a \(t\)-statistic in the dozens, and an \(R^2\) near unity. The fit is spectacular. Yet exactly this spectacular fit arises when the two series are entirely unrelated, provided only that each wanders over time. This chapter explains why — developing autocorrelation, stationarity, and the unit root — and supplies the tests and transformations that separate a genuine co-movement from a coincidence of two trending series. The running example is two independent random-walk series, estimated in Section 15.7.

    15.1 Time-series data and the autocorrelation function

    A time series is a realization of a stochastic process \(\{y_t\}\) indexed by time, in which the ordering of observations is informative because each value is statistically related to its predecessors. This dependence is the defining feature of time-series data and the source of both its structure and its hazards. It is summarized by the autocovariance and autocorrelation functions.

    Definition 15.1 — Autocovariance and autocorrelation

    For a series with constant mean, the autocovariance at lag \(h\) is \(\gamma(h)=\operatorname{Cov}(y_t,\,y_{t-h})\), and the autocorrelation function (ACF) is its standardized form

    \[ \rho(h)=\frac{\gamma(h)}{\gamma(0)},\qquad \gamma(0)=\operatorname{Var}(y_t). \]

    The ACF, plotted against the lag \(h\), is the correlogram; it is the primary descriptive instrument of time-series analysis. A series of independent, identically distributed shocks with zero mean and constant variance — white noise — has \(\rho(h)=0\) for all \(h\neq0\) and serves as the benchmark of no temporal structure. Persistent, slowly decaying autocorrelation indicates that successive observations carry overlapping information, so the effective number of independent observations is below the nominal count; inference that ignores this dependence overstates precision, exactly as unmodelled clustering does in Chapter 11.

    15.2 Stationarity

    Estimation and inference in time series require that the probabilistic structure of the process be stable over time; otherwise a single realization carries no repeatable information about the process generating it. This stability is stationarity.

    Definition 15.2 — Weak (covariance) stationarity

    A series \(\{y_t\}\) is weakly stationary if its first two moments are time-invariant: \(\mathbb{E}[y_t]=\mu\) and \(\operatorname{Var}(y_t)=\gamma(0)\) are finite constants, and the autocovariance \(\gamma(h)=\operatorname{Cov}(y_t,y_{t-h})\) depends only on the lag \(h\), not on the date \(t\). Strict stationarity, the stronger requirement that the entire joint distribution be invariant to time shifts, implies weak stationarity when second moments exist.

    Stationarity is what permits the past to inform the future: because the relationship between observations depends only on their separation, the dependence estimated from history continues to hold. A non-stationary series violates one or more of these conditions — its mean drifts, its variance grows, or its autocovariances change with the date — and the standard regression apparatus, derived for stationary (and in cross-sections, independent) data, no longer delivers valid inference. The central practical task of this chapter is to determine whether a series is stationary and, if not, to render it so.

    15.3 The autoregressive process and the unit root

    The simplest dynamic model expresses the current value as a fraction of its predecessor plus a shock — the first-order autoregressive process,

    \[ y_t=\phi\,y_{t-1}+\varepsilon_t,\qquad \varepsilon_t\ \text{white noise}. \]

    The behaviour of the process is governed entirely by the coefficient \(\phi\). When \(|\phi|<1\) the series is stationary: shocks decay geometrically, the process reverts to its mean, the variance is the finite constant \(\sigma^2/(1-\phi^2)\), and the autocorrelation function declines geometrically, \(\rho(h)=\phi^{h}\). When \(\phi=1\) the process becomes the random walk \(y_t=y_{t-1}+\varepsilon_t\), the leading non-stationary case: shocks are permanent rather than transitory, the series has no tendency to return to any fixed level, and its variance \(t\sigma^2\) grows without bound. The value \(\phi=1\) is the unit root, the boundary separating stationary from non-stationary autoregressive behaviour.

    Deterministic versus stochastic trends, and the order of integration

    A series may trend for two distinct reasons. A deterministic trend, \(y_t=\alpha+\beta t+u_t\) with \(u_t\) stationary, departs from a fixed line only temporarily; it is rendered stationary by detrending (regressing on \(t\)). A stochastic trend, as in the random walk (optionally with drift, \(y_t=\mu+y_{t-1}+\varepsilon_t\)), accumulates permanent shocks and is rendered stationary by differencing. Confusing the two — detrending a unit-root series or differencing a trend-stationary one — misspecifies the dynamics.

    A series is integrated of order \(d\), written \(I(d)\), if it must be differenced \(d\) times to become stationary. A stationary series is \(I(0)\); a random walk is \(I(1)\), since its first difference \(\Delta y_t=\varepsilon_t\) is white noise; a series whose changes themselves wander is \(I(2)\). Most economic levels (prices, GDP, money) are well described as \(I(1)\); their growth rates are \(I(0)\).

    15.4 Spurious regression

    Proposition 15.1 — Spurious regression

    The regression of one independent \(I(1)\) series on another typically yields a large coefficient, a \(t\)-statistic that grows without bound with the sample size, and a high \(R^2\), falsely indicating a relationship where none exists. This is not an exceptional outcome but the usual result of regressing one non-stationary series on another.

    The phenomenon was documented by Granger and Newbold (1974). Its mechanism is that two wandering series, over any finite sample, exhibit apparent co-movement that ordinary least squares registers as a relationship. The difficulty is not merely understated standard errors: under non-stationarity the usual asymptotics fail entirely — the \(t\)-statistic does not converge to a fixed distribution but diverges as the sample grows, so a larger sample makes the spurious relationship appear more significant rather than averaging it away. The high \(R^2\) is genuine arithmetic; the relationship it appears to certify is not.

    A practical warning sign accompanies the spurious result: the residuals of a spurious regression are themselves highly persistent (close to a unit root), so the Durbin–Watson statistic is near zero. A regression whose \(R^2\) exceeds its Durbin–Watson statistic is a classic signature of spuriousness and should not be interpreted before the series are tested for stationarity.

    Key Idea

    For time-series data, a high \(R^2\) and a large \(t\)-statistic are not evidence of a genuine relationship; they are the expected symptoms of regressing one non-stationary series on another. The stationarity of every series must be assessed before any time-series regression is interpreted.

    15.5 Testing for a unit root

    Because stationarity governs the validity of every subsequent step, it must be tested rather than assumed. The standard procedure is the Dickey–Fuller test. Subtracting \(y_{t-1}\) from the AR(1) equation gives \(\Delta y_t=(\phi-1)y_{t-1}+\varepsilon_t=\delta\,y_{t-1}+\varepsilon_t\); the unit-root hypothesis \(\phi=1\) is thus the hypothesis \(\delta=0\). The test regresses the change on the lagged level and examines the coefficient on \(y_{t-1}\).

    The Dickey–Fuller test

    Null hypothesis \(H_0:\ \delta=0\) (a unit root, non-stationary) against \(H_1:\ \delta<0\) (stationary, mean-reverting). The statistic is the \(t\)-ratio on \(\hat\delta\), but under the null it does not follow the Student distribution; it has a non-standard (Dickey–Fuller) distribution whose critical values lie further from zero, so ordinary \(t\)-tables must not be used.

    The augmented Dickey–Fuller (ADF) test adds lagged differences \(\Delta y_{t-1},\dots,\Delta y_{t-p}\) to the regression to absorb residual autocorrelation, so the test concerns the unit root alone; the lag length is chosen by an information criterion. The regression is specified with no constant, with a constant (drift), or with a constant and trend, according to the series' appearance — an incorrect specification distorts the test. Because the null is non-stationarity, failure to reject indicates that the series should be treated as non-stationary. The test has low power against persistent but stationary alternatives, so it is prudent to corroborate it with a complementary test such as KPSS, whose null is stationarity.

    15.6 Differencing and the order of integration

    A series found to contain a unit root is rendered stationary by differencing — replacing it by its period-to-period change \(\Delta y_t=y_t-y_{t-1}\). The first difference of a random walk is its white-noise increment, which is stationary; an \(I(1)\) series is thereby reduced to \(I(0)\) by one difference, and an \(I(d)\) series by \(d\) differences. A spurious relationship in levels disappears under differencing, confirming its spurious nature, whereas a genuine relationship survives. The procedure is therefore to test each series, difference until stationary, and conduct the regression in the transformed variables — with the important exception, developed in Chapter 16, of cointegrated series, whose long-run relationship differencing would discard. One caution: over-differencing a series that is already stationary introduces a non-invertible moving-average structure and inflates the variance, so one differences only as far as the unit-root tests require.

    15.7 Implementation

    The augmented Dickey–Fuller test is provided by statsmodels.tsa.stattools.adfuller, whose second return value is the p-value; differencing uses the diff method (or numpy.diff). A regression in differences typically pairs with autocorrelation-robust (HAC) standard errors (Chapter 16).

    from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import adfuller
import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

# ADF test: H0 is a unit root (non-stationary). Large p -> treat the series as non-stationary.
stat, pvalue = adfuller(df["gdp"], autolag="AIC")[:2]
print(pvalue)

# difference to stationarity, then regress in differences with HAC standard errors
df["d_gdp"] = df["gdp"].diff()
df["d_x"]   = df["x"].diff()
reg = smf.ols("d_gdp ~ d_x", data=df.dropna()).fit(cov_type="HAC", cov_kwds={"maxlags": 4})
print(reg.params["d_x"])

    A discipline applicable to any relationship measured over time is to determine whether it persists in differences. A genuine relationship, in which movements of one series drive movements of another, manifests in the period-to-period changes as well as in the levels: when one series moves, the other responds. A spurious relationship resides only in the shared wandering of the levels and disappears under differencing. The most informative single question to pose of a striking time-series correlation is therefore whether it survives in the differenced data. A relationship dramatic in levels but absent in differences reflects two series wandering in coincidental company rather than a mechanism connecting them; this consideration alone would invalidate a substantial portion of confident time-series claims.

    Chapter 16 · Time Series II

    Cointegration and Forecasting

    The Question

    Chapter 15 prescribed differencing for non-stationary series. But if two series are bound together in the long run, does differencing discard the relationship? And how is a model's predictive performance properly assessed?

    Chapter 15 established that non-stationary series should be differenced before regression. This prescription has an important exception: when two non-stationary series are tied together in the long run, a relationship that differencing would destroy. This chapter develops cointegration, the formal expression of such a long-run relationship, together with the assessment of forecasting models by out-of-sample evaluation and the correction of standard errors for autocorrelation, with their Python implementation.

    Running example

    Consider an analyst tracking two non-stationary price series — a commodity quoted in two markets, say, or an asset and its futures contract. Each price wanders like a random walk, so neither is stationary; yet they never drift far apart, because whenever the gap widens, arbitrage pulls them back together. Differencing each series, as Chapter 15 would prescribe, throws away precisely this long-run tie. The two prices are cointegrated: a particular combination of them is stationary even though each is not, and an error-correction model measures how fast the gap closes when it opens. The running example is a cointegrated pair \(y_t=\beta x_t+u_t\) with stationary \(u_t\), estimated in Section 16.7, where the Engle–Granger test confirms the relationship and the error-correction model recovers the speed of adjustment.

    16.1 Cointegration

    Chapter 15 prescribed differencing for any non-stationary series. There is one important exception. Two \(I(1)\) series may wander individually yet never drift far apart: whenever the gap between them widens, economic forces pull it back. A particular linear combination of the two is then stationary even though each series is not, and that combination expresses a stable long-run relationship the series obey. Such series are cointegrated.

    Definition 16.1 — Cointegration

    Two \(I(1)\) series \(y_t\) and \(x_t\) are cointegrated if there is a constant \(\beta\) (the cointegrating coefficient) such that the combination \(u_t=y_t-\beta x_t\) is \(I(0)\) (stationary). The vector \((1,-\beta)\) is the cointegrating vector, and \(u_t\) is the equilibrium error — the deviation from the long-run relationship \(y_t=\beta x_t\), which is mean-reverting rather than accumulating.

    For cointegrated series the levels regression of \(y_t\) on \(x_t\) is not spurious but informative: because the residual is stationary, ordinary least squares estimates the cointegrating coefficient consistently — indeed super-consistently, converging at rate \(T\) rather than \(\sqrt{T}\). Differencing, by contrast, would discard exactly the long-run relationship that distinguishes cointegration from the spurious case of Chapter 15. The two situations — two series wandering in coincidental company versus two tied together — are separated by whether a stationary linear combination exists.

    16.2 The error-correction model

    Cointegration has an equivalent dynamic representation that unites the long-run relationship with short-run movement. If \(y_t\) and \(x_t\) are cointegrated, their changes obey an error-correction model (ECM):

    \[ \Delta y_t=\alpha\,(y_{t-1}-\beta x_{t-1})+\gamma\,\Delta x_t+\varepsilon_t,\qquad \alpha<0. \]

    The term \(u_{t-1}=y_{t-1}-\beta x_{t-1}\) is the previous period's equilibrium error. Its coefficient \(\alpha\) is the speed of adjustment: when \(y\) lies above its long-run relation with \(x\) (so \(u_{t-1}>0\)), the negative \(\alpha\) pulls \(\Delta y_t\) downward, closing a fraction \(|\alpha|\) of the gap each period; the term \(\gamma\,\Delta x_t\) captures the immediate short-run response. The model thus separates the long run — the equilibrium the series return to — from the short run, how they move between corrections.

    Proposition 16.1 — Granger representation theorem

    Two \(I(1)\) series are cointegrated if and only if an error-correction model relates them. Cointegration (a stationary linear combination) and error correction (a mean-reverting adjustment toward that combination) are two expressions of the same fact: a stable long-run relationship whose deviations are corrected rather than accumulated.

    16.3 Testing for cointegration

    The Engle–Granger procedure tests cointegration in two steps. First, regress \(y_t\) on \(x_t\) in levels and form the residual \(\hat u_t\), the estimated equilibrium error. Second, test \(\hat u_t\) for a unit root: a stationary residual means deviations from the relationship mean-revert, so the series are cointegrated; a residual with a unit root means the apparent relationship is spurious. Because \(\hat u_t\) is itself estimated — the regression chooses \(\hat\beta\) to make the residual as small as possible — the ordinary Dickey–Fuller critical values are too lenient, and the test uses larger cointegration-specific critical values (Engle–Granger / MacKinnon).

    The Engle–Granger test handles a single cointegrating relationship between two series. With more than two series there may be several cointegrating vectors, and the Johansen procedure — a system maximum-likelihood method — tests for and estimates their number. In either case the substantive prerequisite is a restoring mechanism: a reason the gap should close.

    Key Idea

    Differencing is correct for series that merely wander concurrently but destroys the relationship between cointegrated series, which must be analyzed in levels through the cointegrating regression and its error-correction model. The two cases are separated by whether the (individually non-stationary) series possess a stationary linear combination.

    16.4 Forecasting

    A time-series model produces a forecast by projecting its estimated dynamics forward. For an AR(1), \(\hat y_{t+1}=\hat c+\hat\phi\,y_t\) is the one-step forecast; multi-step forecasts are obtained by iterating, \(\hat y_{t+h}=\hat c+\hat\phi\,\hat y_{t+h-1}\), each step feeding the previous forecast back in. Two features deserve emphasis. First, forecast uncertainty grows with the horizon: each additional step compounds the innovation variance, so forecast intervals widen as \(h\) increases — for a stationary series they widen toward the (finite) unconditional variance, while for a random walk they widen without bound. Second, every forecast must be judged against a benchmark. For many economic series the hardest benchmark to beat is the naive random-walk forecast — “tomorrow equals today” — and a model earns its complexity only by beating that benchmark out of sample.

    16.5 Evaluating forecasts

    A model's fit to the data on which it was estimated — its \(R^2\) — is an unreliable measure of predictive performance, because the model was fitted to those very observations. The appropriate measure is performance on data not used in estimation.

    Definition 16.2 — Out-of-sample evaluation

    The data are partitioned into an earlier training period and a later test (holdout) period. The model is estimated on the training period and its forecasts compared with the realized outcomes in the test period. A model that fits the training period well but forecasts the test period poorly has fitted noise rather than structure.

    Forecast accuracy is summarized by the root-mean-square error, \(\mathrm{RMSE}=\sqrt{\tfrac1n\sum_t (y_t-\hat y_t)^2}\), which penalizes large errors heavily, or the mean absolute error, \(\mathrm{MAE}=\tfrac1n\sum_t|y_t-\hat y_t|\), which is more robust to outliers; each is read relative to the benchmark. Whether one model's out-of-sample accuracy is significantly better than another's is assessed by the Diebold–Mariano test for equal predictive accuracy, which examines whether the mean difference of the two models' loss series is zero. Out-of-sample evaluation is the principal safeguard against overfitting and the foundation of the predictive methods of Chapter 17.

    16.6 Autocorrelation-robust standard errors

    Even when a time-series regression is correctly specified in stationary variables, its disturbances are typically serially correlated, so the conventional standard errors of Chapter 4 — derived under independence — are understated, exactly as unmodelled clustering understates them in Chapter 11. The Newey–West (HAC: heteroskedasticity- and autocorrelation-consistent) standard errors correct for both serial correlation and heteroskedasticity, leaving the coefficient estimates unchanged and adjusting only their reported precision. The correction sums residual autocovariances up to a chosen lag (the bandwidth, set with maxlags): too few lags leaves dependence uncorrected, too many adds noise. HAC standard errors are appropriate for essentially any regression on time-ordered data.

    16.7 Implementation

    The Engle–Granger cointegration test is provided by statsmodels.tsa.stattools.coint.

    from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import coint

# Engle-Granger test: H0 is NO cointegration (no stable long-run relationship)
stat, pvalue, _ = coint(df["y"], df["x"])
print(pvalue)        # small p-value: reject -> the two non-stationary series are cointegrated

    Out-of-sample evaluation partitions the sample by time, estimates on the training period, and measures error on the test period; autocorrelation-robust (Newey–West) standard errors are requested with cov_type="HAC".

    import statsmodels.formula.api as smf
import numpy as np

# evaluate a model out of sample: fit on the early period, predict the later period
train = df.iloc[:split]
test  = df.iloc[split:]

model = smf.ols("y ~ x1 + x2", data=train).fit(cov_type="HAC", cov_kwds={"maxlags": 4})
pred  = model.predict(test)
rmse  = np.sqrt(np.mean((test["y"] - pred)**2))     # out-of-sample error, the honest test
print(rmse)

    ★ Sharma's Take

    The question whether two series are cointegrated or merely spuriously related is presented as statistical, but its substantive form is whether a mechanism exists that restores the relationship when it is disturbed. A genuine long-run relationship has an economic basis — arbitrage that eliminates a price differential, a budget constraint linking expenditure to income, a policy rule connecting two rates. Before a cointegration test is conducted, the analyst should be able to name the restoring mechanism. Where the mechanism can be specified, a stable relationship is plausible and the test confirms an existing hypothesis; where no restoring force can be identified, a relationship that the test appears to detect should be regarded with suspicion, since a borderline test result may arise by chance as readily for unrelated series as a spurious one does. The mechanism is logically prior to the test, and the test should not be permitted to supply it.

    Chapter 17 · Prediction and Causal Inference

    Penalized Regression and Double Machine Learning

    The Question

    Algorithms that predict outcomes from many inputs have become extraordinarily accurate. Two questions follow. First, how are such methods constructed, and what do they optimize? Second — and this is the deeper question for an econometrician — can the predictive power of these methods be harnessed to estimate a causal effect, or does prediction belong to a different enterprise altogether?

    Arena⚔ The ML Foundry →

    This chapter develops two bodies of method that have reshaped applied econometrics. The first is penalized regression — the ridge and lasso estimators — which control the variance of an estimator by deliberately introducing bias, and which form the predictive core of modern statistical learning. The second is double (or debiased) machine learning, a framework that uses flexible predictive methods to estimate causal effects in the presence of many control variables. The treatment is self-contained: each estimator is defined formally through the optimization problem it solves, its statistical properties are derived or stated precisely, and its implementation in Python is presented in full. The chapter is longer and more demanding than those preceding it, as befits material that sits at the current research frontier yet rests entirely on the foundations the earlier chapters established.

    Running example

    Two tasks recur through this chapter, and they pull in opposite directions. In the first, a lender must predict which applicants will default from hundreds of candidate features; only out-of-sample accuracy matters, and the lasso of Section 17.5 keeps the few features that carry signal while discarding the rest. In the second, an analyst must estimate a causal effect — the effect of a training program on subsequent earnings, say — when many observed characteristics confound the comparison through unknown, nonlinear channels; here predictive accuracy is beside the point, and a coefficient lifted from an accurate predictive model is biased (Section 17.6). The double-machine-learning estimator of Sections 17.7–17.10 reconciles the two, letting a flexible learner absorb the confounders while protecting the causal parameter; the running causal example — a treatment whose effect is obscured by many nonlinear confounders — is estimated in Section 17.10, and extended to panel data, where unobserved unit effects confound as well, in Section 17.11.

    17.1 Two estimation problems

    It is essential at the outset to distinguish two objectives that are frequently conflated.

    The first is prediction. Given a vector of predictors \(x\), the goal is to construct a function \(\hat f(x)\) whose value is close, on average, to the realized outcome \(y\) for observations not used in estimation. Predictive quality is judged solely by out-of-sample accuracy; the interpretation of the individual coefficients of \(\hat f\) is immaterial.

    The second is causal inference. Here a particular covariate, the treatment \(d\), is singled out, and the goal is to estimate the effect on \(y\) of an exogenous change in \(d\), holding fixed the remaining covariates. As established in Chapters 1, 3, and 9, this requires an identifying assumption about the relationship between \(d\) and the unobserved determinants of \(y\); predictive accuracy is neither necessary nor sufficient for it.

    Key Idea

    Predictive accuracy and causal validity are distinct properties of an estimator. A model may predict \(y\) with high accuracy while yielding a severely biased estimate of the effect of any particular covariate, and conversely. The methods of statistical learning are, in the first instance, prediction methods. Their use in causal inference requires that they be embedded within an identification strategy, a point developed formally in Sections 17.6–17.9.

    17.2 The bias–variance decomposition

    Consider estimating a regression function \(f(x)=\mathrm{E}[y\mid x]\) from a sample, obtaining an estimate \(\hat f\). Fix a target point \(x_0\) and let the data at that point be generated by \(y_0 = f(x_0) + \varepsilon\), where \(\varepsilon\) has mean zero and variance \(\sigma^2\) and is independent of the training sample. The expected squared prediction error at \(x_0\), taken over both the training sample and the new disturbance \(\varepsilon\), admits the decomposition

    \[ \mathrm{E}\big[(y_0 - \hat f(x_0))^2\big] \;=\; \underbrace{\sigma^2}_{\text{irreducible}} \;+\; \underbrace{\big(f(x_0) - \mathrm{E}[\hat f(x_0)]\big)^2}_{\text{bias}^2} \;+\; \underbrace{\mathrm{Var}\big(\hat f(x_0)\big)}_{\text{variance}}. \]

    The three terms are distinct in origin. The irreducible error \(\sigma^2\) reflects noise in the outcome itself and cannot be reduced by any estimator. The squared bias measures how far the estimator is centred from the truth; it is large when the model is too rigid to represent \(f\). The variance measures the sensitivity of the estimate to the particular training sample; it is large when the model is so flexible that it adapts to sample-specific noise.

    The decomposition makes precise a tension that governs every modelling choice. Increasing model flexibility — adding regressors, raising polynomial degree, deepening a tree — reduces bias but raises variance. Restricting flexibility does the reverse. Because the two terms enter the prediction error additively and with opposite responses to flexibility, there exists an intermediate degree of flexibility that minimizes their sum. The estimators of this chapter are devices for locating that minimum in a disciplined way.

    Definition 17.1 — Overfitting

    An estimator overfits when its flexibility is great enough that variance dominates the prediction error: the in-sample fit is excellent because the estimator has adapted to the noise of the training sample, but out-of-sample accuracy is poor because that noise does not recur. Overfitting is diagnosed by a large gap between in-sample and out-of-sample error.

    17.3 Cross-validation

    Because bias and variance cannot be observed directly, the degree of flexibility must be selected by estimating out-of-sample prediction error. The standard procedure is \(K\)-fold cross-validation.

    Definition 17.2 — \(K\)-fold cross-validation

    Partition the \(n\) observations into \(K\) disjoint subsets (folds) of approximately equal size, \(I_1,\dots,I_K\). For each \(k\), estimate the model on the observations not in fold \(k\) and compute its mean squared prediction error on the observations in fold \(k\). The cross-validation estimate of prediction error is the average of these \(K\) fold-specific errors,

    \[ \mathrm{CV} \;=\; \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K} \frac{1}{|I_k|}\sum_{i\in I_k}\big(y_i - \hat f^{(-k)}(x_i)\big)^2, \]

    where \(\hat f^{(-k)}\) denotes the estimator fitted with fold \(k\) removed. To select a tuning parameter, \(\mathrm{CV}\) is computed over a grid of candidate values and the value minimizing it is chosen.

    The validity of the procedure rests on a single principle: each prediction is evaluated on data that played no part in producing it, so the cross-validation error is an approximately unbiased estimate of genuine out-of-sample error. The choice \(K=5\) or \(K=10\) is conventional, trading computational cost against the variance of the estimate.

    17.4 Penalized regression: ridge and lasso

    Ordinary least squares chooses coefficients to minimize the residual sum of squares alone. When the number of predictors \(p\) is large relative to the sample size \(n\), this objective is minimized by coefficients that fit the training noise, producing high variance; and when \(p\) exceeds \(n\) the minimizer is not even unique. Penalized regression addresses both problems by adding to the least-squares objective a penalty that increases with the magnitude of the coefficients, thereby constraining their size.

    Throughout this section the predictors are assumed standardized — each rescaled to have mean zero and unit variance — so that a single penalty applies comparably across predictors measured in different units. The intercept is not penalized.

    17.4.1 The ridge estimator

    Definition 17.3 — Ridge regression

    For a penalty parameter \(\lambda \ge 0\), the ridge estimator solves

    \[ \hat\beta^{\,\text{ridge}}(\lambda) \;=\; \arg\min_{\beta}\;\sum_{i=1}^{n}\Big(y_i - \beta_0 - \sum_{j=1}^{p}x_{ij}\beta_j\Big)^{2} \;+\; \lambda\sum_{j=1}^{p}\beta_j^{2}. \]

    The first term is the familiar residual sum of squares; the second, the \(\ell_2\) penalty, is \(\lambda\) times the sum of squared coefficients.

    The parameter \(\lambda\) governs the strength of the penalty. At \(\lambda=0\) the estimator reduces to ordinary least squares. As \(\lambda\to\infty\) every coefficient is driven toward zero. Intermediate values shrink the coefficients toward zero by an amount that the data, through the residual-sum-of-squares term, can resist in proportion to the evidence for each coefficient.

    Remark 17.1 — closed form and the \(p>n\) case

    Collecting the standardized predictors into the \(n\times p\) matrix \(X\) and the outcomes into the vector \(y\), the ridge objective is \(\lVert y - X\beta\rVert^2 + \lambda\lVert\beta\rVert^2\), whose unique minimizer is

    \[ \hat\beta^{\,\text{ridge}}(\lambda) \;=\; \big(X^{\top}X + \lambda I\big)^{-1}X^{\top}y. \]

    (Matrix notation here merely abbreviates the sums above.) The term \(\lambda I\) adds \(\lambda\) to each diagonal entry of \(X^{\top}X\), which guarantees the matrix is invertible for any \(\lambda>0\) — even when \(p>n\), where \(X^{\top}X\) alone is singular and ordinary least squares is undefined. This is the precise sense in which the penalty stabilizes estimation in high dimensions.

    17.4.2 The lasso estimator

    Definition 17.4 — Lasso regression

    For a penalty parameter \(\lambda \ge 0\), the lasso estimator solves

    \[ \hat\beta^{\,\text{lasso}}(\lambda) \;=\; \arg\min_{\beta}\;\sum_{i=1}^{n}\Big(y_i - \beta_0 - \sum_{j=1}^{p}x_{ij}\beta_j\Big)^{2} \;+\; \lambda\sum_{j=1}^{p}\lvert\beta_j\rvert. \]

    The penalty — the \(\ell_1\) penalty — is \(\lambda\) times the sum of the absolute values of the coefficients.

    The ridge and lasso objectives differ only in whether the coefficients are penalized through their squares or their absolute values. This apparently minor difference has a decisive consequence: the lasso sets some coefficients exactly to zero, and thereby performs variable selection, whereas ridge shrinks all coefficients toward zero but sets none of them exactly to zero.

    The reason is made exact in the special case of orthonormal standardized predictors, where each penalized estimator has a closed form in terms of the ordinary least squares coefficient \(\hat\beta_j^{\,\text{ols}}\):

    \[ \hat\beta_j^{\,\text{ridge}} = \frac{\hat\beta_j^{\,\text{ols}}}{1+\lambda}, \qquad \hat\beta_j^{\,\text{lasso}} = \operatorname{sign}\!\big(\hat\beta_j^{\,\text{ols}}\big)\,\max\!\Big(\big|\hat\beta_j^{\,\text{ols}}\big| - \tfrac{\lambda}{2},\,0\Big). \]

    The ridge solution multiplies every coefficient by the common factor \(1/(1+\lambda)\): a proportional shrinkage that never reaches zero for a finite \(\lambda\). The lasso solution subtracts the constant \(\lambda/2\) from the magnitude of each coefficient and truncates at zero — the operation known as soft-thresholding. Any coefficient whose least-squares magnitude is at most \(\lambda/2\) is set exactly to zero; the remainder are pulled toward zero by the fixed amount \(\lambda/2\). Variable selection is thus a direct consequence of the constant, non-vanishing derivative of the absolute-value penalty, in contrast to the derivative \(2\beta_j\) of the squared penalty, which vanishes as \(\beta_j\to 0\) and so cannot force a coefficient to the boundary.

    Remark 17.2 — the geometric view

    Penalized estimation may equivalently be written as minimizing the residual sum of squares subject to a constraint on the penalty: \(\sum_j\beta_j^2 \le t\) for ridge, \(\sum_j|\beta_j| \le t\) for lasso. The ridge constraint region is a sphere; the lasso constraint region is a diamond (a cross-polytope) whose vertices lie on the coordinate axes. The least-squares contours generically first touch the diamond at a vertex — a point at which some coordinates are exactly zero — whereas they touch the smooth sphere at a generic point with all coordinates nonzero. This is the geometric counterpart of the soft-thresholding formula.

    17.4.3 Selecting the penalty

    The penalty parameter \(\lambda\) controls the position of each estimator on the bias–variance spectrum: small \(\lambda\) yields low bias and high variance (approaching ordinary least squares), large \(\lambda\) yields high bias and low variance. It is therefore selected to minimize estimated out-of-sample error by cross-validation (Definition 17.2), evaluated over a grid of candidate values. Because every penalized coefficient is shrunk toward zero, both estimators are biased toward zero by construction; this bias is the price paid for the reduction in variance, and it is acceptable when the objective is prediction. Section 17.6 shows why it is not acceptable when the objective is the estimation of a causal effect.

    17.5 Estimating ridge and lasso in Python

    Ordinary least squares is estimated with statsmodels, which reports the full inferential table familiar from Chapter 4.

beta_educ = result.params["educ"]  # the estimated slope
se_educ   = result.bse["educ"]     # its standard error

# X is an (n by p) array of predictors; standardize each column to mean 0, variance 1

Xs = scaler.fit_transform(X)       # penalty now treats every predictor on equal footing

    17.6 Why penalized regression does not estimate causal effects

    Consider the apparently natural strategy of estimating the effect of a treatment \(d\) by applying the lasso to a regression of \(y\) on \(d\) together with a large collection of controls \(X\), and reading off the coefficient on \(d\). This strategy is invalid, for two distinct reasons.

    First, the lasso shrinks every penalized coefficient toward zero, including the coefficient on \(d\). The resulting estimate of the treatment effect is attenuated by an amount that depends on \(\lambda\) and is generally unknown. A penalized coefficient is not a consistent estimator of a structural parameter.

    Second, and more seriously, the lasso selects controls according to their value in predicting the outcome. A variable may be a weak predictor of \(y\) yet a strong predictor of the treatment \(d\); such a variable is, by the omitted-variable analysis of Chapter 3, an important confounder, since its omission biases the treatment coefficient in proportion to its association with \(d\). The lasso, judging relevance by outcome prediction alone, may set its coefficient to zero, reintroducing precisely the omitted-variable bias the controls were meant to remove. Selection of controls by outcome prediction is therefore systematically misaligned with the requirements of causal inference.

    Key Idea

    A coefficient produced by a regularized estimator is not a valid estimate of a causal effect: it is attenuated by the penalty, and the variable-selection step discards confounders that predict the treatment but not the outcome. The flexibility of statistical learning is nonetheless valuable for causal inference, provided it is directed at the prediction subproblems and kept away from the target parameter itself. The construction that achieves this is developed next.

    17.7 The partially linear model and orthogonalization

    The framework that reconciles flexible prediction with causal estimation is the partially linear model:

    \[ y \;=\; \theta\, d \;+\; g(X) \;+\; u, \qquad \mathrm{E}[u\mid d,X]=0, \] \[ d \;=\; m(X) \;+\; v, \qquad \mathrm{E}[v\mid X]=0. \]

    Here \(\theta\) is the scalar causal parameter of interest — the effect of the treatment, assumed constant across units — while \(g(X)=\mathrm{E}[y\mid X, d=0]\) and \(m(X)=\mathrm{E}[d\mid X]\) are unknown functions of the controls, permitted to be nonlinear and high-dimensional. These two functions are termed nuisance functions: they are not of direct interest, but must be estimated en route to \(\theta\).

    The route is the partialling-out identity of Chapter 3 (the Frisch–Waugh–Lovell theorem), now applied with general nuisance functions. Define the two residuals

    \[ \tilde y \;=\; y - \mathrm{E}[y\mid X], \qquad \tilde d \;=\; d - m(X) \;=\; v. \]

    Substituting the model for \(y\) and using \(\mathrm{E}[y\mid X] = \theta\, m(X) + g(X)\) gives \(\tilde y = \theta\,\tilde d + u\). Because \(\tilde d = v\) is, by construction, orthogonal to the controls and to \(u\), the parameter \(\theta\) is identified as the coefficient of a simple regression of \(\tilde y\) on \(\tilde d\):

    \[ \theta \;=\; \frac{\mathrm{E}[\tilde y\,\tilde d]}{\mathrm{E}[\tilde d^{\,2}]}. \]

    This is the population analogue of regressing the outcome residual on the treatment residual. The construction residualizes both variables with respect to the controls; the role of the second residualization — of the treatment — is established in the following section.

    17.8 Double machine learning

    The identification result of Section 17.7 requires the nuisance functions \(\mathrm{E}[y\mid X]\) and \(m(X)\), which are unknown. Double machine learning estimates them with flexible predictive methods — penalized regression, random forests, gradient boosting, or others — and forms the residuals from the fitted values.

    Definition 17.5 — The double machine learning estimator

    Let \(\hat\ell(X)\) and \(\hat m(X)\) be estimates of \(\mathrm{E}[y\mid X]\) and \(\mathrm{E}[d\mid X]\). Form residuals \(\hat{\tilde y}_i = y_i - \hat\ell(X_i)\) and \(\hat{\tilde d}_i = d_i - \hat m(X_i)\). The estimator of \(\theta\) is

    \[ \hat\theta \;=\; \frac{\sum_i \hat{\tilde d}_i\,\hat{\tilde y}_i}{\sum_i \hat{\tilde d}_i^{\,2}}, \]

    the slope of the regression of the outcome residual on the treatment residual.

    The decisive property of this estimator is its insensitivity to errors in the estimated nuisance functions. Suppose \(\hat\ell\) and \(\hat m\) deviate from the true \(\ell\) and \(m\) by small amounts. One may show that the resulting error in \(\hat\theta\) depends, to first order, only on the product of the two nuisance errors, not on either separately. Because each error is small, their product is of second order and hence negligible. An estimating equation with this property — that the first-order effect of nuisance error vanishes — is said to satisfy a Neyman orthogonality condition.

    The orthogonality is precisely what the double residualization secures, and it is why the naive single-residualization strategy fails. Had only the outcome been residualized, the error in \(\hat\theta\) would depend on the nuisance error to first order, so that the slow convergence of a flexible, regularized nuisance estimator would transmit directly into a first-order bias in \(\theta\) — the regularization bias of Section 17.6. Residualizing the treatment as well removes that first-order channel, leaving only the harmless second-order product. Under the orthogonality condition, and with the cross-fitting of Section 17.9, \(\hat\theta\) is consistent and asymptotically normal at the parametric rate \(\sqrt{n}\), so that conventional standard errors and confidence intervals are valid even though the nuisance functions are estimated by methods that converge more slowly.

    17.9 Cross-fitting

    One further safeguard is required. If a nuisance function is estimated on the same observations to which it is then applied, a flexible estimator will have partially fitted the noise of those observations, so the residuals are correlated with that noise. This correlation induces a bias in \(\hat\theta\) that does not vanish even asymptotically. The remedy is cross-fitting, an application of sample splitting.

    Definition 17.6 — Cross-fitting

    Partition the sample into \(K\) folds. For each fold \(k\), estimate the nuisance functions on the observations outside fold \(k\), and use those fitted functions to compute the residuals \(\hat{\tilde y}_i,\hat{\tilde d}_i\) for the observations inside fold \(k\). Every observation thus receives residuals computed from nuisance estimates that did not use it. The estimator \(\hat\theta\) is then formed from the assembled residuals as in Definition 17.5.

    Cross-fitting is the same out-of-sample principle as cross-validation (Definition 17.2), applied here not to select a tuning parameter but to ensure that each residual is statistically independent of the nuisance estimate that produced it. Together, Neyman orthogonality and cross-fitting permit the use of arbitrary high-quality predictive methods for the nuisance functions while retaining valid inference on \(\theta\).

    17.10 Estimating a causal effect by double machine learning in Python

    The estimator is assembled from components already encountered: a flexible learner for the two nuisance predictions, a manual cross-fitting loop, and a final residual-on-residual regression with heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors. The learner below is an ensemble of randomized trees (ExtraTreesRegressor); any flexible regressor may be substituted.

    import numpy as np
from sklearn.ensemble import ExtraTreesRegressor
import statsmodels.api as sm

def fit_learner(seed):
    return ExtraTreesRegressor(n_estimators=80, min_samples_leaf=3, random_state=seed)

K = 5
folds = np.array_split(np.random.default_rng(0).permutation(len(y)), K)
y_res = np.zeros(len(y))
d_res = np.zeros(len(y))

for k in range(K):
    test  = folds[k]
    train = np.concatenate([folds[j] for j in range(K) if j != k])
    # predict each test fold from a learner trained on the OTHER folds (cross-fitting)
    y_res[test] = y[test] - fit_learner(0).fit(X[train], y[train]).predict(X[test])
    d_res[test] = d[test] - fit_learner(1).fit(X[train], d[train]).predict(X[test])

# the causal effect is the slope of the residual-on-residual regression
final = sm.OLS(y_res, d_res).fit(cov_type="HC1")
theta = final.params[0]
se    = final.bse[0]

    The loop iterates over folds, fits each nuisance function on the complementary folds, and predicts the held-out fold, so that every residual is computed out of sample. The final regression of y_res on d_res returns the estimate theta and its standard error se.     17.11 Double machine learning with panel data

    The estimator of Sections 17.8–17.10 controls flexibly for observed confounders. Panel data (Chapter 11) offer a complementary protection against a class of unobserved confounders: those that are constant within a unit over time. The two devices combine, so that a single estimator handles observed time-varying confounders flexibly and unobserved time-invariant confounders by the within transformation.

    Augment the partially linear model with a unit-specific effect:

    \[ y_{it}=\theta\,d_{it}+g(X_{it})+\alpha_i+u_{it},\qquad d_{it}=m(X_{it})+\alpha_i^{d}+v_{it}. \]

    The term \(\alpha_i\) collects the time-invariant characteristics of unit \(i\), observed or not; because it enters both the treatment and the outcome it is a confounder of exactly the kind the controls \(X\) may fail to capture. As in Chapter 11, the within transformation — subtracting each unit's time average — eliminates \(\alpha_i\) without its ever being observed:

    \[ \ddot y_{it}=\theta\,\ddot d_{it}+\big[g(X_{it})-\bar g_i\big]+\ddot u_{it}. \]

    Double machine learning is then applied to the within-transformed data: a flexible learner estimates the conditional expectations of \(\ddot y\) and \(\ddot d\) given the controls, the residuals are formed, and \(\theta\) is the slope of their regression. Two adjustments are essential.

    First, cross-fitting must split on units, not observations. Because observations from the same unit are dependent, placing some of a unit's periods in the training fold and others in the test fold would let the nuisance estimate borrow within-unit information about the held-out periods, reintroducing the bias cross-fitting exists to remove. Every observation of a unit must therefore fall in the same fold.

    Second, inference must be clustered by unit. The within-transformed disturbances \(\ddot u_{it}\) remain correlated within a unit, so the final residual-on-residual regression is fitted with cluster-robust standard errors (Chapter 11), clustering on the unit.

    Key Idea

    Combining the within transformation with double machine learning yields an estimator robust on two fronts at once: flexible control for observed time-varying confounders, through the machine-learning nuisance estimates, and exact control for unobserved time-invariant confounders, through the fixed effects. The price is that cross-fitting must respect the panel structure — folds are formed from whole units, and standard errors are clustered by unit.

    One caution carries over from Chapter 11: the within transformation removes only the time-invariant component of unobserved confounding. A confounder that varies over time and is unobserved is not addressed by fixed effects and, if also outside \(X\), leaves the estimator biased — the limitation taken up in general in Section 17.12.

    17.12 Scope and limitations

    The validity of the double machine learning estimator rests on the assumption embedded in the partially linear model of Section 17.7: that conditional on the controls \(X\), the treatment residual \(v\) is uncorrelated with the outcome disturbance \(u\). This is the unconfoundedness (or selection-on-observables) assumption, and it requires that \(X\) contain all common causes of \(d\) and \(y\). No estimation method can establish this assumption from data; it is an identifying restriction justified by subject-matter knowledge.

    The contribution of double machine learning is therefore precisely delimited. It relaxes the functional-form requirement of conventional control-variable regression, permitting the confounders to enter through unknown, possibly nonlinear, high-dimensional functions while preserving valid inference on the treatment effect. It does not relax the identifying requirement that the confounders be observed. When an important confounder is unobserved, the estimator is biased, and identification must instead be sought through the instrumental-variables strategy of Chapter 9 or the quasi-experimental designs of Chapters 12 and 13 — within which, it should be noted, machine learning may still be employed for the associated prediction subproblems.

    Key Idea

    Double machine learning addresses the problem of flexible control for observed confounders; it does not address the problem of identification in the presence of unobserved confounders. The distinction between these two problems is the organizing theme of the entire book, and it is not dissolved by the introduction of more powerful predictive methods.

    17.13 Heterogeneous effects

    The partially linear model restricts the treatment effect \(\theta\) to be constant across units. When the effect is believed to vary with observed characteristics — that is, when one seeks the conditional effect \(\theta(x)\) — the same orthogonalization-and-cross-fitting template extends to estimate it: the final residual-on-residual regression is replaced by a flexible model relating the outcome residual to the treatment residual as a function of covariates. The causal forest is the leading instance of this extension. The methods lie beyond the scope of this chapter, but the underlying principle — orthogonalize to protect the causal parameter, cross-fit to keep predictions honest — is the one developed above, and it is the durable lesson of this material.

    ★ Sharma's Take

    The intended use of an estimate should determine the method chosen to produce it, and the two purposes treated in this chapter call for opposite priorities. When the object is to rank or flag units — to identify which applicants are likely to default or which patients require monitoring — the relevant criterion is out-of-sample predictive accuracy, and the interpretability of individual coefficients is irrelevant; the flexible, regularized methods of Sections 17.2–17.5 are appropriate, and they should be evaluated by cross-validation alone. When the object is to intervene — to determine the consequence of altering a policy, a price, or a rule — predictive accuracy is no longer the operative criterion, and a coefficient extracted from an accurate predictive model is, as Section 17.6 demonstrates, generally a biased estimate of the causal quantity required. The recurring and costly error in applied work is the use of a predictive model's coefficients to guide intervention. Double machine learning is significant precisely because it supplies a principled means of retaining the flexibility of predictive methods while restoring the discipline of causal estimation; it should be understood not as a prediction method but as an estimator of a causal parameter that happens to use prediction methods as components.

    Chapter Summary
    • The expected prediction error of an estimator decomposes into irreducible noise, squared bias, and variance; flexibility lowers bias and raises variance, and the optimal flexibility minimizes their sum (Section 17.2).
    • Cross-validation estimates out-of-sample error by evaluating predictions on held-out folds, and is used to select tuning parameters (Section 17.3).
    • Ridge regression adds an \(\ell_2\) penalty and shrinks coefficients proportionally toward zero without setting any to zero; the lasso adds an \(\ell_1\) penalty and, through soft-thresholding, sets some coefficients exactly to zero, performing variable selection (Section 17.4).
    • Penalized coefficients are biased toward zero and are not valid estimates of causal effects; moreover, lasso selection by outcome prediction may discard confounders, reintroducing omitted-variable bias (Section 17.6).
    • In the partially linear model, the causal parameter is identified as the coefficient of a regression of the outcome residual on the treatment residual, with both residualized against the controls (Section 17.7).
    • Double machine learning estimates the nuisance functions by flexible methods and exploits Neyman orthogonality, which renders the estimator insensitive to first-order nuisance error; cross-fitting removes the bias from in-sample nuisance estimation. Valid \(\sqrt{n}\) inference on the causal parameter follows (Sections 17.8–17.9).
    • With panel data, the within transformation removes unobserved time-invariant unit effects while the machine-learning nuisances handle observed time-varying confounders; cross-fitting is then performed over whole units and standard errors are clustered by unit (Section 17.11).
    • The method relaxes the functional-form requirement of control-variable regression but retains the unconfoundedness requirement; identification with unobserved confounders still requires the designs of Chapters 9, 12, and 13 (Section 17.12).

    1. conceptual State the bias–variance decomposition of expected prediction error and explain, in terms of its three components, why a model that achieves zero training error is generally undesirable.
      Solution
      The decomposition is \(\mathrm{E}[(y_0-\hat f(x_0))^2]=\sigma^2+\text{bias}^2+\text{variance}\). A model with zero training error has adapted to the noise of the training sample; it therefore has very high variance, since a different sample would yield a very different fitted function. Although its bias may be low, the large variance term makes its expected out-of-sample error high. Zero training error is a symptom of overfitting (Definition 17.1).
    2. analytical For a single standardized orthonormal predictor with ordinary least squares coefficient \(\hat\beta^{\,\text{ols}}\), derive the ridge solution \(\hat\beta^{\,\text{ridge}}=\hat\beta^{\,\text{ols}}/(1+\lambda)\) by minimizing \((\hat\beta^{\,\text{ols}}-\beta)^2+\lambda\beta^2\) over \(\beta\).
      Solution
      Differentiate the objective with respect to \(\beta\): \(-2(\hat\beta^{\,\text{ols}}-\beta)+2\lambda\beta=0\). Hence \(\hat\beta^{\,\text{ols}}=\beta(1+\lambda)\), giving \(\beta=\hat\beta^{\,\text{ols}}/(1+\lambda)\). The second derivative \(2(1+\lambda)>0\) confirms a minimum. The shrinkage factor \(1/(1+\lambda)\) is strictly between 0 and 1 for \(\lambda>0\) and never equals zero.
    3. analytical For the same orthonormal case, the lasso minimizes \((\hat\beta^{\,\text{ols}}-\beta)^2+\lambda|\beta|\). Show that the solution is the soft-thresholding rule \(\hat\beta^{\,\text{lasso}}=\operatorname{sign}(\hat\beta^{\,\text{ols}})\max(|\hat\beta^{\,\text{ols}}|-\lambda/2,\,0)\), and explain why this can equal exactly zero whereas the ridge solution cannot.
      Solution
      For \(\beta>0\) the objective is differentiable with derivative \(-2(\hat\beta^{\,\text{ols}}-\beta)+\lambda\); setting it to zero gives \(\beta=\hat\beta^{\,\text{ols}}-\lambda/2\), valid when this is positive. Symmetrically for \(\beta<0\), \(\beta=\hat\beta^{\,\text{ols}}+\lambda/2\) when negative. If \(|\hat\beta^{\,\text{ols}}|\le\lambda/2\) neither one-sided solution is feasible and the minimum is at the kink \(\beta=0\). Combining the cases yields the stated rule. The mechanism is the non-vanishing derivative \(\pm\lambda\) of the penalty at the kink, which the curvature of the data term cannot overcome when the least-squares coefficient is small; the squared penalty has derivative \(2\lambda\beta\to 0\) at the origin and so exerts no such force.
    4. conceptual Explain why standardizing the predictors is necessary before applying ridge or lasso, but not before ordinary least squares.
      Solution
      Ordinary least squares is invariant to the scaling of predictors: rescaling a predictor merely rescales its coefficient, leaving fitted values and inference unchanged. The penalties \(\sum\beta_j^2\) and \(\sum|\beta_j|\), however, depend on the magnitudes of the coefficients, which depend on the units of the predictors. Without standardization, a predictor measured in small units (hence with a large coefficient) would be penalized more heavily than an identical predictor measured in large units. Standardization places all predictors on a common scale so that the penalty is applied comparably.
    5. conceptual A researcher applies the lasso to a regression of an outcome on a treatment and one hundred controls, and reports the lasso coefficient on the treatment as the causal effect. Identify the two distinct errors in this procedure.
      Solution
      First, the lasso penalty shrinks the treatment coefficient toward zero, so the reported value is attenuated by an unknown amount and is not a consistent estimate of the effect. Second, the lasso retains controls according to their usefulness in predicting the outcome; a confounder strongly associated with the treatment but weakly associated with the outcome may be dropped, reintroducing omitted-variable bias. Both errors are discussed in Section 17.6; the remedy is the orthogonalized, cross-fitted estimator of Sections 17.7–17.9.
    6. conceptual Define Neyman orthogonality and explain why it is the property that distinguishes double machine learning from the naive procedure of residualizing only the outcome.
      Solution
      An estimating equation for \(\theta\) is Neyman orthogonal if the first-order effect of small errors in the estimated nuisance functions on the estimate of \(\theta\) is zero. Under orthogonality, nuisance error enters only through the product of the two nuisance errors, which is second order and negligible, so flexible nuisance estimators that converge slowly do not transmit first-order bias to \(\theta\). Residualizing only the outcome does not satisfy orthogonality: the treatment-side nuisance error enters to first order, so regularization bias in the outcome model passes directly into \(\theta\). Residualizing the treatment as well restores orthogonality (Section 17.8).
    7. conceptual Explain the purpose of cross-fitting and the consequence of omitting it.
      Solution
      Cross-fitting computes each observation's residuals from nuisance functions estimated on other observations, so that the residuals are independent of the nuisance estimates applied to them. Without it, a flexible learner partially fits the noise of the observations it is later applied to, making the residuals correlated with that noise and inducing a bias in \(\hat\theta\) that does not vanish asymptotically (Section 17.9).

      Solution
      Replace fit_learner(seed) with a pipeline that standardizes and then applies LassoCV; the cross-fitting loop and final regression are unchanged.     10. python analytical In the double machine learning cell, estimate the effect without cross-fitting by fitting each nuisance function on the full sample and predicting the same sample, and compare with the cross-fitted estimate. Explain any difference using the argument of Section 17.9.
      Solution

    11. conceptual In a panel double-machine-learning analysis, explain why cross-fitting folds must be formed from whole units and why the final standard errors are clustered by unit. What class of confounders does the within transformation remove, and what class does it not?
